# Vorst's K-Regularity Conjecture — Deep Research for Ergon

## Precise Statement

A ring R is **K_n-regular** if the natural map K_n(R) → K_n(R[t_1,...,t_m]) is an isomorphism for all m >= 1. Equivalently, the "nil-groups" NK_n(R) vanish. Vorst's conjecture (1979): if R is a commutative ring essentially of finite type over a field F, and R is K_n-regular for all n, then R is regular (geometrically: Spec(R) is smooth). The converse — regular implies K_n-regular for all n — was proved by Quillen (1973) via the resolution/localization sequence. Vorst's conjecture is the hard direction: K-theory *detects* singularities.

## Proof in Characteristic Zero — cdh Descent

Cortiñas, Haesemeyer, Schlichting, and Weibel (2008) proved the conjecture for fields of characteristic zero. The architecture: they established **cdh descent** for homotopy K-theory KH and for cyclic homology variants (specifically negative cyclic homology HN). The cdh topology on schemes is generated by Nisnevich covers plus abstract blowup squares — crucially, resolution of singularities in char 0 (Hironaka 1964) guarantees that every singular scheme has a cdh cover by smooth schemes. The proof proceeds: (1) construct a comparison fiber sequence relating K-theory, KH-theory, and negative cyclic homology via the "trace" maps; (2) show cdh descent holds for all three theories on smooth schemes over Q; (3) extend to singular schemes using cdh covers, which exist by Hironaka; (4) conclude that if NK_n vanishes (K_n-regularity), the cyclic homology obstruction vanishes, forcing regularity. The key technical tool is the cdh-fibrant replacement, which converts local-to-global arguments on singular varieties into descent spectral sequences on smooth cdh covers.

## Why Characteristic p Is Hard

The entire proof collapses without resolution of singularities. In char p, Hironaka's theorem fails: we cannot resolve arbitrary singularities by blowups. Resolution is known only in dimension <= 3 (Abhyankar 1956 for surfaces; Cossart-Piltant 2008, 2014, 2019 for threefolds in all characteristics). For dim >= 4 in char p, resolution remains one of the deepest open problems in algebraic geometry. Without resolution, cdh covers by smooth schemes need not exist, so the descent spectral sequence that powers the Cortiñas et al. argument has no input. Partial progress: Geisser-Hesselholt (2010) proved Vorst's conjecture for rings essentially of finite type over F_p when dim(R) <= 3, precisely because Cossart-Piltant resolution is available. Kelly (2014) developed alternative "rh descent" (using Riemann-Zariski spaces) to partially circumvent resolution, but this has not yet closed the gap for dim >= 4.

## Computable Test Cases — Singular Rings in Char p

Explicit K-groups are computable for specific singular rings where the singularity structure is simple:

- **Cuspidal curve:** R = F_p[x,y]/(y^2 - x^3). Here NK_1(R) ≅ F_p[t]/(t) (non-trivial), so R is NOT K_1-regular — K-theory correctly detects the cusp. The nil-group is the additive group (F_p, +).
- **Nodal curve:** R = F_p[x,y]/(xy). NK_1(R) ≅ F_p^*, non-trivial — K-theory detects the node. These are computable via Mayer-Vietoris for K-theory of conductor squares.
- **Higher-dimensional:** R = F_p[x_1,...,x_d]/(x_1^2 + ... + x_d^2) for varying d. K-groups are computable via the localization sequence and Karoubi periodicity for d <= 3, but become intractable for d >= 4 — precisely where resolution fails.
- **Whitney umbrella:** R = F_p[x,y,z]/(x^2 - y^2*z). A 3-dimensional singularity where Cossart-Piltant resolution applies, making K-group computation feasible.

## Connection to Singularity Detection and Ergon

The conjecture says K-theory is a **complete invariant for smoothness**. For algebraic geometry, this means: given any finitely generated algebra R over a field, you can detect whether Spec(R) is singular by computing enough K-groups of polynomial extensions R[t_1,...,t_m]. This is a purely algebraic smoothness test — no geometric resolution needed. For Ergon's tensor, this represents a deep bridge between algebra (K-groups) and geometry (singularities) that is *proven* in char 0 but *open* in char p. The obstruction (resolution of singularities) is itself one of the millennium-class problems in algebraic geometry. The computable cases above — cusps, nodes, and low-dimensional singularities over F_p — are concrete targets where Ergon could verify K-regularity detection against known singularity classification, testing whether the conjecture's predictions hold in specific char p cases even before the general proof exists.
