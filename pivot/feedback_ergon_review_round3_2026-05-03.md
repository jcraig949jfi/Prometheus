# Verbatim review of `ergon_learner_proposal_v3.md` — round 3

This file captures the third external review verbatim. Triage and synthesis live in companion file `meta_analysis_ergon_round3_2026-05-03.md`. v4 of the proposal incorporating the round-3 revisions lives in `pivot/ergon_learner_proposal_v4.md`.

The round-3 review reaches deeper than rounds 1 and 2. Where the previous reviewers identified gaps to fill, this reviewer identifies *internal contradictions* (§5.1 Llemma rationale contradicting §3 asymmetry argument) and *missed substrate integrations* (the residual primitive shipped in commit 4872bb4a but the four-counts diagnostic doesn't use it). The design-freeze recommendation in v3's §16 is invalidated by this round; v4 first corrects the contradictions before any subsequent freeze.

---

The proposal is well-structured and the Silver framing is the sharpest part of it, but several of the load-bearing seams are buried where they shouldn't be, and the integration with the residual primitive from the previous thread is missing in a way that costs the document its strongest line of defense.

**The Llemma/Silver shared-prior problem is more structural than the document admits, and it's the central tension of the asymmetry argument.** §5.1 explicitly markets Llemma-7B as "closest to Silver's training distribution" and "closest action-space inheritance to Silver's likely target distribution." This is presented as a strength — the policy inherits relevant priors — but it directly contradicts §3's claim that Ergon covers a different manifold. Proof-Pile-2 is Mathlib + ArXiv math + theorem-proving traces. If Silver fine-tunes on the same corpus, the underlying prior is shared at the corpus level; the asymmetry exists only at the fine-tuning attractor. Fine-tuning pressure is a real force but it's a weaker form of differentiation than "different action spaces, different priors" implies. Open question #1 names this but treats it as a question for review; it's actually the load-bearing crack and the proposal doesn't have a good answer to it. The honest version is something like: "the prior is shared at the corpus level; Ergon's differentiation comes from action-space (typed compositions vs. Lean tactics) and value head (battery survival vs. Lean closure), not from the base model's training distribution." That's defensible, but it's a narrower claim than the document currently makes.

The corollary: if differentiation comes from action space and value head rather than prior, then choosing Llemma specifically is a weak commitment. A general-purpose 7B (Qwen, Llama) fine-tuned on substrate outcomes might produce more divergent fine-tuning attractors than a math-pretrained model whose attractor is partly fixed by pretraining. Worth a paragraph rather than a row in a comparison table.

**The 0-PROMOTE baseline is the empirical fact the rest of the proposal needs to face directly.** §10 acknowledges that the four-counts pilot at 1000×3 episodes returned 0 PROMOTEs in either arm. The proposal then projects 10K-episode pilots that will differentiate operator classes by PROMOTE rate. But if the underlying rate is rare (say, 10⁻⁴ or below), then 10K episodes per arm produces ≤1 PROMOTE per arm, and operator-class differentiation is undetectable at any plausible budget. The diagnostic only becomes statistically powered when PROMOTE rates are high enough that 10⁴–10⁵ episodes produce >10 PROMOTEs per arm. Without an estimate of where that floor sits, the four-counts pilot may be informationally null at every budget the proposal contemplates. This deserves a power calculation rather than a measurement plan. The bear case isn't "neural arm doesn't beat evolutionary arm"; it's "no arm produces enough PROMOTEs to detect any difference, and the engine's contribution to the substrate is uncharacterized for the entire 32-week roadmap."

This connects directly to the previous thread's point about residuals. The four counts as currently specified are catalog-hit, claim-into-kernel, PROMOTE, battery-kill. There's no count for *kills with structured residual* — which is exactly what the residual primitive was designed to capture. A diagnostic that ignores the residual primitive in a system that just shipped it is a missed integration with the architecture's own most recent addition. Adding a fifth count — "kill with structured residual passing residual-class threshold" — gives the diagnostic something to detect even at low PROMOTE rates, because residual events should be more common than promotions by construction. It also gives operator-class differentiation a higher-power test: which arms produce structured residuals vs. uniform noise, even before any of them produce PROMOTEs. Without this, the empirical anchor of the proposal is statistically dependent on a rate you haven't measured and may be too low to resolve.

**The behavior descriptor measures structure-of-proposal, not mathematical-content.** Open question #2 frames this as a degeneracy concern (everything maps to one cell vs. every genome its own cell). The deeper issue is that all five axes — DAG depth, width, equivalence-class entropy, cost tier, output-type signature — are properties of how the genome is *constructed*, not of what it's *doing mathematically*. Two genomes can be structurally identical and mathematically unrelated; two genomes can be structurally divergent and mathematically equivalent. MAP-Elites maximizing diversity along these axes produces diversity-of-construction, which may or may not correlate with diversity-of-discovery. The Phase 2b independence validation was on synthetic landscapes where construction and content were probably correlated by design; in the discovery setting, there's no reason to expect that correlation to hold. The fix is at least one content-aware axis — output-equivalence-class under the canonicalizer (not just output-type-signature), or some embedding-space coordinate over the genome's evaluation result. Without it, MAP-Elites is doing quality-diversity over the wrong manifold for the goal.

**Ring 5 self-play coupling is tighter than §12.3 acknowledges.** The mitigation given ("substrate verdict is independent of policy") addresses verdict independence but not exploration independence. The training data is outcomes from policy-generated CLAIMs filtered by the substrate. If the policy converges on a narrow exploration region, the substrate's outcomes only train the policy on that region, regardless of verdict independence. MAP-Elites archive maintenance provides exploration pressure but the archive is downstream of policy proposals — if the policy never proposes into a cell, MAP-Elites can't elite anything there. The closure has a feedback path that mode-collapses unless one of the four other operator classes (especially `uniform`, the strawman null) is dialed up to maintain exploration. The proposal mentions `uniform` as a strawman null but doesn't commit to a minimum proposal share, which means under selection pressure for cell-fill rate, `uniform` gets squeezed out exactly when it's most needed.

**The Silver-ingestion story in §13 is rhetorical, not operational.** "Silver's outputs become CLAIMs; the substrate's verdicts become Silver's cross-modality verification" is the hinge of the long-term position. But Silver's likely output is Lean-closed proofs. A Lean-closed proof has *already* been mechanically verified — there's no useful substrate-side falsification of "this theorem's Lean proof type-checks," because the Lean kernel did that. The cross-modality verification only makes sense if the substrate can falsify something the Lean kernel didn't check — like the *empirical-pattern conjectures* underlying a theorem, or the *generalization* a Lean proof makes from specific cases. That's a real role, but it's narrower than "Silver's outputs become CLAIMs." The doc should specify what fragment of Silver's output is substrate-ingestible and what verdict the substrate can render that the Lean kernel can't. Otherwise this is a positioning claim without an operational definition.

**Comparison class is missing.** §3 positions against Silver alone. The natural comparison is also Lean Mathlib, AlphaProof, OpenAI's math-tuned models, and the various academic teams running structured-conjecture experiments. Lean Mathlib in particular is a content-addressed, append-only, mechanically-verified substrate of mathematical truth — it's what the Σ-kernel would look like if the kill-test were "Lean kernel accepts." The Σ-kernel's differentiation is the *empirical-pattern* manifold (BSD residuals, Mahler-measure scans, RMT statistics) that Lean doesn't reach today, plus the residual primitive. Naming Mathlib as the comparison rather than Silver clarifies the substrate's actual niche: not "the substrate Silver needs," but "the substrate for empirical mathematical patterns that doesn't exist in Mathlib." That positioning survives any outcome of Silver's company.

**The bear case is deeper than §12.6.** The case in §12.6 is "neural advantage doesn't materialize, calibrated negative result, substrate gains regardless." The deeper bear case is structural: the PROMOTE primitive may be too restrictive for empirical-pattern discovery to fire at all. The battery is calibrated on known truths, which by construction look like known truths. Novel structure may have a different statistical shape that the battery rejects as noise — not because it isn't real, but because it doesn't pass filters tuned to in-distribution truths. In this scenario, you can run any number of arms at any budget and PROMOTE rate stays at zero, and the four-counts diagnostic is never informative. The substrate keeps growing in CLAIMs and FALSIFYs without ever producing typed survivors, which is consistent with both "engine is bad" and "battery is bad," and the architecture doesn't currently have machinery to distinguish.

This is the version of the bear case that connects to the residuals thread and the calibration concern from the thesis review. The fix isn't another arm; it's a meta-loop where Techne occasionally forges a *sharper* checker for high-residual kills, and the substrate audits whether the original battery should have caught what the sharper checker promotes. Without that, "calibrated negative result" is unfalsifiable — you can't tell if the engine failed or the calibration did. Making this distinction operational is probably more important than the v1.0→v2.0 progression.

Net: the proposal is the right shape and the MVP-to-v2.0 progression is conservative in the right way, but the asymmetry argument needs the Llemma-shared-prior issue resolved honestly, the four-counts diagnostic needs a residual-count fifth column to have any statistical power at realistic PROMOTE rates, the behavior descriptor needs at least one content-aware axis, and the deep bear case (battery miscalibrated for novel structure) needs a meta-loop rather than another arm. The Silver framing is good marketing for external readers but Lean Mathlib is the more honest comparison and would make the substrate's niche sharper. None of these are fatal; all of them are seam-tightening before the document goes to Titan Council review.
