## THE OPERATION YOU'RE LOOKING FOR

Modular Symmetric Composition (MSC) has three observable properties:

1. **MODULARITY:** The construction uses discrete, reusable components (tiles, segments, gears, motifs, residue
classes)
2. **COMPOSITION:** Components combine according to rules that preserve a structural property (symmetry, periodicity,
congruence)
3. **INDEPENDENCE:** Each component can be constructed without affecting the others — they don't interfere

The test: Can the artifact be decomposed into modular components? Do the components combine according to an
identifiable group operation? Is the output symmetry determined by the component combination rather than by ad hoc
adjustment? If all three: MSC.

## CRITICAL INSTRUCTIONS

1. **PAGE NUMBERS.** When citing a source, include the page number. "Gerdes (1999)" is useless. "Gerdes (1999, pp.
47-52)" is useful.

2. **HONEST ASSESSMENTS.** If the evidence for Polynesian tapa cloth is weak, say "WEAK — insufficient mathematical
analysis in the literature." Don't inflate.

3. **COUNTEREXAMPLES ARE GOLD.** If you find a tradition that constructs symmetric objects WITHOUT MSC, that's valuable
 — it proves MSC is not trivial. Include it.

 definition (Definition 2.2) assumes commutativity. Find any literature that addresses modular construction with
non-commutative components. This is our biggest technical vulnerability.

5. **DON'T WRITE THE PAPER.** Compile evidence. Flag gaps. Assess strength. The authors will synthesize. Your job is to
 make sure no reviewer can say "the authors didn't check [X]."

6. **SEARCH DEEPLY.** Google Scholar, JSTOR, MathSciNet, arXiv. Check references of references. The most valuable
evidence is often in sources that cite the primary references, not the primary references themselves.
