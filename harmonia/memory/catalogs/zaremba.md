## What the problem is really asking

Six distinct sub-questions live beneath the surface statement. Each
discipline frames it differently, and the framings are not reducible
to one another.

1. **Is the set of "bad q" (integers for which no bounded-CF coprime
   exists) genuinely empty, or merely sparse?** The Bourgain-Kontorovich
   theorem proves density-1 of q are "good"; the conjecture strengthens
   to density-full. The gap between "almost all" and "all" is where the
   action is — a density question from one framing, a Hausdorff-dimension
   question from another.
2. **What is the Hausdorff dimension of the Markov-Hall exceptional
   set?** The set F_A = {x ∈ [0,1] : all partial quotients ≤ A} is a
   Cantor-like fractal. Its dimension δ(A) grows with A. The conjecture
   is equivalent to asking whether the projection of F_A × F_A onto
   rationals a/q hits every denominator once δ(A) crosses a threshold.
3. **Is the problem really about expansion in the Cayley graph of
   SL(2,Z)?** Bourgain-Kontorovich reframed Zaremba as a thin-orbit /
   spectral-gap problem: the semigroup generated by matrices with
   bounded entries must grow fast enough in SL(2,Z) to cover residue
   classes mod q. The conjecture becomes an expander property of a
   thin subset of a non-amenable group.
4. **Is it a Diophantine-regularity statement about q's divisor
   structure?** Partial quotients in a/q are governed by the Euclidean
   algorithm; divisor multiplicity and smoothness of q influence how
   "efficient" some a/q can be. Is bad-q membership decidable from
   multiplicative structure alone?
5. **Is this a quasi-Monte Carlo discrepancy question in disguise?**
   Zaremba's original motivation: lattice rules for numerical
   integration. Bounded partial quotients ⇔ uniformly low-discrepancy
   lattices. The conjecture guarantees existence of good QMC generators
   for every modulus.
6. **Is it the simplest non-trivial instance of a thin-group orbit-
   closure problem on a homogeneous space?** The bounded-CF condition
   carves out a thin subgroup of SL(2,Z). Orbit closures for thin
   groups are governed by Patterson-Sullivan theory, and Zaremba is
   the cleanest concrete test of how far Sullivan-type dimension bounds
   translate to integer-orbit density statements.

## Data provenance

**The problem (1971/1972).** S.K. Zaremba, "La méthode des 'bons
treillis' pour le calcul des intégrales multiples" (1972), and earlier
Zaremba 1966. Originally motivated by numerical integration: good
lattice rules for multi-dimensional QMC require generators whose
continued-fraction expansions have bounded partial quotients. Zaremba
conjectured that every q admits such a generator, and computational
evidence led him to suggest A = 5 suffices (A = 2 fails at q = 54, but
A = 5 is believed to work for all q ≥ 1 with one or two small
exceptions sometimes folded in as A = 6 or 7 depending on convention).

**Known theoretical bounds / partial results.**

- Niederreiter 1978+: extensive tabulation up to q ≈ 10^4; A = 5
  empirically holds. QMC community literature accumulates.
- Hensley 1990s: dim_H(F_A) studied systematically; for A = 2,
  δ(A) ≈ 0.5312..., growing to δ(∞) = 1.
- Bourgain-Kontorovich 2014 ("On Zaremba's conjecture," Ann. Math.):
  density-1 of integers q satisfy Zaremba for some A (specifically
  A = 50 suffices for density-1). Breakthrough result; first
  substantive infinite-family result.
- Huang 2015 (and later Frolenkov-Kan): improvements reducing the
  required A for density-1 statements. Huang brought A down; the
  Bourgain-Kontorovich machinery is continually sharpened.
- Magee-Oh-Winter 2019 and related: spectral-gap / expansion results
  for thin subgroups of SL(2,Z); bounds on the spectral radius of
  the transfer operator for the bounded-CF subshift.
- Kan 2017+: partial results for specific arithmetic progressions.

**The shape of the data.** For each q ∈ [1, N], the quantity
M(q) = min over coprime a of max partial quotient of a/q is finite
conjecturally, with M(q) ≤ 5 conjecturally. Empirically, M(q) ≤ 5
holds through very large N (≥ 10^7 in various enumerations). The
distribution of M(q) is heavy-tailed: most q have M(q) = 2 or 3;
very few q push up to 4 or 5. No q with M(q) > 5 has been found.
Exceptional candidates (where M(q) is hardest to reduce) tend to be
highly composite or smooth q.

**LMFDB / Prometheus hooks.** `nf_fields` (~22M number fields)
contains regulators and class numbers whose continued-fraction
decompositions are computable; the specialized domain
`nf_cf` referenced in `aporia/mathematics/lesser_known_open_problems.md`
entry #6 is the natural substrate for a Prometheus F-ID on Zaremba.
OEIS sequence A174465 and related give M(q) tabulated for small q.

**No counterexample. Exhaustive verification up to ≈ 10^7 for A = 5.**

## Motivations

- **Quasi-Monte Carlo.** Zaremba's original application. Bounded-CF
  generators yield star-discrepancy O((log N)^s / N) for s-dimensional
  integration — sharp up to constants. Without the conjecture, QMC
  users must search per-modulus rather than have guaranteed existence.
- **Thin-orbit theory / Zaremba index.** Zaremba is one of the
  canonical testbeds for thin-group / affine-sieve machinery
  (Bourgain-Gamburd-Sarnak). Its resolution would calibrate a whole
  family of affine-sieve results.
- **Apollonian packings and curvature spectra.** Bourgain-Kontorovich
  machinery directly relates Zaremba's conjecture to the set of
  curvatures in integer Apollonian packings (local-global conjecture).
  Both problems are density-1 proven, density-full conjectured.
- **Spectral gap / expansion.** The semigroup action on the modular
  surface / SL(2,Z) governing Zaremba has a spectral gap; the gap's
  size is the controlling quantity for density statements. Stronger
  gap ⇒ smaller A.
- **Cross-domain bridges (Prometheus-aligned).** Connects number
  theory (CF expansions of rationals), dynamics (Gauss map restriction),
  fractal geometry (Markov-Hall sets), spectral theory (transfer
  operators), and Diophantine approximation. A single-number invariant
  (A = 5) compressing many domains.
- **Pedagogical.** Statement is elementary (anyone can compute
  min-max partial quotient for small q); deep structure is hidden.

## Lens catalog (23 entries)

### Lens 1 — Ergodic / invariant-measure theory

- **Discipline:** Ergodic theory (Gauss measure, CF shift map)
- **Description:** Treat the Gauss map T(x) = {1/x} as an ergodic
  transformation of [0,1] with invariant Gauss measure
  dμ = dx/((1 + x) log 2). Zaremba's conjecture becomes a question
  about orbits whose symbolic CF expansions stay in the alphabet
  {1, ..., A}. This is a thin subset of full Lebesgue measure zero.
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** An ergodic theorist would predict the
  set F_A of x ∈ [0,1] with all partial quotients ≤ A has Hausdorff
  dimension δ(A) strictly less than 1 but approaching 1 as A → ∞
  (Hensley-type bounds). The prediction is that the exceptional set
  of q failing Zaremba — if non-empty — corresponds to a set of
  rationals missing from every F_A × F_A projection, and should be
  forbidden by ergodic equidistribution once δ(A) + δ(A) > 1 (roughly
  A ≥ 4 empirically). Stance: conjecture is true for A = 5 via a
  dimensional-threshold argument; the exceptional set has dimension
  zero. Plausible but unverified specific prediction: δ(5) ≈ 0.84
  so δ(5) + δ(5) ≈ 1.68 > 1, sum-set argument closes.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not invoke
  Bourgain-Kontorovich spectral estimates (those belong to the
  spectral / thin-group thread); may not appeal to additive-
  combinatorics expansion results.
- **Data hooks:** compute empirical dim_H of F_A at finite scale by
  box-counting; compare to Hensley's analytic values. Enumerate
  q ∈ [1, 10^6], record M(q), and regress δ_empirical(A)
  against Hensley's theoretical δ(A). Check whether density-1
  statements asymptotically saturate the Hensley threshold.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes — ergodic theory provides a distinct
  coordinate class from algebraic-number-theoretic framings.

### Lens 2 — Information-theoretic / Kolmogorov complexity

- **Discipline:** Algorithmic information theory
- **Description:** Partial quotients of a/q form a finite symbolic
  sequence; bounded-CF rationals are precisely those whose CF
  encoding is low-complexity in a specific sense (alphabet size ≤ A).
  Zaremba's conjecture: for every q, at least one a ∈ (Z/q)* has
  a "compressible" CF representation.
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** An algorithmic-information theorist
  would predict that the number of bounded-CF rationals at
  denominator q grows as q^{2δ(A) - 1} by a Kolmogorov-dimension
  argument (the CF encoding of a/q has length ~log q, restricted
  to alphabet A gives ~A^{log q} choices, intersected with the
  [0, q] grid gives q^{2δ(A) - 1}). For this to exceed 1 for every
  q, need 2δ(A) > 1, i.e., δ(A) > 1/2. Since δ(2) ≈ 0.531, A = 2
  nearly suffices but misses some q; A = 3 gives δ(3) ≈ 0.705,
  giving comfortable margin. Stance: conjecture true for A ≥ 3 via
  dimension-counting; A = 5 safely covers all residue-class
  obstructions. Plausible but unverified.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not appeal to
  SL(2,Z) expansion (that's the thin-group thread); may not use
  fractal dimension arguments (ergodic thread's territory, even
  though Kolmogorov dimension and Hausdorff dimension partially
  overlap).
- **Data hooks:** compute CF-encoding entropy of bounded-CF
  rationals at denominator q; compare empirical count of good a/q
  to q^{2δ(A) - 1} prediction; identify q where the count is
  anomalously low (candidate "hard q").
- **Tier contribution:** Yes (distinct from ergodic via
  complexity-theoretic framing).

### Lens 3 — Random walk / probabilistic heuristic

- **Discipline:** Probability theory
- **Description:** Model the partial-quotient sequence of a random
  a/q as an i.i.d. sample from the Gauss distribution
  P(a_i = k) = log(1 + 1/(k(k+2))) / log 2. Compute the probability
  that a random a has max partial quotient ≤ A: this is
  P(a_i ≤ A for all i ≤ ~log q) ≈ (1 - ε_A)^{log q} for some ε_A > 0.
  Then sum over coprime a to estimate expected number of good a per q.
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** A probabilist would compute
  ε_A = sum_{k > A} log(1 + 1/(k(k+2))) / log 2 ≈ 1/A for large A
  (specifically ε_5 ≈ 0.17, ε_2 ≈ 0.50). Expected good-a count at
  denominator q is ≈ φ(q) · q^{-c·ε_A / log q} which tends to
  infinity as q → ∞ for any A ≥ 2. Stance: conjecture should hold
  with probability 1 via Borel-Cantelli; the exceptional set
  (finitely many q?) would require Cramér-like large-deviation
  concentration that doesn't seem present. Plausible specific
  prediction: expected good-a count at q scales as φ(q) / q^{ε_A}
  for large q.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not use Gauss map
  ergodicity as an input (ergodic thread's primitive); may not
  invoke specific expansion coefficients from SL(2,Z) representations
  (thin-group thread).
- **Data hooks:** Monte Carlo — sample random a uniformly in
  (Z/q)*, count the fraction with max partial quotient ≤ A, compare
  to Gauss-model prediction. Identify q where empirical fraction is
  more than 3σ below predicted — those are candidate "anomalously
  bad" q.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes.

### Lens 4 — Graph-theoretic / functional graph

- **Discipline:** Combinatorics / graph theory
- **Description:** Construct a graph G_A where vertices are
  reduced residues a mod q with max partial quotient ≤ A, edges
  connect residues related by CF-step transformations (or by the
  Euclidean-algorithm step a/q → (q mod a)/a). Zaremba's conjecture
  becomes: for every q, the vertex set of G_A is non-empty.
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** A combinatorialist would analyze the
  functional graph of the Euclidean-algorithm shift on (Z/q)* and
  predict that G_A for A = 5 has connected-component structure
  matching the class-group / multiplicative-group structure of
  (Z/q)*. Stance: expected vertex count |V(G_A)| grows polynomially
  in q (|V| ≈ q^{2δ(A)} by the Kolmogorov-dimension argument).
  For |V| = 0 (conjecture fails), one would need a combinatorial
  obstruction — a residue class forbidden for all a — and no such
  obstruction is known or plausible by the prior.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not use measure-
  theoretic framings (ergodic thread); may not invoke probability
  distributions on CF digits (random walk thread).
- **Data hooks:** enumerate q ∈ [1, 10^6] and construct G_5
  explicitly; record connected-component counts, vertex counts,
  and isolated-vertex rates. Search for q with |V(G_5)| < some
  threshold as candidates for near-failure.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes.

### Lens 5 — Computability / proof theory

- **Discipline:** Mathematical logic
- **Description:** Zaremba's conjecture is a Π⁰₁ statement (for
  every q, there exists witness a, both bounded by q). Ask: what
  axiomatic strength is required to prove it? Is it equivalent
  to or weaker than Bourgain-Kontorovich-style density statements,
  which are themselves Π⁰₂?
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** A proof theorist would predict
  Zaremba is provable in RCA₀ + some effective analysis principle
  (Weak Kőnig's Lemma is plausibly sufficient, since the bounded-CF
  search is effectively searchable at each q). Stance: Zaremba
  is true and provable in PA (no transfinite induction needed),
  but the known proof techniques (Bourgain-Kontorovich) use
  substantial analytic machinery that bootstraps through real
  analysis and representation theory — much stronger than needed
  in principle. Plausible but unverified: the elementary "core"
  proof exists but hasn't been found.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not appeal to
  specific analytic techniques (transfer operators, expansion);
  must argue from complexity of the statement alone.
- **Data hooks:** none directly — proof-theoretic analysis is
  meta-mathematical. But: formalization of the Bourgain-Kontorovich
  proof in Lean/Coq would bound the effective proof strength.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes — surfaces a truth-vs-provability
  distinction as in Collatz thread 5.

### Lens 9 — FRACTRAN / computational universality

- **Discipline:** Theoretical computer science
- **Description:** Collatz's FRACTRAN lens asks whether the
  iteration embeds universal computation. For Zaremba: is the
  bounded-partial-quotient property decidable in a specific
  complexity class? Could Zaremba-like problems embed undecidable
  search?
- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** The bounded-CF check for any specific
  (a, q) is computable in O(log q) steps (Euclidean algorithm).
  Searching all coprime a is polynomial in q. So the conjecture
  is a Π⁰₁ statement that's trivially decidable per instance.
  The meta-question: does the mapping q → M(q) encode universal
  computation in some regime? Stance: unlikely. The CF/Euclidean
  structure is too rigid for FRACTRAN-style Turing-completeness —
  bounded-CF is a GCD-like property and GCD is firmly in P.
  Stance: decidable and low-complexity.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not appeal to
  complexity-theoretic barriers (natural-proofs etc. belong to a
  different class of problems); must argue from CF/Euclidean
  structure directly.
- **Data hooks:** none directly computational; could compare
  empirical complexity of M(q) computation to theoretical bounds.
- **Tier contribution:** Partial (distinct discipline but the
  stance is weaker — "unlikely to embed universality").

### Lens 10 — Symbolic dynamics on the CF alphabet

- **Discipline:** Symbolic dynamics
- **Description:** The set of CF sequences with digits in {1, ..., A}

  entropy, and specification properties.
- **Status:** PROPOSED

  topological entropy of Σ_A as log A (it's a full shift), observe
  that the Gauss-map shift restricted to Σ_A is a subshift of finite
  type with spectral gap, and predict that the projection from Σ_A
  to rationals (finite orbits of the shift) is surjective onto
  denominators q once the shift's growth rate exceeds the required
  density. Stance: for A = 5 the subshift has sufficient growth
  (entropy log 5 ≈ 1.609) to cover all residue classes; conjecture
  holds by specification property of the shift.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not use Gauss-measure
  invariance (ergodic thread); may not use transfer-operator
  spectral gap (that's the operator-theory thread, see blended
  lenses).
- **Data hooks:** compute the "shift-image" at denominator q —
  i.e., the subset of (Z/q)* hit by bounded-CF orbits — and measure

  is the conjecture at q.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes.

### Lens 11 — Physics / statistical mechanics

- **Description:** The transfer operator L_A for the Gauss map

- **Status:** PROPOSED
- **Prior result / stance:** A mathematical physicist would
  identify the Ruelle-Mayer transfer operator on Σ_A with a

  for A = 5, λ_5 is sufficiently far from the critical value

  Plausible but unverified: the "mass gap" (λ_1 - λ_2) of the
  transfer operator is the rate-controlling quantity for

- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not use measure-

- **Data hooks:** numerical computation of the leading eigenvalue

  to analytical predictions.
- **Tier contribution:** Yes.

### Lens 20 — Hausdorff dimension of Markov-Hall sets (NEW)

- **Description:** The Markov-Hall set F_A — bounded-CF reals

  The Markov-Hall theorem says F_∞ has dimension 1; Hensley,
  Jenkinson-Pollicott computed δ(A) for small A precisely. Zaremba
  reframes as: the embedding Q ∩ [0,1] ↪ [0,1] intersects F_A
  surjectively on denominators.
- **Status:** NEW
- **Prior result / stance:** A fractal geometer would cite
  Jenkinson-Pollicott 2018 (δ(2) = 0.5312805...) and generalizations;
  note that δ(A) → 1 as A → ∞ at known rate. Stance: for Zaremba
  to hold, need enough dimensional mass at A = 5 that rational
  projections saturate; empirically δ(5) ≥ 0.8, which is
  comfortable. Plausible but unverified: a direct dimension-based
  proof of Zaremba is obstructed by the difference between
  "positive-dimension Cantor set" and "contains every rational" —
  the latter is a cardinality-saturation question the former
  doesn't resolve.
- **Forbidden moves (under this prior):** may not use spectral
  gap / expansion (thin-group thread); may not invoke Gauss

- The lenses *agree* that Zaremba empirically holds (density-1 via
  current machinery, empirically verified to large N). On that

- The lenses *disagree* on the *controlling object*:
  - Ergodic (Lens 1): Hausdorff dimension δ(A) is the control.

  - Patterson-Sullivan (Lens 22): critical exponent δ_Γ is the
    control.
- These are all the *same number* in some sense (they all equal δ(A)
  at A fixed, or are related by explicit formulas), but the lenses
  frame the object differently enough that "what is the compression
  direction" is genuinely unresolved. The lens convergence on a
  shared δ(A) is strong evidence the object is real; the disagreement
  on which framing is primary is the `divergent_map` dimension.

**This is the expected pattern for thin-orbit problems.** Collatz
converged on τ(n)/log n ≈ 6.95; Zaremba converges on δ(A) ≈ 0.84 at
A = 5 — but the interpretation (dimension? entropy? critical exponent?
spectral gap?) diverges. The multi-lens run produces a map of
equivalent reframings, not a settled answer.
