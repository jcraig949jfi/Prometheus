### 3. Failure Modes and Plateaus

#### Bloat in Variable-Length Genomes (DAGs up to 25 primitives)
*   **What it is:** The uncontrolled growth of program size (number of primitives, edges, lines of code) without a corresponding increase in fitness. Leads to slower evaluation, harder-to-understand programs, and reduced evolvability.
*   **Why it happens:** Neutral mutations (additions that don't change behavior) accumulate. Crossover can combine large programs.
*   **GP Literature Solutions:**
    1.  **Parsimony Pressure (already doing):** Your "parsimony" objective is the primary defense. Ensure its weight/scaling is appropriate.
        *   **Actionable:** Experiment with different parsimony functions (e.g., linear penalty, quadratic penalty, lexicographic comparison where smaller programs are preferred if fitness is equal).
    2.  **Size Limits:** Hard limits on the number of primitives or edges.
        *   **Actionable:** You have a soft limit (up to 25 primitives). Consider enforcing a hard limit, e.g., if a mutation exceeds 25 primitives, it's rejected or truncated. This can be done *before* evaluation.
    3.  **Tarpeian Selection:** Penalize larger programs more aggressively if they don't significantly outperform smaller ones.
        *   **Actionable:** In your multi-objective setup, this means ensuring that a larger program *must* achieve a substantially better score on other objectives to be non-dominated by a smaller program.
    4.  **Strict Type/Structure Checking:** Ensure mutations don't introduce redundant or semantically meaningless structures. Your "ablation gate" is a good step here.
        *   **Actionable:** Enhance your LLM prompts to explicitly ask for *minimal* code or *essential* wiring. Add post-processing to simplify the DAG (e.g., remove disconnected nodes, merge identical sub-DAGs if possible).
    5.  **Specialized Crossover/Mutation:** Design operators that are less prone to bloat.
        *   **Actionable:** Your LLM-assisted mutations can be prompted to "simplify" or "refactor" code, acting as an anti-bloat operator.

#### Convergence Traps in Multi-Objective Evolutionary Search (NSGA-II)
*   **When NSGA-II gets stuck:**
    1.  **Lack of Diversity:** If the population loses diversity, it can't explore the Pareto front effectively. This is common in high-dimensional objective spaces (6D).
    2.  **Local Optima:** The population converges to a local Pareto front, unable to escape to a globally better one.
    3.  **Domination Pressure vs. Diversity:** NSGA-II balances these, but if the non-dominated individuals are too similar, the crowding distance metric might not be enough to drive exploration.
*   **What breaks it out:**
    1.  **Increase Population Size:** More individuals can cover a larger front.
    2.  **NSGA-III or MOEA/D:** As mentioned, these are better for many-objective problems as they explicitly maintain diversity using reference points or decomposition.
    3.  **Hybrid Approaches:**
        *   **Island Models:** Splitting the population into sub-populations with periodic migration (see Hybrid Approaches section).
        *   **Restart Mechanisms:** If the population stagnates for N generations, re-initialize a portion of it randomly or with LLM-generated diverse seeds.
        *   **Diversity-Enhancing Operators:** Introduce mutation operators specifically designed to increase structural or behavioral diversity.
    4.  **Adjust Objective Weights/Scaling:** Ensure all 6 objectives are appropriately scaled and contribute meaningfully to selection. If one objective dominates, others might be ignored.
    5.  **Novelty Search Integration:** Your novelty objective is crucial here. Ensure it's effectively driving exploration.

#### LLM Mutation Mode Collapse
*   **What it is:** The LLM, over time, starts generating a narrow range of similar outputs, even with diverse inputs. This reduces the exploratory power of your LLM mutator.
*   **Why it happens:**
    1.  **Training Data Bias:** The LLM's training data might have a bias towards certain code patterns.
    2.  **Prompt Engineering:** Prompts might implicitly guide the LLM to a narrow solution space.
    3.  **Lack of Diversity in Examples:** If few-shot examples are used, they might be too similar.
    4.  **Temperature/Top-P Settings:** Too low temperature or too high top-p can make the LLM deterministic.
*   **Literature/Solutions:**
    1.  **Dynamic Prompt Engineering:**
        *   **Actionable:** Vary your prompts. Instead of "rewrite this code," try "rewrite this code to be more efficient," "rewrite this code to handle edge cases," "rewrite this code using a different logic flow."
        *   **Actionable:** Include negative examples or constraints in prompts: "Do NOT use a simple if-else chain; explore a switch-like structure."
    2.  **Temperature/Top-P/Top-K Tuning:**
        *   **Actionable:** Experiment with higher `temperature` (e.g., 0.8-1.2) and lower `top_p` (e.g., 0.7-0.9) during generation to encourage more diverse outputs. Monitor the quality of these more diverse outputs.
    3.  **Diverse Few-Shot Examples:**
        *   **Actionable:** If you use few-shot prompting, ensure your examples cover a wide range of successful and diverse mutations.
    4.  **LLM-Assisted Self-Correction/Reflection:**
        *   **Actionable:** After an LLM generates code, prompt it again: "Is this code truly novel? How could it be made more diverse or explore a different approach?" This is computationally expensive but can be used periodically.
    5.  **Hybrid Mutation Strategies:**
        *   **Actionable:** If LLM mode collapse occurs, temporarily increase the rate of AST-only mutations or introduce a "random LLM prompt" mutation that uses a very generic prompt to break out of the rut.

#### Novelty Search Saturation
*   **What it is:** The novelty archive becomes "full" or too dense, making it hard for truly novel organisms to be added, or the behavioral space becomes so thoroughly explored that new behaviors are genuinely rare.
*   **When it stops driving exploration:**
    1.  **Archive Size:** If the archive is too small, it might thrash (random replacement is a symptom). If it's too large, it becomes computationally expensive and might not effectively guide search.
    2.  **Behavioral Characterization:** If your behavioral signature (k-NN on output vectors) is too coarse or too fine-grained, it can lead to saturation.
        *   Too coarse: Many distinct programs map to the same signature, leading to false positives for novelty.
        *   Too fine: Every tiny change is considered novel, leading to a bloated archive of irrelevant variations.
    3.  **Local Novelty Traps:** The search gets stuck exploring a small region of the behavioral space, finding many slightly novel variants but no truly new "modes."
*   **Early Warning Signals:**
    1.  **Stagnant Archive Growth:** The number of unique entries in the novelty archive stops increasing significantly, or the rate of new additions drops sharply.
    2.  **Low Novelty Scores:** The average novelty score (e.g., distance to k-nearest neighbors) of new offspring decreases consistently.
    3.  **Clustering in Behavioral Space:** Visualizing your behavioral signatures (e.g., with t-SNE or UMAP) shows tight clusters rather than spread-out points.
*   **Solutions:**
    1.  **Adaptive Archive Management:**
        *   **Actionable:** Instead of random replacement, use a strategy like "age-based replacement" (remove oldest) or "sparsest region replacement" (remove from dense areas).
        *   **Actionable:** Consider a fixed-size archive with replacement based on novelty score (replace the least novel if a new, more novel one comes along).
    2.  **Dynamic Behavioral Characterization:**
        *   **Actionable:** Experiment with different behavioral descriptors. Instead of just output vectors, consider:
            *   **Internal State Vectors:** Record the internal states of primitives during execution.
            *   **Control Flow Traces:** A sequence of primitives activated.
            *   **Feature Vectors from GNNs:** Train a small GNN to embed your DAGs into a behavioral space.
            *   **LLM-Generated Descriptors:** Prompt an LLM to describe the "behavior" or "strategy" of a program.
    3.  **Hybrid Novelty/Quality:** Your 6D Pareto already includes novelty. Ensure it's weighted appropriately.
    4.  **Curiosity-Driven Mechanisms:** Introduce a "curiosity" objective that rewards exploring regions of the behavioral space where the surrogate model's prediction error is high.

#### Early Warning Signals for Plateaus
Monitor these metrics over generations:
1.  **Pareto Front Movement:**
    *   **Signal:** The average or maximum values of your 6 fitness objectives stop improving significantly. The Pareto front itself stops expanding.
    *   **Actionable:** Plot the front's extent (min/max for each objective) over time.
2.  **Population Diversity:**
    *   **Signal:**
        *   **Genomic Diversity:** Low variance in genome features (primitive counts, DAG depth, code complexity).
        *   **Behavioral Diversity:** Low average novelty scores, or clustering in the behavioral space (if you visualize it).
    *   **Actionable:** Track average pairwise genomic distance (e.g., edit distance on canonical DAG representation) and average pairwise behavioral distance.
3.  **Novelty Archive Growth Rate:**
    *   **Signal:** The rate at which new, unique entries are added to the novelty archive drops to near zero.
    *   **Actionable:** Plot `archive_size` vs. `generation`.
4.  **Offspring Crash Rate:**
    *   **Signal:** If the crash rate remains consistently high (e.g., >50%), it indicates mutations are too destructive, or the LLM is generating too much invalid code. This can starve the population of viable offspring.
    *   **Actionable:** Track `valid_offspring_count / total_offspring_count`.
5.  **LLM Mutation Success Rate:**
    *   **Signal:** The percentage of LLM-generated mutations that are syntactically valid and semantically meaningful drops.
    *   **Actionable:** Track `valid_llm_mutations / total_llm_mutations`.

#### Island Models / Migration
*   **Yes, highly recommended for diversity and exploration.**
*   **Concept:** Split your population into 2-3 (or more) independent sub-populations (islands). Each island runs its own evolutionary process. Periodically, "migrants" (fit or novel individuals) are exchanged between islands.
*   **Benefits:**
    1.  **Diversity:** Different islands can explore different regions of the search space, preventing premature convergence.
    2.  **Parallelism:** Each island can run on its own CPU core or even a separate GPU (though your single GPU constraint means LLM inference would still be shared or batched across islands).
    3.  **Robustness:** If one island gets stuck, others might still make progress.
*   **Implementation on a Single GPU:**
    *   You can run multiple `pymoo` instances (one per island) in separate processes using `multiprocessing`.
    *   **LLM Sharing:** The `Qwen2.5` model would still be loaded once on the single GPU. Each island's LLM mutation requests would be batched and sent to this shared LLM instance. This is efficient.
    *   **Migration Strategy:**
        *   **Frequency:** Every `M` generations (e.g., 10-20).
        *   **Number of Migrants:** `N` individuals (e.g., 1-5) per migration.
        *   **Selection of Migrants:** Best individuals (highest hypervolume contribution), most novel individuals, or a random sample from the non-dominated front.
        *   **Replacement Strategy:** Replace worst individuals, oldest individuals, or random individuals on the receiving island.
*   **Actionable:**
    1.  Start with 2-3 islands.
    2.  Implement a simple migration scheme: every 10 generations, send the top 2 non-dominated individuals from each island to a random other island, replacing the 2 worst individuals.
*   **Relevant Papers:**
    *   **"Island Models in Evolutionary Computation: A Survey"** by *E. Alba and J. M. Troya* (2008, *Genetic Programming and Evolvable Machines*). While older, it's a comprehensive survey. Recent work applies it to GP.
    *   **"Distributed Genetic Programming with Island Models for Large-Scale Problems"** by *A. G. G. de Souza et al.* (2022, *IEEE Transactions on Evolutionary Computation*).

#### Curriculum Learning for Evolutionary Search
*   **Yes, highly effective for complex tasks.**
*   **Concept:** Start with simpler versions of the reasoning tasks, and gradually introduce more complex or challenging tasks as the population improves.
*   **Benefits:**
    1.  **Bootstrapping:** Allows evolution to find basic solutions quickly.
    2.  **Avoids Local Optima:** Prevents the population from getting stuck on simple solutions to hard problems.
    3.  **Guides Complexity:** Encourages the development of more sophisticated reasoning strategies.
*   **Actionable Strategies:**
    1.  **Task Difficulty Metrics:** Define a metric for task difficulty (e.g., number of steps required, number of variables, ambiguity, number of constraints).
    2.  **Dynamic Task Battery:**
        *   **Threshold-based:** When the average accuracy (or another fitness metric) on the current task set exceeds a threshold (e.g., 90%), introduce a new set of harder tasks.
        *   **Adaptive Sampling:** Sample tasks based on the current population's performance. Prioritize tasks where performance is improving but not yet perfect, or tasks that are "maximally informative" (e.g., tasks where the population shows high variance in performance).
        *   **LLM-Generated Tasks:** Prompt an LLM to generate new tasks that are slightly harder than the current "mastered" tasks, or tasks that target specific weaknesses of the current population.
*   **Relevant Papers:**
    *   **"Curriculum Learning for Reinforcement Learning"** by *S. Bengio et al.* (2009, *ICML Workshop on Learning in Non-Stationary Environments*). While RL-focused, the principles apply.
    *   **"Evolutionary Curriculum Learning for Program Synthesis"** by *P. K. Singh et al.* (2023, *GECCO*). Directly applies curriculum learning to program synthesis.

### 5. Libraries and Tools We Might Be Missing

#### Python Libraries for Program DAG Manipulation (beyond `ast`)
*   **`networkx`:**
    *   **Pros:** The de-facto standard for graph manipulation in Python. Excellent for representing your DAGs (nodes for primitives, edges for wiring). Provides algorithms for graph traversal, shortest path, topological sort, etc.
    *   **Cons:** Doesn't inherently understand code semantics. You'd build your DAG on top of it.
    *   **Actionable:** Use `networkx` to represent your DAGs. This will make structural mutations (adding/removing nodes/edges, re-wiring) much cleaner and more robust than raw AST manipulation. You can store primitive types and router code snippets as node/edge attributes.
*   **`graphviz` (Python wrapper):**
    *   **Pros:** For visualization of your DAGs. Invaluable for debugging and understanding evolved programs.
    *   **Actionable:** Integrate `graphviz` to render your evolved DAGs.
*   **`pygraphviz`:** Similar to `graphviz` but a different Python binding.
*   **`astor`:**
    *   **Pros:** Useful for converting ASTs back to Python code. Can help with pretty-printing and normalization.
    *   **Actionable:** Use `astor.to_source(ast_node)` after your LLM or AST mutations to get clean Python code.
*   **`libcst` (Meta):**
    *   **Pros:** A concrete syntax tree (CST) library. Preserves whitespace and comments, making it better for code transformations where you want to maintain formatting. More robust for refactoring than `ast`.
    *   **Cons:** Steeper learning curve than `ast`.
    *   **Actionable:** If `ast` mutations are too destructive to formatting or you need more fine-grained control over code structure, `libcst` is a powerful alternative.

#### Better Sandboxing than RestrictedPython
`RestrictedPython` is good for basic isolation, but for truly untrusted, evolved code, you need stronger guarantees.
*   **`gVisor` (Google):**
    *   **Pros:** A user-space kernel that intercepts syscalls, providing a strong isolation boundary between the application and the host kernel. Used by Google for container sandboxing.
    *   **Cons:** More complex to set up, typically used with Docker/Kubernetes. Might introduce some performance overhead.
    *   **Actionable:** If security is paramount and you can containerize your evaluation, `gVisor` is a top-tier solution.
*   **`firejail` (Linux):**
    *   **Pros:** A SUID program that can restrict the capabilities of a process, including network access, file system access, and resource limits. Simpler to use than `gVisor` for individual processes.
    *   **Cons:** Linux-specific.
    *   **Actionable:** For a quick and effective security boost on Linux, `firejail` can wrap your `python` interpreter calls for evaluation.
*   **`Docker` / `Podman`:**
    *   **Pros:** Standard containerization. Provides process isolation, resource limits (CPU, memory), and network segmentation. You can run each organism's evaluation in a fresh container.
    *   **Cons:** Overhead of container startup/teardown for each evaluation.
    *   **Actionable:** If `0.5s timeout` is already a bottleneck, container startup might add too much. However, if you can batch evaluations within a container or pre-warm containers, it's a strong option.
*   **`nsjail` (Google):**
    *   **Pros:** A lightweight process isolation tool that uses Linux namespaces and seccomp-bpf. Very fast and efficient for sandboxing single processes.
    *   **Cons:** Linux-specific.
    *   **Actionable:** `nsjail` is an excellent choice for fast, strong sandboxing of individual Python script executions. It's often used in CTF environments.

#### Efficient Behavioral Signature Computation
Your `k-NN on output vectors` is a good start.
*   **Dimensionality Reduction:**
    *   **Concept:** If your output vectors are high-dimensional, `k-NN` can be slow and suffer from the curse of dimensionality. Reduce the dimensionality before `k-NN`.
    *   **Methods:**
        *   **PCA (Principal Component Analysis):** Simple, linear.
        *   **UMAP / t-SNE:** Non-linear, better for visualizing clusters and preserving local structure.
        *   **Autoencoders:** Train a small neural network to compress the output vectors into a lower-dimensional latent space.
    *   **Actionable:** Apply UMAP to your output vectors. Use the UMAP-transformed vectors for `k-NN` distance calculation. This can make `k-NN` faster and more meaningful.
*   **Approximate Nearest Neighbors (ANN):**
    *   **Concept:** For very large archives, exact `k-NN` is slow. ANN algorithms provide fast, approximate results.
    *   **Libraries:**
        *   **`faiss` (Facebook AI Similarity Search):** Highly optimized C++ library with Python bindings for efficient similarity search and clustering of dense vectors. Supports GPU.
        *   **`annoy` (Spotify):** C++ library with Python bindings for approximate nearest neighbors in high-dimensional spaces.
        *   **`hnswlib`:** Another fast ANN library.
    *   **Actionable:** If your novelty archive grows very large (thousands of entries), integrate `faiss` or `annoy` for much faster `k-NN` lookups.
*   **Behavioral Characterization beyond Output Vectors:**
    *   As mentioned in "Novelty Search Saturation," consider internal states, control flow traces, or LLM-generated descriptions. These can capture more nuanced behaviors.

#### AutoML / Neural Architecture Search (NAS) World Transfer
*   **Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for Program Representation:**
    *   **Concept:** NAS heavily uses GNNs to represent and reason about neural network architectures (which are DAGs). You can apply the same idea to your reasoning DAGs.
    *   **Transfer:** Train a GNN to embed your primitive DAGs into a vector space. This embedding can then be used for:
        *   **Surrogate Fitness Prediction:** Input to your surrogate model.
        *   **Behavioral Signature:** The GNN embedding itself can be a behavioral signature.
        *   **LLM Context:** Provide the GNN embedding to the LLM as additional context for mutation.
    *   **Libraries:** `PyTorch Geometric (PyG)`, `Deep Graph Library (DGL)`.
    *   **Actionable:** This is a more advanced step, but consider it for richer program representation. Start by training a simple GNN (e.g., GCN, GAT) to classify or regress on some simple property of your DAGs.
*   **Search Space Definition and Pruning:**
    *   **Concept:** NAS often defines a flexible search space and uses techniques to prune unpromising regions.
    *   **Transfer:** You have 25 fixed primitives. Can you define rules or constraints that prune the space of valid DAGs *before* LLM mutation, or guide the LLM to avoid known "bad" patterns?
    *   **Actionable:** Analyze common failure patterns or bloat-inducing structures. Create a "blacklist" of DAG patterns that the LLM should avoid or that are immediately rejected.
*   **Evolutionary NAS Algorithms:**
    *   **Concept:** Many NAS algorithms are evolutionary (e.g., ENAS, DARTS). They often use specialized mutation/crossover for graph structures.
    *   **Transfer:** Look at their graph mutation operators for inspiration for your own LLM-assisted wiring mutations.
*   **"Neural Architecture Search: A Survey"** by *T. Elsken, M. B. Zela, and F. Hutter* (2019, *Journal of Machine Learning Research*). While older, it provides a comprehensive overview of techniques, many of which are graph-based.
