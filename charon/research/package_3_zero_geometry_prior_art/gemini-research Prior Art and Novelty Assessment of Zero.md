A highly promising avenue involves representing these objects as points in a geometric space defined by the low-lying zeros of their associated $L$-functions. This produces a sequence of points in high-dimensional space that can theoretically act as coordinates in a metric space. By applying a raw $k$-nearest neighbor search in this space, one intends to recover arithmetic identities, such as those governed by the modularity theorem, and to cluster objects by invariants like analytic rank. These studies involve massive computations of zeros at high altitudes or across large families to generate histograms of consecutive spacings or $n$-level correlations.
