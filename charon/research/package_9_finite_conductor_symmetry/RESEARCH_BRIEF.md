# Research Package 9: Symmetry Type at Finite Conductor
## Priority: MEDIUM — bounds the reliability of our cross-family claims

---

## Research Question

At conductor ≤ 5000 with 10-20 zeros per object, are the Katz-Sarnak symmetry type predictions (orthogonal, symplectic, unitary) reliably distinguishable? Or are we in a pre-asymptotic regime where all families look similar?

## Context

Our system compares zero vectors across object types. The council raised the concern that different types have different background symmetry types, making cross-type Euclidean distances unreliable. But if symmetry types aren't distinguishable at our conductor/zero count, then the concern is moot — and so is any symmetry-type-based explanation of our results.

## Specific Questions

1. How many zeros of an L-function are needed to reliably distinguish SO(even) from SO(odd) symmetry? What conductor is needed for the 1-level density to be a reliable discriminator?

2. At conductor N ≈ 1000-5000 for elliptic curve L-functions, is the 1-level density already in the asymptotic regime? Or do finite-conductor corrections dominate?

3. The "support of the test function" in 1-level density computations — Iwaniec-Luo-Sarnak achieved support up to (-2,2). What support is needed to distinguish orthogonal from unitary? Can our 10-20 zeros provide that support?

4. Miller (2004) computed 1-level densities for families of elliptic curves. At what conductor did the data convincingly match the predicted symmetry type? Was there a "crossover" region where the data was ambiguous?

5. For weight-2 modular forms with non-trivial character at level ≤ 5000 — has anyone computed their 1-level density and confirmed the predicted symmetry type? Or is this an open question at small level?
