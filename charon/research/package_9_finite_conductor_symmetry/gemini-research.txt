Symmetry Type Distinguishability and Finite-Conductor Effects in Families of L-functionsIntroduction and Theoretical ContextThe statistical distribution of the zeros of L-functions represents a cornerstone of modern analytic number theory, providing profound insights into the arithmetic geometry of elliptic curves, modular forms, and Dirichlet characters. Historically, the connection between number theory and quantum chaos was crystallized when it was observed that the pair correlation of the high-lying zeros of the Riemann zeta function mimics the eigenvalue spacing of random Hermitian matrices drawn from the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE). This observation, pioneered by Montgomery and Dyson and subsequently verified by Odlyzko through the computation of millions of zeros near the $10^{20}$th zero , laid the foundation for Random Matrix Theory (RMT) as an indispensable predictive tool.However, the modern paradigm, driven by the Katz-Sarnak density conjecture, shifts the focus from the high-lying zeros of a single L-function to the low-lying zeros—those closest to the central point $s = 1/2$—averaged over a family of L-functions. The Katz-Sarnak philosophy posits that as the analytic conductor $N$ of a family approaches infinity, the statistical distribution of these low-lying zeros asymptotically aligns with the distribution of eigenvalues near $1$ of matrices drawn from specific classical compact groups: the Unitary group $U(N)$, the Symplectic group $USp(2N)$, and the Orthogonal group $O(2N)$, which splits into the special orthogonal sub-groups $SO(even)$ and $SO(odd)$.In large-scale computational systems designed to map and compare zero vectors across diverse L-function types, a critical theoretical vulnerability must be addressed: whether the underlying asymptotic symmetry type of a given family dictates the structural geometry of its zero vectors to such an extent that cross-family Euclidean distances become fundamentally unreliable. The foundational premise of this concern is that an algorithm calculating the Euclidean distance between a zero vector from an orthogonal family and a zero vector from a unitary family will measure an inherent geometric distortion caused by their differing eigenvalue repulsions, rather than any meaningful arithmetic similarity.However, this theoretical premise relies entirely on the assumption that the zero statistics are operating within the asymptotic regime. The analytical problem arises when evaluating these families at a strict, finite conductor interval, specifically $N \le 5000$, while utilizing highly truncated zero vectors containing only the first $10$ to $20$ zeros per object. At such small conductors, the statistical framework is subjected to massive finite-conductor corrections, soft repulsion dynamics, and arithmetic lower-order terms that severely distort the universal limits prescribed by Random Matrix Theory.This exhaustive research report investigates the distinguishability of symmetry types under the rigorous constraints of conductor $N \le 5000$ and zero-vector lengths of $10-20$. It analyzes the required support for test functions in the 1-level density, the precise nature of the pre-asymptotic crossover regime for elliptic curve L-functions, and the prevailing behavior of weight-2 modular forms with non-trivial characters. Ultimately, the analysis resolves whether the Katz-Sarnak symmetry types act as a reliable discriminator at small conductors, or whether finite-conductor arithmetic artifacts fully obfuscate the asymptotic symmetries, thereby rendering concerns over cross-family distance metrics moot.The Formulation of the 1-Level DensityTo precisely quantify the distribution of low-lying zeros across a family of L-functions, the primary mathematical statistic utilized is the 1-level density. For a finite family of L-functions $\mathcal{F}_N$ consisting of functions $f$ with analytic conductors $c_f \approx N$, the 1-level density evaluates a localized, weighted sum over the non-trivial zeros $\rho_f = 1/2 + i\gamma_f$. Assuming the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis (GRH), the ordinates $\gamma_f$ are entirely real.The density is defined utilizing an even Schwartz test function $\phi(x)$ that is localized near the origin. The test function is chosen such that its Fourier transform $\hat{\phi}(y)$ has compact support in a restricted interval $(-\sigma, \sigma)$. The 1-level density $D_{1,\mathcal{F}_N}(\phi)$ is defined as:$$D_{1,\mathcal{F}_N}(\phi) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}_N|} \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}_N} \sum_{\gamma_f} \phi\left( \frac{\gamma_f \log c_f}{2\pi} \right)$$The scaling factor $\frac{\log c_f}{2\pi}$ normalizes the zeros such that the average spacing between them is equal to $1$. Because $\phi(x)$ is a Schwartz function, it decays rapidly as $x \to \infty$. Consequently, only the "low-lying" zeros—those within a distance $\ll 1/\log c_f$ of the central point $s = 1/2$—contribute significantly to the sum. The higher-lying zeros are naturally suppressed by the rapid decay of the test function.As the conductor $N$ tends to infinity, the Katz-Sarnak conjecture dictates that the 1-level density converges to the integral of the test function against the probability density function of the corresponding classical compact group $\mathcal{W}_G(x)$ :$$\lim_{N \to \infty} D_{1,\mathcal{F}_N}(\phi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(x) \mathcal{W}_G(x) dx$$By the application of Plancherel's Theorem, this integral can be evaluated equivalently in the Fourier domain by integrating the Fourier transform of the test function $\hat{\phi}(u)$ against the Fourier transform of the group density $\hat{\mathcal{W}}_G(u)$. This transformation is critical because the distinguishability of the various symmetry types is entirely dependent on the structural variations within $\hat{\mathcal{W}}_G(u)$.Distinguishing Symmetry Types via the Fourier DomainThe ability to categorize a family of L-functions into its correct Katz-Sarnak symmetry type hinges upon whether the integral of the 1-level density yields a unique value for each specific group. This uniqueness is dictated by the support of the test function's Fourier transform, denoted by $\sigma$.Let $\delta_0(u)$ denote the standard Dirac delta functional, and let $\eta(u)$ denote the indicator function which equals $1$ for $|u| < 1$, $1/2$ for $|u| = 1$, and $0$ for $|u| > 1$. The theoretical Fourier transforms of the 1-level densities for the classical compact groups are established as follows :| Symmetry Group | Fourier Transform of Density $\hat{\mathcal{W}}_G(u)$ | Evaluated for restricted support $|u| < 1$ || :--- | :--- | :--- || Unitary | $\delta_0(u)$ | $\delta_0(u)$ || Symplectic | $\delta_0(u) - \frac{1}{2}\eta(u)$ | $\delta_0(u) - \frac{1}{2}$ || Orthogonal (O) | $\delta_0(u) + \frac{1}{2}$ | $\delta_0(u) + \frac{1}{2}$ || SO(even) | $\delta_0(u) + \frac{1}{2}\eta(u)$ | $\delta_0(u) + \frac{1}{2}$ || SO(odd) | $\delta_0(u) - \frac{1}{2}\eta(u) + 1$ | $\delta_0(u) + \frac{1}{2}$ |The Distinction Between Unitary, Symplectic, and OrthogonalA critical examination of the Fourier transforms reveals the necessary conditions for distinguishing the overarching families. For any test function whose Fourier transform has an arbitrarily small positive support (i.e., $\sigma > 0$), the Unitary, Symplectic, and Orthogonal ensembles generate mathematically distinct integrals.To reliably distinguish an Orthogonal family from a Unitary family, the necessary support is merely $\sigma > 0$. When evaluating the integral for $|u| < 1$, the Orthogonal density contains the constant term $+\frac{1}{2}$, whereas the Unitary density strictly evaluates to the Dirac delta $\delta_0(u)$ with no additional background term. Similarly, the Symplectic density subtracts $\frac{1}{2}$. Therefore, any numerical computation of the 1-level density that successfully captures a statistically significant sample of low-lying zeros will immediately reveal whether the symmetry is Unitary, Symplectic, or Orthogonal, regardless of how highly restricted the support of the test function is.The Support Barrier for SO(even) and SO(odd)While separating the macroscopic symmetry classes requires negligible support, differentiating the sub-classes of the Orthogonal group—$SO(even)$ and $SO(odd)$—presents a formidable analytic constraint. As demonstrated in the table above, the Fourier transforms of the 1-level densities for $O$, $SO(even)$, and $SO(odd)$ are perfectly identical for $|u| < 1$. The indicator function $\eta(u)$ ensures that the structural differences between even and odd orthogonal matrices only manifest when $|u| \ge 1$.Consequently, to reliably distinguish $SO(even)$ from $SO(odd)$ symmetry using solely the 1-level density, the support of the test function's Fourier transform must be rigorously extended beyond the interval $(-1, 1)$. The groundbreaking theorem of Iwaniec, Luo, and Sarnak (ILS) achieved test function support up to $(-2, 2)$ for families of weight $k$ cuspidal newforms of level $N$, thereby permitting the definitive classification of $SO(even)$ and $SO(odd)$ based on the sign of the functional equation.However, achieving support beyond $(-1, 1)$ analytically is extraordinarily difficult. It requires profound, unconditional control over the non-diagonal terms in the Petersson trace formula and highly complex estimates of Kloosterman sums. For many families of L-functions, the analytic tools available do not permit extending the support beyond $(-1, 1)$ unconditionally.If the support is restricted to $(-1, 1)$, the 1-level density acts as a degenerate discriminator between $SO(even)$ and $SO(odd)$. In such scenarios, researchers must rely on the 2-level density. Steven J. Miller demonstrated that the 2-level density—which evaluates pairs of low-lying zeros—successfully distinguishes all three orthogonal ensembles for arbitrarily small support, bypassing the $(-1, 1)$ barrier inherent in the 1-level density.The Myth of the Zero-Count ConstraintWithin computational geometry and vector analysis, there is a recurring concern regarding the dimensionality of the zero vectors. Specifically, whether possessing only $10$ to $20$ zeros per object is mathematically sufficient to provide the support needed to distinguish symmetry types, particularly Orthogonal from Unitary.This concern is rooted in a fundamental misinterpretation of how the Katz-Sarnak statistics operate. The 1-level density is an average taken over a family of objects, not an analysis of the deep spectrum of a single object. The statistic is designed explicitly to measure the distribution of the lowest-lying zeros.When observing $10$ to $20$ zeros per L-function across a sufficiently large family, the zero count per object is not a constraint. The test function $\phi(x)$, by definition of being a Schwartz function, rapidly converges to zero as the ordinates increase. The overwhelming majority of the integral's value is derived from the first few zeros. The 10th or 20th zero of an L-function is located so far up the critical line (relative to the scaling factor $\frac{\log c_f}{2\pi}$) that its contribution to the 1-level density is functionally microscopic.The limitation in providing support for the test function's Fourier transform does not arise from truncating the vector at 20 zeros; it arises from the difficulty of bounding the prime sums in the Explicit Formula as the support parameter $\sigma$ increases. Therefore, a vector containing $10-20$ zeros is entirely adequate, and indeed mathematically optimal, to provide the functional coverage necessary to distinguish Unitary from Orthogonal symmetry, provided the family size is statistically significant. The barrier to distinguishability lies in the conductor size, not the zero count per object.The Pre-Asymptotic Regime: Finite-Conductor Dynamics at $N \le 5000$While Random Matrix Theory provides a mathematically elegant framework for infinite conductors, the application of this theory to conductors in the range of $N \approx 1000 - 5000$ encounters catastrophic interference from finite-conductor effects. The central inquiry of this report is whether the 1-level density for elliptic curve L-functions at $N \le 5000$ operates in an asymptotic regime or a pre-asymptotic regime.The empirical and theoretical evidence overwhelmingly dictates that $N \le 5000$ represents a deeply pre-asymptotic state where arithmetic corrections entirely dominate the geometric symmetry. The Katz-Sarnak limits are only recovered when the conductor $N \to \infty$. In the finite domain, the statistics are polluted by highly structured lower-order terms.The Explicit Formula and the Magnitude of Lower-Order TermsTo study the zeros of the functions in a family, analytic number theorists rely on the Explicit Formula, which converts sums over the complex zeros into sums over prime powers. Evaluating this formula yields an asymptotic expansion where the 1-level density $D_{1, \mathcal{F}_N}(\phi)$ is expressed as the main RMT term plus an infinite series of lower-order terms scaled by inverse powers of the logarithm of the conductor.The expansion generally takes the form:$$D_{1,\mathcal{F}_N}(\phi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(x) \mathcal{W}_G(x) dx + \frac{c_1}{\log N} + \frac{c_2}{(\log N)^2} + \frac{c_3}{(\log N)^3} + \dots$$The magnitude of these lower-order terms is directly responsible for the pre-asymptotic distortion. For a conductor $N \approx 5000$, the natural logarithm evaluates to $\log(5000) \approx 8.5$. Consequently, the primary scaling factor for the first finite-conductor correction is $1/8.5 \approx 0.117$. This signifies that the first lower-order term alters the expected density by nearly $12\%$. When $N \approx 1000$, the scaling factor is $1/6.9 \approx 0.144$, generating a $14.4\%$ distortion.These scaling factors are massive. They easily engulf the subtle theoretical differences between the asymptotic integrals of the various symmetry types, especially when evaluated over restricted test functions.Arithmetic Contamination: The Constants $c_i$These lower-order terms are not random statistical noise; they are structured arithmetic artifacts that reveal profound differences between families that share the identical asymptotic symmetry type. The magnitude and sign of the lower-order terms depend heavily on specific arithmetic properties of the curves within the family, represented by the constants $c_i$.An analysis of these constants illuminates why the symmetry type is obscured at small conductors:Lower-Order ConstantArithmetic Origin and Influence on 1-Level Density$c_{1,i}$Average Logarithm of Conductors: Provides a lower-order approximation to the average of the logarithm of the conductors for the families. It depends on the weighting function and normalizes the high zeros.$c_{3,i}, c_{4,i}, c_{6,i}$Effects of Bad Reduction: These constants measure the arithmetical impact of primes that divide the conductor $N$. $c_{3,i}$ measures the effect of how many primes divide the conductor, while $c_{6,i}$ is proportional to the number of independent divisors. Families with a relatively large number of primes of bad reduction surprisingly exhibit relatively fewer low-lying zeros.$c_{5,i}$Arithmetical Average of Coefficients: Derived from averaging the Dirichlet coefficients $\lambda(p^\nu)$. Low-lying zeros are more numerous in families that possess a relatively large number of points modulo $p$. If a family is "biased" (possessing many points mod $p$ for small primes), this term can grow arbitrarily large.$e_i$Congruence Conditions: Reflects conditions such as $a \equiv a_0 \pmod q$, fixing arithmetical properties that are reflected directly in the lower-order density.This arithmetic dependence creates a profound paradox at $N \le 5000$. Two entirely distinct families of elliptic curves may both inherently belong to the $SO(even)$ symmetry class asymptotically. However, they will display radically different 1-level densities at $N = 5000$ solely because one family has conductor factorizations dominated by two large primes while the other is dominated by three, altering $c_{6,i}$. Furthermore, differences in torsion groups (e.g., $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ versus $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$) cause the arithmetical average of coefficients $c_{5,i}$ to diverge rapidly.Because these arithmetic biases manifest so strongly at the $N \approx 1000 - 5000$ scale, the 1-level density acts as a signature of the family's arithmetic composition rather than its Katz-Sarnak symmetry type. The system is fundamentally entrenched in a pre-asymptotic regime where finite-conductor corrections effectively blind any cross-family distance metric to the true underlying asymptotic symmetry.Miller's Numerical Data, Soft Repulsion, and the Crossover RegionTo conclusively substantiate the pre-asymptotic nature of elliptic curve families at moderate conductors, it is vital to analyze the pivotal 2004 and 2006 investigations conducted by Steven J. Miller. Miller computed the one- and two-level densities for rational families of elliptic curves, generating massive datasets to detect the underlying group symmetries. The inquiry into whether there is a "crossover" region where the data is ambiguous, and at what conductor the data convincingly matched the predicted symmetry type, forms the empirical backbone bounding the reliability of cross-family claims.The Discovery of Soft RepulsionIn the limit of large conductors, the distribution of the first zero of elliptic curve L-functions is theoretically mandated to tend to the large-$N$ limit of the first eigenvalues of $SO(2N)$ matrices. However, in 2004, Miller discovered a significant, qualitative discrepancy for finite conductors that could not be explained by simply employing finite-sized random matrices or by the inclusion of standard lower-order arithmetical terms.At finite conductors, the zeros of elliptic curve L-functions exhibited a massive "repulsion" from the central point $s=1/2$. The probability of finding an L-function within the family with a low first zero was drastically lower than the standard $SO(2N)$ model predicted. This phenomenon, entirely unpredicted by foundational RMT, became known as "soft repulsion".The Discretization of Central ValuesThe underlying mechanism for this repulsion is geometric and arithmetic. By the formulas of Waldspurger, Kohnen-Zagier, and Baruch-Mao, the central values of elliptic curve L-functions are not continuous variables. Instead, they are highly discrete: they either evaluate exactly to zero (in the case of odd rank), or they are greater than or equal to a strict, finite minimal size determined by the arithmetic of the curve.Because the central value cannot be arbitrarily small without being identically zero, a natural void is created in the distribution of low-lying zeros. The L-functions simply cannot possess zeros infinitesimally close to the central point without violating the arithmetic minimal size constraint.The Excised Orthogonal EnsembleTo mathematically model this "sieving-off" of small values, Miller and his collaborators introduced the Excised Orthogonal Ensemble. This modified random matrix model takes the standard ensemble of $SO(2N)$ matrices and explicitly cuts out (excises) any matrix whose characteristic polynomial evaluated at the symmetry point falls below a specific threshold.Let $\Lambda_A(e^{i\theta}, N) = \det(I - A e^{-i\theta})$ be the characteristic polynomial of a matrix $A \in SO(2N)$. The Excised Orthogonal Ensemble considers only those matrices where $|\Lambda_A(1, N)| \ge \exp(X)$, utilizing a cut-off value $\exp(X)$. The joint probability density function is multiplied by the Heaviside step function $H(\log|\Lambda_A(1, N)| - X)$, perfectly capturing the two distinct features of finite-conductor elliptic curves :The Hard Gap: An exponentially small interval near the origin where absolutely no zeros lie, corresponding to the void created by the arithmetic minimal size.The Soft Repulsion: A vast region beyond the hard gap where the density of zeros is highly suppressed, interpolating slowly back to the bulk distribution of the un-excised orthogonal ensemble.Conductor Ranges and the Reality of the CrossoverMiller tested these hypotheses across massive datasets, evaluating conductors across ranges such as $40,000$ to $130,000$ , and further expanding the analysis to databases of curves with conductors extending up to $10^7$ and $10^8$.The critical observation from this data is that the soft repulsion effect decreases as the conductor increases. In the matrix model, to achieve quantitative agreement with the number-theoretical data, the cut-off scale $-X$ must be proportional to the matrix size $N$. As the conductor $N \to \infty$, the effects of the excision diminish relative to the overall density, and the distribution ultimately recovers the limiting $SO(2N)$ behavior.However, the "crossover" region where the data is ambiguous is vast. At $N \approx 1000 - 5000$, the data is entirely subjugated by soft repulsion. This is not merely an ambiguous region; it is a regime where the arithmetic constraints violently push the zeros away from the origin, causing the 1-level density to look absolutely nothing like the un-excised asymptotic $SO(2N)$ prediction.Furthermore, Miller observed that the repulsion is highly dependent on the rank of the family. There is greater repulsion in the first normalized zero for subsets of curves of rank 2 from one-parameter families of rank 0, compared to subsets of curves of rank 2 from one-parameter families of rank 2.Rank ParameterBehavior of Low-Lying Zeros in Miller's DataRank 0 FamiliesExhibit the standard soft repulsion modeled by the Excised Orthogonal Ensemble.Rank 2 FamiliesShow distinct repulsion characteristics. Differences between adjacent normalized zeros remain statistically independent of repulsion, but the macroscopic density is heavily shifted.The data only begins to display uninhibited orthogonal symmetry limits at conductors several orders of magnitude higher (e.g., $N > 10^6$), and even then, lower-order arithmetical artifacts require careful isolation. Therefore, for elliptic curve L-functions at $N \approx 1000-5000$, there is no convincing match with the predicted asymptotic symmetry type. The 1-level density is entirely dominated by the finite-conductor artifacts of the Excised Ensemble.Weight-2 Modular Forms with Non-Trivial CharacterThe complexity of distinguishing symmetry types at small conductors extends beyond elliptic curves with trivial characters to higher-order constructs, specifically weight-2 modular forms with non-trivial Dirichlet characters.Theoretical Symmetry Predictions and the Unitary ShiftThe Katz-Sarnak philosophy provides clear symmetry predictions based on the functional equation of the L-function and the arithmetic nature of the character. For a family of modular forms attached to a character $\chi$:If the character $\chi$ is trivial, or if $\chi$ is a quadratic Dirichlet character (taking values strictly in $\{-1, 1\}$), the coefficients of the L-function remain self-dual. The family generally inherits Orthogonal or Symplectic symmetry, depending on the sign of the functional equations (root numbers). For example, the 1-level density for holomorphic even weight $k$ cuspidal newforms of square-free level $N$ is Orthogonal.If the character $\chi$ is non-quadratic (complex), the self-duality is broken. The non-trivial, non-quadratic character destroys the constraints that force the eigenvalues into orthogonal or symplectic pairings. Thus, the expected asymptotic symmetry type shifts to Unitary symmetry.The expected asymptotic 1-level density for weight-2 modular forms with non-trivial, non-quadratic characters is therefore the Unitary density, whose Fourier transform is simply $\delta_0(u)$.Empirical Confirmation at Small Levels ($N \le 5000$)The specific inquiry is whether this predicted Unitary symmetry has been computationally confirmed for weight-2 modular forms with non-trivial characters at levels $N \le 5000$, or if it remains an open question at such small levels.The theoretical proofs establishing these symmetries for large families have been rigorously confirmed in the literature, with major contributions by Iwaniec, Luo, Sarnak, Hughes, Rudnick, and Miller. However, achieving a pure empirical confirmation at the exceptionally low bound of level $N \le 5000$ is functionally impossible due to the identical finite-conductor pathology observed in elliptic curves.While one can theoretically compute the 1-level density for these forms, observing a pristine Unitary distribution at $N \le 5000$ is prevented by the scale of the pre-asymptotic corrections. The Explicit Formula demands that the lower-order terms $\mathcal{O}(1/\log N)$ dictate the numerical output. As previously calculated, at $N \le 5000$, $\log(N) \approx 8.5$, rendering the pre-asymptotic noise roughly $11-15\%$ of the main term.To confirm the symmetry computationally at small levels, modern number theorists rely on the Ratios Conjecture. Formulated by Conrey, Farmer, Keating, Rubinstein, and Snaith, the Ratios Conjecture provides a highly sophisticated heuristic to predict the exact form of the lower-order terms. The Ratios Conjecture demonstrates that the 1-level density at small levels is heavily skewed by the specific sums of the Dirichlet coefficients $\lambda(p)$ for the family in question.Researchers, including Miller and Montague, have tested the accuracy of the Ratios Conjecture for various families, finding agreement with the finite-level data up to an error of $\mathcal{O}(X^{1/2+\epsilon})$. However, this confirms the Ratios Conjecture, not the asymptotic pure RMT limit. Consequently, while the predicted symmetry type (Unitary) is established mathematically, numerical computations at level $\le 5000$ do not "convincingly match" the pure Unitary RMT density in a vacuum. The computational output matches a heavily distorted Ratios prediction.Distinguishing a Unitary family of modular forms from an Orthogonal family of elliptic curves at level 5000 using pure Euclidean distance of zero vectors is therefore highly volatile. The variance induced by the $1/\log N$ lower-order terms easily exceeds the variance induced by the differing main RMT terms.Structural Implications for Cross-Family Distance AlgorithmsThe insights generated by evaluating the $N \le 5000$ conductor constraint yield profound implications for algorithmic systems designed to map and compare zero vectors across different L-function types.The original concern raised by the council was that different L-function types possess different background symmetry types (Orthogonal, Symplectic, Unitary), inherently rendering cross-type Euclidean distances of zero vectors unreliable. If an algorithm attempts to measure the similarity between an elliptic curve (Orthogonal) and a modular form with a complex character (Unitary) by computing the Euclidean distance of their first 20 zeros, the baseline geometry of the zeros is theoretically offset by the differing eigenvalue repulsions of their respective symmetry groups. If this offset is large, the Euclidean distance is measuring the Katz-Sarnak class, not the intrinsic arithmetic relationship between the objects.However, the exhaustive analysis of the pre-asymptotic regime demonstrates that this theoretical concern is effectively moot at the operational parameters of $N \le 5000$.The Dominance of Pre-Asymptotic Artifacts Over Geometric SymmetryAt conductors ranging from 1000 to 5000, the zero statistics are overwhelmingly governed by finite-conductor corrections. The universal asymptotic symmetry types predicted by Katz and Sarnak only emerge cleanly as $N \to \infty$. In the finite-conductor regime, the 1-level density and the specific locations of the first 10-20 zeros are aggressively distorted by three primary forces:Lower-Order Term Contamination: Generating structural offsets proportional to $1/\log N$ ($\approx 0.11$ to $0.14$), which heavily skew the zero densities based on the arithmetic sums of the primes.Soft Repulsion and Excision: For elliptic curves, the discretization of central values forcibly evacuates zeros from the origin, creating a hard gap and a repulsive drift that does not correspond to standard Orthogonal matrix physics.Bad Reduction Scaling: Families with highly composite conductors experience arithmetic dampening of low-lying zeros entirely independent of their symmetry class.The True Measurement of the Vector MetricWhen a system calculates the Euclidean distance between zero vectors at $N \le 5000$, it is not measuring the theoretical delta between pure Orthogonal and pure Unitary symmetries. The geometric differences caused by the background symmetry types are completely subordinated by the variance in the lower-order arithmetic terms.Instead, the Euclidean distance algorithm is inadvertently acting as a highly sensitive metric for arithmetic congruence. It is measuring the differences in the number of points modulo $p$, the structural differences in the bad reduction primes (the prime factorization of the conductor), and the effects of the excised central values.Therefore, if the system discovers that two families "look similar" or cluster tightly together in high-dimensional vector space at this conductor range, it is because they share similar finite-conductor arithmetic properties, not because they are bypassing their asymptotic symmetry types. The pre-asymptotic regime acts as an overwhelming geometric equalizer that masks the theoretical Katz-Sarnak groups beneath a thick layer of arithmetical lower-order dynamics. The concern that underlying symmetries make cross-type distances unreliable is invalidated by the sheer magnitude of the finite-conductor noise.Synthesis and Final DeterminationsThe exhaustive evaluation of finite-conductor dynamics, lower-order term expansions, and test function support requirements yields the following definitive resolutions to the core research questions:1. Distinguishing Symmetries and Zero Counts
Distinguishing $SO(even)$ from $SO(odd)$ symmetry strictly requires a test function whose Fourier transform has support exceeding the interval $(-1, 1)$. If analytical constraints restrict the support to $(-1, 1)$, the 1-level density is entirely blind to the difference, and the 2-level density must be utilized. Conversely, distinguishing the broader Orthogonal class from the Unitary or Symplectic classes requires only any arbitrarily small support $\sigma > 0$. The constraint of having only 10-20 zeros per object is a non-issue; the 1-level density operates exclusively on the lowest-lying zeros, and the Schwartz test function rapidly dampens higher zeros. Statistical power is derived from the size of the family, not the length of the zero vector for a single object.2. The Asymptotic State of Elliptic Curves at $N \approx 1000 - 5000$
The 1-level density of elliptic curves at conductors $1000 - 5000$ is unequivocally operating in a pre-asymptotic regime. Finite-conductor corrections heavily dominate the distribution. The Explicit Formula expansion relies on $(\log N)^{-1}$, which at this scale enforces massive lower-order arithmetic biases driven by the number of points modulo $p$ and the number of independent prime divisors of the conductor.3. Miller's 2004 Crossover Data
Miller's analysis of elliptic curves revealed that at $N \le 130,000$, the data does not convincingly match the pure $SO(2N)$ asymptotic prediction due to the phenomenon of "soft repulsion". The necessity of central values to exist as discrete quantities creates a hard gap near the origin, requiring modeling via the Excised Orthogonal Ensemble. At $N \approx 1000-5000$, the system is completely entrenched within this repulsive crossover region, rendering pure symmetry matching impossible.4. Weight-2 Modular Forms with Non-Trivial Character
For weight-2 modular forms, the introduction of a non-trivial, non-quadratic character alters the expected symmetry to Unitary. While this theoretical classification is highly secure, computations at $N \le 5000$ do not yield pristine, asymptotic Unitary curves. The identical $1/\log N$ lower-order term scaling affects these functions, rendering empirical data heavily reliant on Ratios Conjecture corrections to achieve any semblance of a match.Conclusion on Cross-Type ReliabilityThe operational concern that different L-function types possess varying background symmetry types—thus invalidating cross-type Euclidean distances—is fundamentally superseded by the reality of the pre-asymptotic regime. At conductors $\le 5000$, the true Katz-Sarnak symmetries are heavily obscured by finite-conductor arithmetic corrections, soft repulsion, and lower-order term variance. All families exhibit profound geometric distortion relative to their asymptotic limits. Any symmetry-type-based explanation of the vector results at this scale is moot; the similarities and distances captured by the algorithm are predominantly a reflection of localized arithmetic properties rather than universal asymptotic symmetries.
