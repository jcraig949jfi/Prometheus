Random Matrix Theory and Eigenvalue Ablation: Theoretical Grounding for Ensemble Classification and L-Function Zero ClusteringIntroduction and Contextual FrameworkRecent empirical clustering analyses of L-function zero vectors have uncovered a counterintuitive phenomenon regarding feature selection and geometric classification. Specifically, when attempting to cluster families of L-functions by rank, the removal of the first non-trivial zero from the feature vector measurably improves geometric rank clustering accuracy. Baseline clustering utilizing all low-lying zeros yields an Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) of 0.5456. However, when the first zero is ablated, the ARI increases to 0.5486. Furthermore, restricting the feature vector exclusively to an intermediate subset of zeros—specifically zeros 5 through 19—yields the optimal classification result, achieving an ARI of 0.5548.From a purely number-theoretic perspective, this finding initially appears paradoxical. The first zero is mathematically the most "structured" element of the L-function's spectrum; it directly encodes the analytic rank via its order of vanishing and carries profound arithmetic information regarding the specific curve or form. However, in the context of unsupervised machine learning and geometric clustering, this highly specific structure acts not as a signal, but as classification noise. The first zero actively degrades the algorithm's ability to group curves by their broader geometric families.This comprehensive research report provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for this empirical finding by establishing a direct equivalence with Random Matrix Theory (RMT). In RMT, the problem of identifying which Gaussian ensemble—the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE), the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE), or the Gaussian Symplectic Ensemble (GSE)—generated a given finite sample of eigenvalues is directly analogous to classifying L-function families. By analyzing the statistical dichotomy between the spectral bulk and the spectral edge, the unique distributions governing extreme eigenvalues (such as the Tracy-Widom distribution), and the Katz-Sarnak philosophy connecting random matrices to L-functions, this report demonstrates that eigenvalue ablation is a fundamental requirement for optimal ensemble discrimination. The theoretical mechanisms driving this phenomenon confirm that extreme eigenvalues—and by extension, the lowest-lying L-function zeros—are dominated by localized, finite-scale perturbations that obscure the universal, global structure required for precise family classification.The Statistical Mechanics of Random Matrix EnsemblesTo establish the theoretical basis for eigenvalue ablation, it is necessary to first examine the generative structures of the primary random matrix ensembles. The classification of a random matrix relies on identifying the underlying symmetry constraints of the physical or mathematical system it models, a framework formalized by Dyson's Threefold Way.The Gaussian Ensembles and the Dyson IndexThe three classical invariant ensembles are defined by the nature of their matrix entries and their behavior under rotational similarity transformations. The Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) consists of real symmetric matrices and models systems with time-reversal invariance and rotational symmetry. The Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE) comprises complex Hermitian matrices and models systems where time-reversal symmetry is broken. The Gaussian Symplectic Ensemble (GSE) consists of quaternion self-dual matrices and applies to systems with time-reversal symmetry but broken rotational symmetry, typically involving half-integer spin.These ensembles are mathematically parameterized by the Dyson index, denoted as $\beta$, which counts the number of real components per matrix element. Consequently, $\beta = 1$ for the GOE, $\beta = 2$ for the GUE, and $\beta = 4$ for the GSE. The Dyson index is the fundamental parameter that an ensemble classification algorithm attempts to identify.The Joint Probability Density Function and the Coulomb GasThe theoretical justification for why extreme eigenvalues contain less classification information than intermediate eigenvalues begins with the Joint Probability Density Function (JPDF) of the matrix spectrum. For a matrix of dimension $N$, the JPDF of its eigenvalues $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots, \lambda_N)$ in the Gaussian ensembles is given by:$$P(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_N) = \frac{1}{Z_{N,\beta}} \prod_{1 \le i < j \le N} |\lambda_i - \lambda_j|^\beta \exp\left( -\frac{\beta}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \lambda_i^2 \right)$$where $Z_{N,\beta}$ represents the normalization constant. This mathematical formulation is deeply analogous to the statistical mechanics of a one-dimensional Coulomb gas in thermal equilibrium at an inverse temperature proportional to $\beta$.The JPDF consists of two competing forces. The exponential term, $\exp(-\frac{\beta}{2} \sum \lambda_i^2)$, represents a global harmonic confining potential that binds the eigenvalues near the origin, preventing the gas from expanding infinitely. The product term, $\prod |\lambda_i - \lambda_j|^\beta$, is the Vandermonde determinant, which induces a logarithmic repulsive force between every pair of eigenvalues. This pairwise repulsion means that it is statistically highly improbable for two eigenvalues to occupy the same state or exist in close proximity.The critical insight derived from the JPDF is that the classifying parameter $\beta$ strictly governs the strength of the pairwise repulsion. Because this interaction is pairwise and systemic, its aggregate structural signature—the "rigidity" or "level repulsion" of the spectrum—is most profoundly expressed in regions where a given eigenvalue interacts with the maximum number of neighbors. Bulk statistics pertain to intervals inside the dense support of the limiting spectral measure, whereas edge statistics pertain to the boundaries of the support. This dichotomy directly addresses the research question regarding whether the removal of the smallest eigenvalue improves ensemble classification accuracy. The statistical laws governing these two regions are fundamentally divergent.Universality and Level Repulsion in the BulkIn the bulk of the spectrum, the local spacing between adjacent eigenvalues is governed by Wigner-Dyson-Mehta universality. In this dense interior, the eigenvalues form a highly correlated, rigid lattice dictated by the sine-kernel. The localized fluctuations of the gaps between adjacent eigenvalues follow the Wigner surmise, an analytical approximation of the Gaudin distribution. The probability $P(s)$ of observing a normalized spacing $s$ between adjacent eigenvalues in the bulk is explicitly dependent on the symmetry class:$$P_{GOE}(s) \approx \frac{\pi}{2} s \exp\left(-\frac{\pi}{4} s^2\right)$$$$P_{GUE}(s) \approx \frac{32}{\pi^2} s^2 \exp\left(-\frac{4}{\pi} s^2\right)$$$$P_{GSE}(s) \approx \frac{2^{18}}{3^6 \pi^3} s^4 \exp\left(-\frac{64}{9\pi} s^2\right)$$These distributions explicitly demonstrate that the probability of two eigenvalues being infinitely close ($s \to 0$) vanishes proportionally to $s^\beta$. Because an eigenvalue in the bulk is surrounded by neighbors on both sides, it is subjected to symmetrical, high-density repulsive forces. The competing pressures from adjacent eigenvalues lock the spacing into a stable distribution that perfectly reflects the underlying $\beta$ parameter.Empirical studies in random matrix classification confirm that ensemble discrimination power is maximized when utilizing these bulk properties. Diagnostics relying on "persistence entropy," which captures the global shape of the spacing distribution, explicitly utilize the "central 80% of eigenvalues" to classify GOE, GUE, and Wishart matrices. By intentionally ablating the top 10% and bottom 10% of the spectrum (the edges), these algorithms achieve near-perfect classification accuracy. The Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) for ensemble separation demonstrates that analyzing the bulk yields massive separation ratios, such as a Wasserstein-2 distance ratio of 25.6 when discriminating between GOE and Wishart matrices.The Volatility of the Spectral Edge: Tracy-Widom and Bessel DistributionsConversely, the extreme eigenvalues at the spectral edges are entirely divorced from the sine-kernel dynamics and the Wigner surmise. The fluctuations of the extreme eigenvalues in Gaussian ensembles are instead described by the Tracy-Widom distributions, introduced in 1994 and 1996. The Tracy-Widom law, defined via the Fredholm determinant of the Airy kernel and intimately linked to the Painlevé II non-linear differential equation, dictates the asymptotic distribution of the normalized largest (and symmetrically, the smallest) eigenvalue of a random Hermitian matrix.While the Tracy-Widom distribution was originally formulated for the largest eigenvalue, analogous work extensively characterizes the smallest eigenvalue. For symmetric Wigner matrices where the spectrum spans both negative and positive values, the smallest eigenvalue simply follows a reversed Tracy-Widom distribution. However, for positive-definite matrices such as the Wishart-Laguerre covariance ensembles (which are critical in machine learning applications), the spectrum encounters a "hard edge" at zero. In these instances, the distribution of the smallest eigenvalue is governed by Bessel kernels.The critical discriminator between the bulk and the edge is the scaling of the minimal eigenvalue spacing. In the bulk, the mean spacing between independent eigenvalues scales proportionally to $N^{-1}$. However, the spacing between the smallest eigenvalue and the second smallest eigenvalue (the minimal gap) scales uniquely: the rescaled minimal spacing scales as $N^{-3/2}$ for the GOE and $N^{-4/3}$ for the GUE. This scaling indicates that while level repulsion exists at the edge, it operates under an entirely different dynamic regime than the bulk.An extreme eigenvalue fundamentally lacks the symmetrical bounding pressures present in the bulk; it experiences repulsion only from the interior of the spectrum. Consequently, its position is subject to high variance, finite-$N$ corrections, and structural boundary effects. In the context of algorithmic classification, the smallest eigenvalue functions as a statistical outlier. Its distribution is dominated by its specific location relative to the spectral boundary (whether a soft Airy edge or a hard Bessel edge) rather than the global correlation structure dictated by $\beta$. Incorporating the smallest eigenvalue into a feature vector introduces high-variance noise that actively dilutes the rigid, highly stabilized signal of the bulk.The Katz-Sarnak Philosophy and L-Function Zero StatisticsThe empirical finding that removing the first zero improves rank clustering maps directly onto the random matrix theory dichotomy of bulk versus edge. The theoretical bridge between these two domains is the Katz-Sarnak density conjecture, which posits that the statistical distribution of the non-trivial zeros of families of L-functions, in the limit of large analytic conductors, precisely mirrors the eigenvalue distributions of large random matrices drawn from classical compact groups.The Mapping of Families to Matrix GroupsThe Katz-Sarnak philosophy dictates that different number-theoretic families map to specific symmetries. For example, the zeros of the Riemann zeta function are modeled by the Unitary group $U(N)$, analogous to the GUE. Families of elliptic curves with a positive sign in their functional equation are modeled by the special orthogonal group $SO(even)$, while those with a negative sign map to $SO(odd)$. Families of Dirichlet characters map to either unitary or symplectic groups depending on the specific characteristics of the family.In this framework, the zeros closest to the central point $s = 1/2$ (the "low-lying zeros") are analogous to the extreme eigenvalues closest to 1 in the corresponding unitary matrix ensembles. The analysis of these low-lying zeros, typically accomplished via the 1-level density statistic with restricted test functions, is the standard method for identifying the specific symmetry group of the family. However, this theoretical equivalence relies on an asymptotic limit where the analytic conductor $N \to \infty$. In finite, real-world datasets utilized for clustering algorithms, the lowest-lying zeros are subject to extreme arithmetic perturbations.Conductor Dependence and Arithmetic Noise at the First ZeroThe precise location of the first non-trivial zero of an L-function is intrinsically dependent on the analytic conductor of the specific curve or form. For a given L-function $L(s, f)$ with analytic conductor $c_f$, the first few zeros are generally expected to be located at a height proportional to $1/\log c_f$. However, empirical and numerical studies consistently reveal massive deviations from this theoretical expectation for the very first zero.Numerical evidence from finite-conductor families of elliptic curves demonstrates a distinct "repulsion" of the first zero away from the central point, a phenomenon that starkly contradicts the asymptotic $SO(2N)$ random matrix predictions. For instance, 1-level density plots for families with conductors up to 400,000 show a massive drop to zero probability near the origin.This repulsion is not a geometric property; it is an artifact of discrete arithmetic. According to the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer (BSD) conjecture, the L-function of a rank 0 curve cannot take on arbitrarily small positive values at the central point; a non-zero value below a specific cut-off $C(T)$ is never observed. This absolute lower bound on the central value forces the lowest-lying zeros to push away from the origin. To mathematically model this within RMT, researchers have had to abandon standard GOE/GUE ensembles and construct an "excised" random matrix model. In this specialized model, matrices are only drawn from the $SO(2N)$ ensemble if their characteristic polynomial at the central point exceeds a specific, conductor-dependent cutoff value $X$.Furthermore, the displacement of the first zero is profoundly modulated by other highly specific BSD invariants, most notably the Tate-Shafarevich group ($\Sha$). Recent large-scale analyses of murmuration phenomena have demonstrated that elliptic curves with a Tate-Shafarevich group of order $|\Sha| \ge 4$ exhibit a systematically distinct distribution of low-lying zeros. For these specific curves, the first zero is displaced significantly higher, creating an even larger zero-free gap near the central point, while the subsequent zeros pack more tightly. The joint Hotelling's $T^2$ test across these low-lying zeros confirms this displacement is statistically highly significant ($p = 5.4 \times 10^{-9}$), with the first zero alone bearing the majority of this discrepancy ($p = 8.0 \times 10^{-6}$).In the context of unsupervised machine learning and rank clustering, these findings provide a definitive explanation for the empirical results. The first zero is an "arithmetic sponge." Its precise location is violently perturbed by the specific analytic rank, the conductor size, the Tate-Shafarevich group, and the discretization of central values. While this high sensitivity makes the first zero incredibly valuable for analyzing the distinct arithmetic properties of an individual curve, it renders the first zero highly detrimental for classifying the broad geometric family to which the curve belongs. The inclusion of the first zero introduces massive within-class variance (classification noise) that completely obscures the subtle, global symmetry signatures required by clustering algorithms.The Universality of Higher ZerosAs the analysis moves higher up the critical line, transitioning from the first zero to the higher, intermediate zeros, the influence of the specific arithmetic of the family systematically decays. The intermediate and high zeros of an individual L-function, or a family of L-functions, begin to exhibit a universal statistical behavior that perfectly aligns with the bulk statistics of the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE), regardless of the family's orthogonal or symplectic origins at the central point.This phenomenon is mathematically codified by the moment theory of Satake parameters. In the theoretical calculation of the n-level density of zeros, the main mathematical terms—which dictate the universal, geometric behavior of the distribution—are entirely independent of the arithmetic of the family. The arithmetic elements, which are derived from the higher moments of the Satake parameters, manifest strictly as lower-order terms.Much like the Central Limit Theorem where higher moments only affect the rate of convergence to the universal Gaussian limit, the arithmetic lower-order terms in L-functions only dictate the rate of convergence to the universal RMT limit. Because these lower-order arithmetic terms exert their maximum perturbative influence at the origin (the first zero) and decay as one moves up the spectrum, the intermediate zeros represent an optimal diagnostic window.In this intermediate bulk zone, the volatile, high-variance arithmetic noise of the first zero has dissipated.
