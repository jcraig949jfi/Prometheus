Cross-Family Zero Distributions and Metric Spaces of L-functions: A Geometric and Analytic SynthesisIntroduction to the Topography of L-functionsThe integration of diverse arithmetic and geometric objects into a unified, computationally tractable search space represents a formidable frontier in modern analytic number theory. At the heart of this endeavor is the $L$-function, an analytic continuation of Dirichlet series that encodes the deepest arithmetic properties of the object from which it originates. Historically, the study of $L$-functions has been partitioned into distinct silos: the Riemann zeta function, Dirichlet $L$-functions associated with characters, Hasse-Weil $L$-functions of elliptic curves and algebraic varieties, and Artin $L$-functions attached to Galois representations. Constructing a unified $k$-nearest neighbors ($k$-NN) search space in which elliptic curves, modular forms, and genus-2 curves coexist as parameterized coordinates fundamentally alters the approach to these objects. It transitions the study of $L$-functions from isolated algebraic analysis into the realm of high-dimensional geometric embedding and spatial topology.However, mapping these objects based on the similarity of their zero vectors—specifically, the imaginary ordinates of the non-trivial zeros lying on the critical line $\Re(s) = 1/2$—introduces a profound theoretical vulnerability. This concern is anchored in the foundational predictions of Random Matrix Theory (RMT) and the Katz-Sarnak philosophy. Different types of arithmetic objects belong to distinct statistical ensembles whose low-lying zeros are governed by different classical compact groups. Therefore, the distance between the zero vector of an elliptic curve and the zero vector of a symmetric-square $L$-function is intrinsically convoluted by the local repulsion characteristics of their respective symmetry types. Raw Euclidean distances in this cross-family context will inadvertently measure the divergence of random matrix ensembles rather than any latent arithmetic or geometric isomorphism.This comprehensive report examines the feasibility, mathematical foundations, and existing literature surrounding cross-family zero comparisons. It addresses the specific challenges of formally defining symmetry-normalized distance metrics, the utilization of the Selberg class axioms to enforce degree and conductor normalizations within a metric space, and the explicit object-to-object comparison of zero distributions. Furthermore, the analysis investigates how topological singularities such as arithmetic equivalence disrupt zero-vector similarity and evaluates the conceptualization of zero-space embeddings within the wider analytic number theory landscape. To construct a metric that authentically captures structural relationships between different arithmetic families, the underlying scaling laws of these ensembles must be mathematically neutralized.The Katz-Sarnak Philosophy and Level DensitiesThe Katz-Sarnak predictive framework demonstrates that the statistical behavior of the zeros of an $L$-function near the central point $s = 1/2$, in the limit as the analytic conductor tends to infinity, exactly matches the scaling limit of the eigenvalues of random matrices drawn from specific classical compact groups. These scaling limits are deeply sensitive to the arithmetic nature of the family in question. In this framework, the symmetry types observed are primarily Orthogonal (O), Symplectic (Sp), and Unitary (U). For instance, the family of $L$-functions associated with elliptic curves over the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ (and more generally, weight $k$ holomorphic modular forms) typically exhibits orthogonal symmetry. In these orthogonal families, the 1-level density of zeros reveals a distinct behavior near the central point, often with a peak or a specified structural density depending on the evenness or oddness of the functional equation. Conversely, families such as the real quadratic Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s, \chi_d)$ or symmetric-square $L$-functions $L(s, \text{sym}^2 f)$ fall under the symplectic (Sp) symmetry class.
