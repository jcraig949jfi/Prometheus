The Arithmetic Signatures of Higher L-Function Zeros: Spectral Tails, Murmurations, and Information ContentIntroduction: The Spectral Tail Anomaly in Arithmetic ClusteringIn the extensive study of elliptic curves over the rational numbers, the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer (BSD) conjecture posits a profound and enduring connection between the analytic behavior of an elliptic curve's L-function, $L(E, s)$, at the central critical point $s = 1/2$ (or $s=1$ depending on the normalization prior to shifting), and the algebraic rank of the curve's Mordell-Weil group. Consequently, decades of mathematical research have concentrated almost exclusively on the central vanishing region and the behavior of the lowest-lying zeros. The Katz-Sarnak density conjecture formalized this localized focus, demonstrating that the statistical distribution of low-lying zeros, when scaled by the logarithm of the analytic conductor, mirrors the eigenvalue distributions of classical compact groups near the origin. Because the rank of the elliptic curve dictates the multiplicity of the central zero, the lowest-lying zeros ($\gamma_1$ through $\gamma_4$) have historically been viewed as the primary analytical arbiters of the curve's global arithmetic properties.However, recent empirical machine learning investigations have revealed a persistent structural anomaly that challenges this origin-centric paradigm. When clustering large datasets of elliptic curves to predict rank parity via unsupervised learning, utilizing the "spectral tail" of the L-function zeros—specifically, the imaginary parts of the zeros in positions 5 through 19 ($\gamma_5$ to $\gamma_{19}$)—yields a stronger rank-correlated clustering metric than utilizing the complete set of the first 20 zeros. In these experiments, isolating the spectral tail produced an Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) of 0.5548, whereas incorporating the central vanishing region and the lowest zeros degraded the ARI to 0.5456. This counterintuitive finding indicates that the inclusion of the lowest-lying zeros introduces geometric instability or confounding variance that obscures the pure rank signal in unsupervised clustering spaces.This exhaustive research report investigates the theoretical, statistical, and information-theoretic mechanisms underlying this phenomenon. By synthesizing recent breakthroughs in murmuration theory , explicit formulas connecting zeros to primes , random matrix theory (RMT) involving lower-order correction terms , and the information-theoretic interpretations of zero distributions , this analysis maps precisely what the $n$-level densities of higher zeros encode about arithmetic families. It demonstrates that while the primary symmetry type of the family is heavily anchored near the origin, the spectral tail acts as a mathematically stable repository of rank parity and arithmetic structure. This upper sequence of zeros is decoupled from the localized statistical volatility induced by confounding arithmetic invariants, such as the Tate-Shafarevich group ($\Sha$), allowing it to serve as an optimized, high-fidelity feature space for algorithmic classification.1. The Katz-Sarnak Density Conjecture and the Localization of Symmetry1.1 The 1-Level Density and Compact Group TheoryThe foundational framework for understanding the statistics of L-function zeros stems from the Katz-Sarnak density conjecture. This hypothesis posits that the statistical behavior of zeros near the central point of any natural family of L-functions, as the analytic conductor $c_f$ approaches infinity, is well-modeled by the behavior of eigenvalues near 1 of a specific classical compact group. Depending on the arithmetic nature of the family, this governing group is either the unitary group $U(N)$, the symplectic group $USp(2N)$, or one of the orthogonal groups $O(N)$, $SO(\text{even})$, or $SO(\text{odd})$. For families of L-functions attached to holomorphic cuspidal newforms and elliptic curves, the fundamental symmetry type has been rigorously demonstrated to be orthogonal.The seminal work of Iwaniec, Luo, and Sarnak (2000) established the 1-level density for holomorphic even-weight cuspidal newforms of square-free level, proving that these families exhibit $SO(\text{even})$ and $SO(\text{odd})$ symmetries when split by the sign of their functional equations. The primary analytic tool utilized to measure these distributions is the 1-level density. For a suitably chosen test function $\Phi$ whose Fourier transform $\hat{\Phi}$ is compactly supported within a restricted bandwidth, the 1-level density for a form $f$ is defined as the sum over its non-trivial zeros $\rho_j = 1/2 + i\gamma_j$:$$\mathcal{D}_1(f; \Phi) = \sum_{j} \Phi \left( \gamma_j \frac{\log c_f}{2\pi} \right)$$The Katz-Sarnak philosophy dictates that as the analytic conductors grow, the expected value of this sum over the family converges to an integral $\int \Phi(x) W(G)(x) dx$, where $W(G)(x)$ represents the eigenvalue density distribution of the corresponding symmetry group $G$. The function $W(G)(x)$ possesses distinct boundary behaviors near $x=0$ that differentiate the orthogonal, symplectic, and unitary ensembles, thereby allowing number theorists to identify the symmetry type of the arithmetic family by analyzing the lowest-lying zeros.1.2 The Erasure of Symmetry at Higher ZerosA critical question immediately arises in the context of the clustering anomaly: does the 1-level density evaluated at higher zeros—away from the central point—continue to carry family-distinguishing symmetry information, or does this signal concentrate entirely near the origin? The theoretical consensus, supported by the analytical properties of the 1-level density and the work of Rudnick and Sarnak (1996), confirms that the symmetry-type signal concentrates heavily near the central point and washes out at higher indices.This concentration is mechanically enforced by two distinct mathematical realities. First, the construction of the test function $\Phi$ actively suppresses the contribution of higher zeros. To satisfy the convergence criteria of the explicit formula and trace formulas used in these proofs, $\Phi(x)$ must be an even Schwartz function of rapid decay, meaning it decays super-polynomially as $|x| \to \infty$. In the scaling factor $\gamma_j \frac{\log c_f}{2\pi}$, as the zero index $j$ increases, the imaginary part $\gamma_j$ grows. Consequently, the test function evaluates to an infinitesimally small number for higher zeros, rendering their contribution to the 1-level density mathematically negligible in the asymptotic limit.Second, from a pure Random Matrix Theory (RMT) perspective, the distinguishing features of the classical compact groups are strictly boundary effects localized near the origin. As one traverses further into the eigenvalue spectrum—analogous to moving to higher zeros $\gamma_n$ for large $n$—the local density of states asymptotically approaches the universal bulk distribution. For the unscaled spacings of all these groups, the bulk distribution is governed by the sine kernel, and the macroscopic density of states is governed by the Wigner semicircle law. At these higher spectral reaches, the densities $W(SO)(x)$, $W(USp)(x)$, and $W(U)(x)$ become analytically indistinguishable from one another. The $n$-level density measures the spatial correlations between $n$-tuples of zeros, capturing the intricate repulsive and attractive forces that govern the geometry of the critical line. The $n$-level density is defined by summing a multidimensional test function over distinct zero indices:$$\mathcal{D}_n(f; \Phi) = \sum_{j_1, \dots, j_n \text{ distinct}} \Phi \left( \gamma_{j_1} \frac{\log c_f}{2\pi}, \dots, \gamma_{j_n} \frac{\log c_f}{2\pi} \right)$$Computing the $n$-level density for $n > 2$ mathematically requires advanced knowledge of the distribution of the signs of the functional equations within the specific family of L-functions. For many years, this posed an insurmountable theoretical barrier, limiting rigorous number-theoretic results to the 1-level and 2-level densities. However, the introduction of the Ratios Conjecture by Conrey, Farmer, Keating, Rubinstein, and Snaith revolutionized this field.As detailed in the literature concerning the $n$-level density of L-function zeros for orthogonal and symplectic symmetries, the Ratios Conjecture permits the calculation of these multidimensional densities by utilizing averages of ratios of characteristic polynomials. Crucially, the Ratios Conjecture formulation does not simply yield the asymptotic random matrix limit; it produces explicit conjectures that include all the lower-order correction terms that differentiate finite number-theoretic data from pure, infinite-dimensional random matrices. These lower-order terms are the mathematical reservoirs of the arithmetic data for finite conductors.2.2 Rank Repulsion and Tighter Density PackingDo the 2-level or 3-level densities of zeros in the range $\gamma_5$ to $\gamma_{20}$ differ between rank 0 and rank 1 families? Theoretical predictions and empirical models strongly confirm that they do, and this difference is driven by the phenomenon of zero repulsion.The defining characteristic of higher rank elliptic curves is the presence of forced zeros at the central point $s=1/2$. The existence of these central zeros induces a strong "repulsion" effect on the adjacent non-trivial zeros. In rank 1 families, the first non-trivial zero $\gamma_1$ is repelled further up the imaginary axis compared to its expected baseline position in a rank 0 family. Miller (2004) observed this phenomenon extensively when calculating the one- and two-level densities for rational families of elliptic curves, noting that the presence of rank alters the support and behavior of the lowest zeros compared to the theoretical orthogonal limits.This repulsion is not isolated to $\gamma_1$; it propagates up the critical line, analogous to an electrostatic chain reaction. Consequently, while $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ are displaced significantly upward, the subsequent zeros—specifically those in our spectral tail from $\gamma_5$ to $\gamma_{20}$—are forced to "pack more tightly". The Weil Explicit Formula and the Weighting of Higher Zeros3.1 The Analytical Framework of the Explicit FormulaTo fully comprehend why higher zeros encode specific arithmetic information, one must examine the Riesz-Weil explicit formula. This theorem constitutes the profound mathematical bridge linking the analytic distribution of L-function zeros to the arithmetic distribution of primes and, by extension, the Frobenius traces of elliptic curves. In its most general form, the explicit formula relates the sum of a carefully chosen test function $\Phi$ evaluated over prime powers to the sum of its Mellin transform $F(s)$ evaluated over the non-trivial zeros $\rho = 1/2 + i\gamma$.For an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, the explicit formula tightly constrains the relationship between the sequence of Frobenius traces $a_p = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ at primes of good reduction and the curve's zero spectrum:$$\sum_{p \le X} a_p \log p \cdot p^{-1/2} \approx -\sum_{\rho} \frac{X^{\rho - 1/2}}{\rho - 1/2}$$In this identity, the sum over the zeros generates the oscillatory terms that perfectly reconstruct the fluctuations of the prime trace distributions.3.2 Dual Decay and the Suppression of Higher ZerosDoes the explicit formula's weighting predict that higher zeros encode different arithmetic information? Yes, it fundamentally guarantees it. The contribution of a zero $\rho = 1/2 + i\gamma$ to the prime sum is weighted inversely by its height on the critical line, owing to the complex denominator $\rho - 1/2$ and the decay characteristics of the chosen test functions.In modern computational number theory, evaluating the explicit formula requires test functions like the Truncated Gaussian (TG) kernel, which are specifically engineered with "dual decay" properties. Because the kernel is essentially a Gaussian, its Mellin transform $F(s)$ decays super-polynomially along the imaginary directions. This specific mathematical property ensures that the individual contributions from zeros at large imaginary heights (the higher zeros) decay incredibly rapidly. Consequently, only the first several non-trivial zeros contribute significantly to the macroscopic amplitude of the prime-counting calculation, while the numerical contributions from higher zeros become exponentially small.However, this mathematical suppression highlights a profound duality: while higher zeros are rendered mathematically negligible for bulk approximations of the amplitude, they uniquely hold the fine-structure arithmetic data. The lowest zero $\gamma_1$ dictates the macroscopic "mean shift" and the primary wave amplitude of the prime oscillations. In contrast, the zeros in the spectral tail ($\gamma_5-\gamma_{20}$) must account for the high-frequency residual amplitudes and the exact structural permutations of the curve's secondary invariants. The weighting predicts that higher zeros act as phase-correction parameters, adjusting the broad strokes of $\gamma_1$ to perfectly match the precise arithmetic reality of the specific curve.3.3 The Murmuration Phenomenon and $\Sha$-Modulated Zero DisplacementThe intricate interaction between higher zeros and the explicit formula has recently been thrust to the forefront of arithmetic geometry by the discovery of "murmurations". In 2022, He, Lee, Oliver, and Pozdnyakov discovered that when averaging the normalized Frobenius traces $a_p / \sqrt{p}$ over elliptic curves in sliding conductor windows, striking oscillatory patterns emerge that depend distinctly on the rank of the curves. Curves of rank 0 and rank 1 oscillate in perfect anti-phase.Subsequent theoretical breakthroughs by Cowan (2023) and Zubrilina (2023) proved that these murmurations are directly mediated by the quasi-periodic distribution of the L-function zeros. By applying the explicit formula, Zubrilina demonstrated that a displacement in the zero distribution maps directly to a phase shift in the murmuration wave.Crucially, Wachs (2026) demonstrated that this murmuration is not uniformly dictated by rank alone. While the global BSD invariants themselves (such as the real period, Tamagawa product, and torsion order) do not exhibit murmurations when averaged, they systematically modulate the shape of the trace murmurations within a fixed rank. Stratifying curves by the analytic order of the Tate-Shafarevich group ($\Sha$) yields significantly different murmuration profiles.Through extensive zero distribution analysis, Wachs revealed that curves with $|\Sha| \ge 4$ possess systematically different low-lying zeros. Specifically, the first zero $\gamma_1$ is displaced noticeably higher, while the subsequent zeros—our spectral tail—are forced to pack more tightly to compensate. The explicit formula mathematically binds this tight packing of zeros 5-20 to the altered murmuration profile observed in the traces. Because the spectral tail reflects the $\Sha$-induced geometric compression as a stable, distributed density shift rather than the volatile, single-point extreme variance seen at $\gamma_1$, clustering algorithms utilizing $\gamma_5-\gamma_{19}$ successfully capture the modulated geometric state. This allows the algorithm to optimize rank isolation without being confused by the large amplitude swings of $\gamma_1$ caused by varying $\Sha$ sizes.4. Transition to Universality: Arithmetic vs. Universal Regimes4.1 The GUE Hypothesis and the Limits of UniversalityTo fully contextualize the arithmetic value of zeros 5 through 20, one must understand the boundary where arithmetic ends and universal randomness begins. The Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE) hypothesis, famously sparked by the serendipitous interaction between Hugh Montgomery and Freeman Dyson, dictates that the statistical distribution of the spacings between high-lying zeros of the Riemann zeta function universally aligns with the spacing distribution of eigenvalues in random Hermitian matrices.The Katz-Sarnak philosophy successfully expanded this random matrix theory correspondence to families of L-functions, establishing the orthogonal and symplectic analogues. However, a critical distinction exists between the asymptotic limit assumed in these theorems and the finite, observable computational realm of elliptic curves. At what height does "universal" behavior kick in, and are zeros 5-20 for conductors $N \le 5000$ still in the arithmetic regime?Odlyzko's monumental computational verification of the GUE hypothesis relied on analyzing the nearest-neighbor spacings among 70 million zeros beginning at the $10^{20}$-th zero. At such staggering heights up the critical line, the arithmetic specificities of the underlying function dissolve entirely, overwhelmed by the universal RMT mechanics. The local spacing distributions become mathematically indistinguishable from the bulk sine-kernel determinantal forms of GUE. In this deep universal regime, individual zeros contain absolutely no information about the conductor, rank, or specific arithmetic coefficients of the origin family.4.2 The Finite Conductor and the Arithmetic RegimeFor rational elliptic curves with conductors $N \le 5000$, examining the zeros $\gamma_5$ through $\gamma_{20}$ places the analysis firmly within the arithmetic regime. At these relatively low indices, the transition to pure universality has not yet occurred, and the zeros remain highly structured algebraic signatures.The analytical reason for this lies in the mechanics of the conductor scaling limit. The normalized spacings $\delta_n$ used to compare L-function zeros to random matrix eigenvalues depend strictly on the density scaling factor $\frac{\log c_f}{2\pi}$. For elliptic curves where $N \le 5000$, the logarithm of the analytic conductor $c_f$ remains quite small. This implies that the "zoomed-in" scale required to observe pure universal random matrix spacing has not yet asymptotically detached from the lower-order Euler product corrections.In this finite conductor regime, the distribution of zeros is governed heavily by the exact prime factors of the conductor (bad reduction primes) and the specific Dirichlet coefficients (the Frobenius traces) associated with the L-function's Euler product. The Ratios Conjecture formulation of the $n$-level density explicitly requires these lower-order arithmetic terms to correct the GUE or orthogonal bulk prediction so that it matches actual finite number-theoretic data.Therefore, zeros 5-20 for conductors $\le 5000$ are not random noise; they are deeply structured mathematical objects. They exist in a unique transitional "Goldilocks" window: they are high enough up the critical line to escape the localized boundary effects and repulsion volatility of the central point's symmetry restriction , yet they are low enough to remain deeply embedded in the arithmetic regime, immune to Odlyzko's universal washout. This precise positioning allows the 5-19 spectral tail to serve as a high-fidelity proxy for arithmetic invariants like rank parity.5. Information Content, Entropy, and Mutual Information of Individual Zeros ($\gamma_n$)5.1 Shannon Entropy and the Quantification of Mathematical UncertaintyTo mathematically formalize exactly why zeros 5-19 provide superior clustering accuracy, one must invoke Information Theory. Are there theoretical frameworks that address whether the $n$-th zero carries $X$ bits of information about an invariant $Y$?Information theory, built upon Claude Shannon's foundational formulation of entropy, quantifies the uncertainty inherent in a random variable. For a discrete arithmetic invariant like rank parity ($R \in \{0, 1\}$), assuming a roughly equal prior distribution of rank 0 and rank 1 curves in a given dataset, the Shannon entropy is calculated as:$$H(R) = -\sum_{r \in \{0, 1\}} P(R=r) \log_2 P(R=r) \approx 1 \text{ bit}$$This equation dictates that identifying the rank parity of an unknown curve requires the acquisition of exactly 1 bit of information.If we view the distribution of an individual zero $\gamma_n$ across a large family of L-functions as a continuous random variable, we can calculate its differential entropy. More importantly, the exact amount of information that a specific zero $\gamma_n$ provides about the rank parity $R$ is formalized by the Mutual Information (MI), denoted as $I(R; \gamma_n)$ :$$I(R; \gamma_n) = H(R) - H(R | \gamma_n)$$where $H(R | \gamma_n)$ is the conditional entropy of the rank given exact knowledge of the position of the $n$-th zero. If knowing $\gamma_n$ perfectly predicts the rank, the conditional entropy drops to zero, and the mutual information is 1 bit. If $\gamma_n$ provides no predictive value, the mutual information is 0 bits.5.2 The Mutual Information Profile of the Spectral TailWhile number theory papers do not traditionally title themselves "the $n$-th zero carries $X$ bits of information," the modern application of information sensitivity functions and mutual information to continuous structural spaces directly assesses this dynamic.
