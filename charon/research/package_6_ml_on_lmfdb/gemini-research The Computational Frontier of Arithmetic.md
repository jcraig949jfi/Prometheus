The Computational Frontier of Arithmetic Geometry: A Comprehensive Survey of Machine Learning on LMFDB Data (2022–2026)1. Introduction: The Intersection of Data Science and Arithmetic GeometryThe application of machine learning, data science, and advanced computational search methods to pure mathematics has precipitated a fundamental paradigm shift in how researchers formulate conjectures, analyze high-dimensional arithmetic structures, and understand the deep statistical properties of geometric objects. At the epicenter of this movement in modern number theory is the L-functions and Modular Forms Database (LMFDB), an exhaustive, searchable online compendium of mathematical objects and their meticulously computed invariants. Originally constructed to catalog the outputs of rigorous, deterministic algorithmic computations, the LMFDB has increasingly evolved to serve a secondary, revolutionary purpose: acting as the foundational training dataset for complex statistical, heuristic, and deep learning models.This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level survey of the complete landscape of machine learning applied to LMFDB data from 2020 through the first quarter of 2026. The explicit objective of this analysis is to map the entire perimeter of adjacent work to establish a clear demarcation of novelty for future research initiatives. While the initial discovery of "murmurations"—scale-invariant oscillatory patterns in the average values of Frobenius traces—by He, Lee, Oliver, and Pozdnyakov catalyzed widespread interest across the mathematical community , the ecosystem of machine learning applications on the LMFDB has rapidly and aggressively diversified. Researchers are now deploying one-dimensional convolutional neural networks (CNNs), principal component analysis (PCA), random forests, and transformer architectures to predict elliptic curve ranks, the vanishing orders of L-functions, Fricke signs of Maass forms, and the Galois groups of high-degree number fields.To provide a complete picture of the current research landscape, this report systematically addresses the specific methodologies, data representations, and collaborative networks defining this space. Furthermore, the analysis will identify and explicitly articulate critical voids in the existing literature—most notably, the untapped potential of graph neural networks (GNNs) operating on the complex isogeny graphs intrinsic to the LMFDB, and the distinct absence of formalized intrinsic arithmetic distance metrics for geometric clustering. By juxtaposing the probabilistic nature of these machine learning heuristics against the highly rigorous deterministic computational methodologies presented in the LuCaNT (LMFDB, Computation, and Number Theory) conference series, this report outlines the theoretical boundaries and the future trajectory of AI-assisted arithmetic geometry.2. The Murmuration Paradigm and AI-Assisted Mathematical Discovery (2026 Perspective)Before surveying the chronological expansion of machine learning literature, it is necessary to establish the theoretical and philosophical framework that currently governs the intersection of artificial intelligence and number theory. The 2026 preprint "Murmurations: A Case Study in AI-Assisted Mathematics" (arXiv:2603.09680) by Yang-Hui He, Kyu-Hwan Lee, Thomas Oliver, and Alexey Pozdnyakov serves as the definitive manifesto for this new paradigm.The murmuration phenomenon refers to the emergence of striking, scale-invariant oscillatory patterns that appear when averaging suitably normalized arithmetic data—such as the correlation of Frobenius traces of elliptic curves or Hecke eigenvalues at primes—over large families ordered by conductor. Initially discovered computationally by applying machine learning algorithms to datasets extracted from the LMFDB, murmurations constitute a genuinely new and intriguing phenomenon in arithmetic statistics. The underlying arithmetic mechanisms connect deeply to central themes surrounding the conjecture of Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer (BSD) and perspectives derived from random matrix theory.The 2026 case study emphasizes that the discovery of murmurations illustrates a fundamentally non-linear and less predictable interaction between artificial intelligence and pure mathematics than is typically portrayed in mainstream computer science literature. Unlike theorem-proving environments where artificial intelligence proposes a logical step that can be immediately verified or refuted by a formal system, the patterns spotted by machine learning in the LMFDB required deep, human-driven mathematical interpretation to transform a statistical anomaly into a precise mathematical framework. The authors highlight the critical role of interpretability tools in this discovery pipeline. By employing principal component weightings, saliency curves, and convolutional filters on the trained models, researchers were able to "open the black box" of the neural networks to understand precisely which prime indices were driving the predictive accuracy of the models.This interplay reflects a broader lesson for the discipline: while artificial intelligence and deep learning architectures excel at large-scale pattern recognition within massive repositories like the LMFDB, the formulation of meaningful mathematical questions and the rigorous interpretation of the algorithmic outputs remain deeply dependent on human arithmetic expertise. The murmuration case study effectively demonstrates how classical number theory, arithmetic statistics, and interpretable machine learning can symbiotically interact to produce genuinely new mathematical theory, setting the methodological standard for all subsequent research in this domain.3. A Comprehensive Taxonomy of Machine Learning on LMFDB Data (2020–2026)To fully contextualize the current state of research and establish parameters for novelty, it is imperative to construct a complete taxonomy of all machine learning papers utilizing the LMFDB as a primary data source from 2020 through early 2026. The literature reveals a rapid, progressive evolution from simple supervised classifiers applied to isolated arithmetic invariants to highly complex deep learning and unsupervised architectures operating across heterogeneous arithmetic families.The integration of machine learning with LMFDB data began in earnest with attempts to classify arithmetic curves by their Sato-Tate groups. In late 2020, He, Lee, and Oliver published foundational work demonstrating that Bayesian classifiers could distinguish between Sato-Tate groups using only a small number of Euler factors extracted from the associated L-functions. This research showed that with impressive accuracy—achieving between 99 percent and 100 percent precision—a machine learning model could separate generic elliptic curves, characterized by the Sato-Tate group $\text{SU}(2)$, from those possessing complex multiplication. Extending this logic to genus 2 curves, the researchers successfully utilized principal component analysis to separate the generic Sato-Tate group $\text{USp}(4)$ from various non-generic groups, proving that statistical models could learn underlying Sato-Tate distributions highly efficiently.Building upon this success, the same collaborative team expanded their focus to number fields in 2022. By utilizing logistic regression and random forest classifiers, they demonstrated the capacity to predict fundamental invariants such as degree, signature, Galois group, and class number. The conjecture of Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer quickly became a primary target for data-driven analysis. Alessandretti, Baronchelli, and He conducted extensive empirical analyses on over 2.5 million elliptic curves from the Cremona database and the LMFDB. Utilizing gradient-boosted trees and topological data analysis, they investigated the statistical properties of rank, Weierstrass coefficients, Tamagawa numbers, and the BSD ratio, revealing deep underlying patterns in the high-dimensional point clouds generated by these invariants.The methodological sophistication of the field escalated significantly in 2022 with the introduction of deep neural networks. Kazalicki and Vlah applied one-dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to the problem of predicting the analytic rank of elliptic curves. Their data representation utilized the conductor of the curve alongside a truncated sequence of normalized Fourier coefficients for primes of good reduction. The CNNs were trained on data sourced from the LMFDB as well as a custom dataset of curves with conductors up to $10^{30}$. The results were revelatory: the convolutional neural networks vastly outperformed classical Mestre-Nagao sums on the LMFDB dataset, demonstrating that neural architectures could synthesize complex, non-linear heuristic rank predictors far more effectively than isolated, human-derived analytical sums.By late 2024 and early 2025, the application of machine learning on the LMFDB expanded across a diverse array of arithmetic objects. Babei, Banwait, and collaborators trained feed-forward neural network classifiers on subsets of invariants arising from the BSD conjectural formula to predict the order of the Shafarevich-Tate group of an elliptic curve. The Shafarevich-Tate group is notoriously difficult to compute rigorously, often requiring exhaustive descent methods; however, the neural network classifiers achieved accuracies exceeding 0.9. Furthermore, the team developed a regression model capable of predicting orders of this group not seen during the training phase, applying it to high-rank curves such as the rank 29 curve discovered by Elkies and Klagsbrun.Simultaneously, machine learning techniques were applied to the class numbers of real quadratic fields by Amir, He, Lee, Oliver, and Sultanow , while Lee and Lee utilized decision trees and logistic regression on Dedekind zeta coefficients to successfully classify the Galois groups of number fields of degrees up to 10. Deep Dive: Machine Learning the Vanishing Order of Rational L-FunctionsA highly significant development in the application of data science to the LMFDB is the February 2025 preprint titled "Machine learning the vanishing order of rational L-functions" (arXiv:2502.10360). This comprehensive body of work represents a distinct maturation of the field, moving away from homogeneous datasets—such as isolating only elliptic curves—toward the holistic analysis of large, heterogeneous datasets comprising L-functions with highly diverse geometric and arithmetic origins.The research was conducted by a massive collaborative team whose efforts stem largely from the fall 2024 program on Mathematics and Machine Learning hosted at the Harvard Center of Mathematical Sciences and Applications. The authors of this pivotal paper are Joanna Bieri, Giorgi Butbaia, Edgar Costa, Alyson Deines, Kyu-Hwan Lee, David Lowry-Duda, Thomas Oliver, Yidi Qi, and Tamara Veenstra.The methodological framework deployed by the research team involved a multi-tiered approach that seamlessly integrated both unsupervised and supervised learning paradigms. As an unsupervised exploratory technique, Principal Component Analysis was utilized to observe spatial clustering within the high-dimensional data. The researchers discovered that PCA successfully and autonomously clusters rational L-functions according to their vanishing order without requiring any prior labeling from the algorithms. To formalize this predictive capability, the team then employed Linear Discriminant Analysis as a baseline supervised classification technique to draw definitive linear decision boundaries between L-functions possessing different vanishing orders. Finally, advanced feed-forward neural networks were utilized to maximize predictive accuracy based on the input representations. The results confirmed that both LDA and neural networks can accurately predict the vanishing quantity of these functions.The fundamental challenge in applying machine learning to arithmetic geometry lies in the mathematical representation of the data. In this study, each rational L-function was represented by a finite sequence of its Dirichlet coefficients. Because L-functions arise from incredibly diverse contexts—including Artin representations, arithmetic curves such as elliptic curves and genus 2 curves defined over number fields, and various modular forms such as classical, Hilbert, and Bianchi modular forms—the normalization of these coefficients was highly dependent on the specific arithmetic context.To standardize the inputs, the authors utilized normalized coefficients, denoted as $\tilde{a}_p$. In sub-datasets where the root number $w$ equals $1$, this representation simplified to $\tilde{a}_p = a_p$. By plotting the average values of these normalized coefficients across specific sub-datasets, such as elliptic curves over number fields and genus 2 curves over the rationals, the authors observed distinct murmuration-like oscillatory patterns that separated visually based strictly on the vanishing order. The input feature space for the neural networks was effectively a truncated vector of these coefficients, providing empirical evidence that the vanishing order at the central point—which is conjecturally linked to the deep arithmetic described by the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer and Beilinson–Bloch–Kato conjectures—leaves a detectable statistical footprint in the early Dirichlet coefficients.While previous localized studies had achieved exceptionally high accuracies on isolated datasets, the true novelty of this 2025 research lies in its expansive scope. The models demonstrated the robust capacity to accurately predict the vanishing quantity across a massive, heterogeneous dataset. Furthermore, the authors explicitly explored the concept of transfer learning within pure mathematics. By training a machine learning model on one specific sub-dataset, such as classical modular forms, and evaluating its predictive accuracy on a completely different sub-dataset, such as Hilbert modular forms, the researchers successfully probed the universal nature of the arithmetic structures that govern vanishing orders across different domains of algebraic geometry.5. Unsupervised Learning and Visualization: Thomas Oliver’s ICMS 2024 ContributionsThe transition from supervised predictive modeling to unsupervised structural discovery in arithmetic geometry was formally articulated at the International Congress on Mathematical Software (ICMS) in 2024. During the session dedicated to machine learning within computer algebra systems, Thomas Oliver presented a pivotal talk titled "Machine Learning for Number Theory: Unsupervised Learning with L-Functions".Oliver's presentation outlined a research programme that began in 2020 and culminated in the discovery of murmurations, emphasizing the adoption of a "data scientific perspective on old problems". The unsupervised component of this extensive research relied predominantly on Principal Component Analysis. The features fed into the PCA algorithms were massive vectors consisting of Euler factors and Dirichlet coefficients meticulously extracted from the LMFDB.By projecting these high-dimensional arrays of Dirichlet coefficients into lower-dimensional principal component spaces, Oliver and his collaborators observed that the arithmetic data naturally bifurcated along deep invariant lines. As demonstrated in their work on genus 2 curves, the PCA algorithms autonomously separated the generic Sato-Tate group $\text{USp}(4)$ from non-generic groups without any prior algorithmic labeling or human intervention. This powerful result proved that the underlying arithmetic geometry dictates the variance in the coefficient sequences strongly enough to be detected and categorized by standard variance-maximizing linear transformations.Regarding the inquiry into a "searchable component" of Oliver's ICMS 2024 work, an analysis of the presentation and the associated literature clarifies that the primary searchable infrastructure remains the LMFDB itself. The LMFDB, which went live in the mid-2010s, serves as the "huge arithmetic online database" providing the robust querying backend required for massive dataset generation. Oliver's work did not introduce a standalone, novel search engine; rather, it demonstrated how modern data scientists interface with the LMFDB programmatically to extract large tensors of arithmetic data for downstream machine learning pipelines.In terms of visualization, the unsupervised learning approach relied heavily on plotting principal component weightings and saliency curves to make the neural network's internal processing human-interpretable. Furthermore, Oliver's bibliography highlights the integration of advanced visualization techniques such as UpSet plots, developed by Lex et al. in 2014, to visualize complex intersecting sets of arithmetic invariants. This indicates a strong, deliberate emphasis on visual data analytics and interpretability alongside standard dimensional reduction graphs to foster human-AI mathematical discovery.6. The Evolution of the "Machine Learning Meets Number Theory" EcosystemThe phrase "Machine Learning meets Number Theory" originated as the title of a seminal 2019 preprint, which was subsequently published in 2023, by Laura Alessandretti, Andrea Baronchelli, and Yang-Hui He, titled "Machine Learning Meets Number Theory: The Data Science of Birch-Swinnerton-Dyer". This paper established the foundational framework for treating highly curated databases like the LMFDB not merely as lookup tables, but as high-dimensional point clouds uniquely suited for advanced data science.The research initiated by this movement resulted in a highly significant proceedings volume titled "Machine Learning in Pure Mathematics and Theoretical Physics," published by World Scientific in 2023. These proceedings serve as the bedrock text for the discipline and contain several key chapters that outline the initial scope of the field. Beyond the titular chapter by Alessandretti, Baronchelli, and He, the volume features "Machine Learning: The Dimension of a Polytope" by Coates, Hofscheier, and Kasprzyk, "Deep Learning: Complete Intersection Calabi–Yau Manifolds" by Erbin and Finotello, and "Deep-Learning the Landscape" by He.The momentum generated by these foundational publications spawned a robust series of dedicated workshops and academic programs between 2023 and 2026. A cornerstone of this ecosystem was the CMSA Mathematics and Machine Learning Program held at Harvard University during the 2024-2025 academic year. This long-running program heavily featured the analysis of LMFDB data, and the workshop topics covered a broad spectrum of advanced applications. Sessions focused intensely on the unsupervised learning of rational L-functions, deep investigations of murmurations and related oscillatory phenomena across different arithmetic families, and attempts to heuristically learn the class groups of number fields.A significant methodological evolution emerged during the 2024-2026 workshop sessions: a decisive transition from standard convolutional and feed-forward neural networks toward Large Language Models (LLMs) and transformer architectures. Workshops explicitly explored the use of transformers for processing integer sequences. This paradigm shift included the development of highly specialized tools, such as the int2int package developed by François Charton, which natively treats integer sequences as distinct mathematical tokens for sequence-to-sequence prediction models.Further evidence of this evolution was presented at the 2026 Joint Mathematics Meetings (JMM), where Geordie Williamson delivered talks on AI-assisted mathematical discovery. These sessions highlighted the application of AlphaEvolve and LLMs to combinatorial invariance and Bruhat graphs, expanding the perimeter of low-resource AI tools available to the pure mathematician. The workshop ecosystem also featured algorithmic bootcamps designed specifically to expose pure mathematicians to advanced symbolic regression algorithms like eureqa and AIFeynman. These tools are critical for the field, as they enable researchers to derive exact, human-readable mathematical formulas directly from the opaque output weights of trained neural networks, bridging the gap between statistical correlation and mathematical proof.8. Identifying the Methodological Void: Graph Neural Networks and Isogeny TopologiesOne of the primary strategic objectives of this comprehensive survey is to identify unoccupied vectors of adjacent work to firmly establish novelty for future research. A highly significant inquiry is whether Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) or advanced graph learning methodologies have been applied to the inherent, complex relationship structures natively cataloged within the LMFDB—specifically, isogeny graphs, modularity maps, and twist relationships.The LMFDB explicitly models and catalogs vast, complex graph structures that represent deep mathematical relationships. For instance, the database contains highly detailed "Isogeny graphs" and "Isogeny matrices" for elliptic curves, mapping the intricate networks of rational isogenies between curves within specific isogeny classes (e.g., classes 121.a and 175.b). Furthermore, extensive deterministic research by computational number theorists such as David Roe, Edgar Costa, and others has focused heavily on computing the isogeny classes of abelian surfaces and mapping the structural topological relationships of modular curves.Despite the profound presence of these rich topological structures within the database, an exhaustive review of the current literature reveals a striking methodological void. There are absolutely no documented instances in the 2020–2026 corpus of Graph Neural Networks or graph representation learning being directly applied to the LMFDB's isogeny, modularity, or twist graphs.While the surveyed literature frequently mentions Graph Neural Networks, their application is strictly confined to fundamentally different scientific domains. For example, GNNs are noted as highly powerful tools for accurately predicting materials and molecular properties in computational chemistry pipelines, and they have been utilized to effectively encode the geometric substructure of Complete Intersection Calabi-Yau (CICY) 3-folds in theoretical string theory physics.However, in the specific realm of arithmetic curves and L-functions sourced from the LMFDB, machine learning researchers have overwhelmingly, and exclusively, preferred sequence-based inputs. The models detailed throughout this report rely on one-dimensional CNNs operating on linear arrays of Fourier coefficients, or flat feature vectors fed into standard feed-forward networks, PCA algorithms, and decision trees. Therefore, the application of Graph Representation Learning, Node2Vec embeddings, or sophisticated Graph Neural Networks to the native isogeny graphs or modularity networks of the LMFDB represents a completely unoccupied niche. Exploiting this graph topology, rather than treating the data as isolated time-series sequences, constitutes a highly defensible and potent claim of absolute novelty for future research endeavors.9. The Theoretical Gap in Formalizing Arithmetic Similarity MetricsA theoretically profound area of inquiry closely related to the structural analysis of LMFDB data is the development of formalized "arithmetic similarity metrics" or intrinsic "L-function distance functions." Machine learning algorithms intrinsically rely on mathematical distance metrics—such as Euclidean distance, Manhattan distance, or cosine similarity—to perform spatial clustering in PCA and establish decision boundaries in classification algorithms like Support Vector Machines or Linear Discriminant Analysis.Currently, when machine learning models cluster rational L-functions, they achieve this by embedding the L-function into a standard Euclidean space defined strictly by its first $N$ Dirichlet coefficients ($a_1, a_2,..., a_N$). Consequently, the "distance" between two L-functions is implicitly, and somewhat crudely, defined simply as the Euclidean distance or cosine similarity between these truncated, real-number vectors.While data scientists utilize these array-based Euclidean metrics for neural networks, the pure mathematics literature does engage with analytical distance functions in other rigorous contexts. For example, in the study of Voronin's universality theorem, the topological distance between an L-function and a target analytic function is rigorously measured using the supremum norm evaluated over compact subsets of the critical strip. Furthermore, the dense literature surrounding the Selberg class discusses the fundamental difficulty of smoothly interpolating between L-functions. Because the Selberg class is not closed under addition, formulating a continuous metric space or a natural topology of arithmetic objects presents severe theoretical roadblocks. Other arithmetic distance concepts appear in peripheral contexts, such as the distance with respect to the Euclidean norm for well-rounded lattices defined over quadratic number fields , or specific distance functions defined on the vertices of expander graphs related to Galois representations.However, this comprehensive survey confirms a critical theoretical gap: no formal "L-function distance metric" that accurately captures deep arithmetic, motivic, or geometric similarity—beyond the raw, superficial comparison of Dirichlet coefficients—has been formalized specifically for use in machine learning objective functions. Developing a custom algorithmic loss function or a mathematically rigorous clustering metric that calculates spatial distance based on functional equation properties, conductor metrics, or motivic weight, rather than relying on generic spatial coefficient differences, would constitute highly novel, groundbreaking work at the frontier of arithmetic data science.10. Conclusion and Strategic Vectors for NoveltyThe application of machine learning, data science, and deep neural architectures to the L-functions and Modular Forms Database has evolved at a breathtaking pace. Transitioning from simple Bayesian classification tasks in 2020 to deep, structurally profound explorations of fundamental arithmetic statistics by 2026, the field has permanently altered the landscape of computational number theory. The computational discovery of murmurations definitively proved that neural networks, principal component analysis, and sophisticated interpretability tools could detect previously unseen, scale-invariant structures embedded within massive sequences of Fourier traces. Current state-of-the-art research, exemplified by the extensive collaborative networks of Costa, Oliver, Bieri, He, Lee, and others, successfully leverages convolutional neural networks, transformer models, and linear discriminant analysis operating on meticulously normalized sequences of Dirichlet coefficients. These models are now capable of predicting deep analytical invariants—such as the vanishing order of rational L-functions, the analytic ranks of elliptic curves, and the Fricke signs of non-holomorphic Maass forms—with astonishing accuracy, effectively bypassing decades of computationally prohibitive deterministic algorithms.For researchers seeking to establish claims of absolute novelty in this rapidly saturating space, this exhaustive survey identifies three highly strategic, completely unclaimed vectors of research:The Application of Graph Neural Networks on Arithmetic Topologies: While sequence-based machine learning operating on one-dimensional arrays of Dirichlet coefficients is nearing methodological saturation, the application of Graph Neural Networks to the rich, topological structures natively cataloged in the LMFDB is entirely absent from the current literature. Processing the LMFDB as a massive, interconnected topological network of isogeny graphs, torsion structures, and modularity maps, rather than treating the objects as isolated time-series sequences, represents a major, undefended opportunity for groundbreaking geometric deep learning.The Formalization of Intrinsic Arithmetic Distance Metrics: Current unsupervised learning models implicitly rely on standard Euclidean distances between truncated arrays of coefficients to perform spatial clustering. Developing, formalizing, and implementing a mathematically rigorous "L-function distance metric"—perhaps derived from Weil-Petersson metrics, Tannakian symbols, or fundamental differences in analytic conductor and motivic weight—would advance the field from utilizing generic data science algorithms into the realm of true, bespoke arithmetic machine learning.Heuristic-Guided Deterministic Computation: The rigid, historical separation between the rigorous computational methodologies documented in the LuCaNT proceedings and the purely heuristic models deployed by the machine learning community presents a prime opportunity for synthesis. Utilizing artificial intelligence not merely as a black-box predictor of a final invariant, but as an active, heuristic guide to heavily optimize the internal search logic, bound estimations, and branch pruning of rigorous deterministic algorithms would successfully bridge the two dominant paradigms of modern arithmetic geometry, creating a new standard for mathematical computation.
