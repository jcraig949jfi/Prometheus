# Council Review: Noesis Tensor Encoding Architecture

## Context

Noesis is a tensor-guided mathematical composition discovery engine — it searches concept space for compositional bridges across disparate mathematical and scientific fields. It uses DuckDB storage and tensor train compression (LANL THOR library). Its role in the larger system is foundational: it sits at the top of a self-reinforcing flywheel where discovered compositions become reasoning training examples for small language models (0.5B–1.5B parameters).

Noesis has confirmed 3.4x random performance in a clean factorial design, and building blocks have been shown to transfer and amplify discoveries by two orders of magnitude. However, we have identified a critical architectural concern with how mathematical concepts are currently encoded into the tensor space.

## The Problem

The tensor representations for mathematical concepts were generated by asking a language model to produce the encoding scheme. We have not yet closely examined what the model actually gave us, but based on a pattern we've observed across the project, we have strong reason to suspect the encoding is shallow.

**The pattern:** When we asked a small model to generate verification logic for our reasoning tool pipeline (Forge), it produced regex — the shallowest, most available form of structural checking. When we ask a model to encode mathematical concepts into tensors, it likely produced the encoding equivalent of regex: surface-level feature representations that are easy to search but capture associative proximity rather than structural proximity.

This matters because Noesis needs to find *compositional bridges* — connections between concepts whose internal architecture is analogous even when they share nothing at the surface. If the tensor space is organized by shallow features (shared terminology, similar contexts, similar algebraic properties), then:

- Proximity searches find concepts that are associatively related rather than structurally bridgeable
- The 3.4x result may be real but measuring something different from what we intend
- Discovered compositions would look like findings but wouldn't generate reasoning-relevant training signal downstream
- The entire flywheel inherits a shallow-similarity bias from the top

## The Deeper Question: What Should Mathematical Proximity Mean?

Mathematical "nearness" is at least three different things that don't align:

1. **Syntactic proximity** — formulas that look similar, share symbols, appear in similar papers. This is what embedding models produce naturally. Almost useless for composition discovery.

2. **Semantic proximity** — concepts that mean related things (group theory ↔ symmetry, derivatives ↔ rates of change). Closer to useful but finds connections that are already known.

3. **Structural proximity** — concepts whose internal architecture is analogous even when they share nothing at surface or semantic levels (Fourier transforms ↔ wavelet decomposition, recursion ↔ fixed-point theorems). Defined by: what does this concept take in and produce? What constraints does it enforce? What breaks when you remove a component? What proof strategy does it yield to?

Category 3 is where Noesis needs to operate.

## The Multi-Type Problem

Mathematical concepts are not a single kind of thing. Some are algebraic objects (with properties like closure, associativity, identity elements). Some imply time series (stationarity, periodicity, causal ordering). Some are steps in proof sequences (what they assume, what they establish, what techniques they employ). Some are visual/symbolic notation that carry spatial/relational semantics in human perceptual space.

Each type implies a different similarity metric. Two algebraic structures are close when they share operational properties. Two proof steps are close when they're interchangeable in a reasoning chain. Two visual notations are close when they cue the same spatial reasoning.

This suggests Noesis may need not one tensor space but a typed family of them, with composition discovery happening *across* types rather than within. The interesting bridges are almost always cross-type: an algebraic property that implies a visual symmetry, a time series technique that mirrors a proof strategy.

A single tensor search flattens a multi-perspectival object into one view and calls it "proximity." Parallel multi-typed search is more honest — it admits that nearness depends on what you're looking for.

## The Anthropomorphism Problem

Mathematical formalism feels objective but is a human artifact — shaped by human perceptual biases, notational conventions, and intuitions about similarity. Any encoding inherits those biases invisibly. The deeper goal of this project is building a knowledge substrate that could support thoughts humans can't think. If meaning is a human construct and math derives from it, then the current encoding is necessarily trapped in human-legible similarity.

The question: can we build a representation that preserves human-originated structure while leaving room for relationships humans wouldn't recognize?

## Relevant Existing Fields

Several fields have attacked parts of this problem:

- **Noether's theorem**: Every continuous symmetry of a physical system has a corresponding conservation law. This is the deepest known genealogical link — proving that separate equations (energy conservation, momentum conservation) are siblings with a common symmetry parent.

- **Dimensional analysis / natural units**: The reducibility of ~300 physical constants to a handful of dimensionless numbers is itself a genealogical claim — most constants are derived from a small basis set.

- **Category theory**: Studies mathematical structures and their relations through functors (structure-preserving maps between categories). Doesn't ask "are these similar?" but "is there a structure-preserving map between them?" John Baez's Rosetta Stone program explicitly maps between physics, topology, logic, and computation showing shared categorical ancestry.

- **The Langlands program**: Connects number theory, algebraic geometry, and representation theory through deep structural correspondences.

None of these have produced a *computationally searchable* representation. Category theory gives the language but not the tensor. Noether gives genealogy but doesn't extend to arbitrary formula-to-formula relationships.

## The Idea: Physical-Universe Math as Base Truth

One possible anchor: separate the mathematics that describes the physical universe — constants, quantum equations, conservation laws, symmetry groups — as a "ground category." Not because it's more true in an absolute sense, but because physical law provides empirically verified structural constraints that could serve as a fixed reference frame against which other structural relationships become measurable.

Is there an etymology of formulas that interconnects the fundamental equations of physics? Can the genealogical links between physical constants and the equations that contain them serve as a base layer of structurally grounded relationships?

## What I'm Asking the Council

### 1. Tear apart the current approach
Assume the model-generated tensor encoding is shallow (surface features, co-occurrence patterns, terminological similarity). What specific failure modes would this produce in composition discovery? Give me concrete examples of:
- Two concepts this encoding would place close together that are NOT compositionally bridgeable
- Two concepts it would place far apart that ARE deeply structurally analogous

### 2. Design the encoding dimensions
If you were designing a tensor representation for mathematical concepts optimized for *structural composition discovery* (not similarity search, not retrieval, not clustering), what dimensions would you include? Be specific and operational — not "structural properties" but exactly what you'd measure and how.

### 3. Address the multi-type problem
How should different types of mathematical objects (algebraic, time-series, proof-step, symbolic/visual, physical-law) be encoded in relation to each other? One space with typed dimensions? Multiple spaces with cross-space bridges? Something else? What are the tradeoffs?

### 4. Evaluate the base-truth anchor idea
Is using physically-grounded mathematics (constants, conservation laws, symmetry groups, quantum equations) as a reference category viable? What would this buy us? What would it miss? Is there existing work on the genealogy/etymology of physical equations that we should be building on?

### 5. Address the anthropomorphism trap
How do we encode mathematical concepts in a way that doesn't just recapitulate human intuitions about similarity? Is this even possible, or is human mathematical intuition the only available grounding? If we build a multi-lens parallel search system, could the *disagreements* between lenses point toward genuinely novel (non-human-legible) structural relationships?

### 6. Category theory as search metric
Can categorical concepts (functors, natural transformations, adjunctions) be operationalized as a computational search metric rather than just a proof language? Has anyone attempted this? What would a "morphism signature" encoding look like in practice — representing each mathematical object by what categories it maps between, what structure it preserves, and what it breaks?

## Constraints
- The system operates at small model scale (0.5B–3B parameters). Solutions must be computationally tractable.
- Tensor train decomposition (THOR library) is the current compression framework. Alternative compression approaches are welcome but must justify the migration cost.
- The encoding must be machine-searchable. Beautiful theoretical frameworks that can't be converted into proximity operations in a vector/tensor space don't solve the problem.
- We are not looking for incremental improvements to embedding-based search. We are looking for a fundamentally different representation of mathematical knowledge that supports composition discovery.
