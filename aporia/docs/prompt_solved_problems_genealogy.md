## THE PROMPT:

I am building a catalog of SOLVED mathematical problems — problems that were once open and then got resolved. For each, I need the "proof genealogy": the sequence of mathematical ideas, techniques, and intermediate results that led to the solution.

This is NOT about listing famous theorems. It's about understanding the STRUCTURE of how solutions happen:
- Did it require one breakthrough insight, or a chain of incremental advances?
- Did the solution come from the same field as the problem, or from a completely different domain?
- Were there multiple independent paths to the solution?
- What prerequisites had to be in place before the solution became possible?
- How long was the gap between the problem being posed and being solved?

Give me 25 solved problems, with diversity across:
- TIME SCALE: some solved quickly (< 10 years), some after decades, some after centuries
- SOLUTION TYPE: some by single insight, some by long programs, some by computer
- DOMAIN TRANSFER: some solved within their own field, some by importing ideas from far away
- DIFFICULTY: from undergraduate-level to Fields-Medal-level

For each problem, provide:

1. **Problem name and statement** (precise)
2. **Year posed / Year solved** (and by whom)
3. **Time gap** (years open)
4. **The proof genealogy** — list the KEY STEPS in order:
   - Step 1: [Year] [Who] [What they contributed]
   - Step 2: [Year] [Who] [What they contributed]
   - ... continuing to the final solution
   - Note any BRANCHES (independent parallel paths) and MERGES (where separate lines converge)
5. **Domain transfer?** Did the solution technique come from the same field or a different one?
6. **Solution type**: Single insight / Long program / Computer-assisted / Multiple independent proofs
7. **What barrier did it break?** (Search space / Finite-infinite bridge / Representation / Conceptual / Metamathematical)
8. **What did solving it unlock?** What other results followed?
9. **Could it have been predicted?** In hindsight, were there signs that the solution was close?

PRIORITIZE problems where:
- The solution came from an UNEXPECTED domain (technique transfer)
- Multiple proof paths exist (shows the solution was "ripe")
- The proof genealogy has interesting BRANCHING/MERGING structure
- Solving it opened floodgates (many subsequent results)

Include at least:
- 3 problems solved by computer or computer-assisted proof
- 3 problems where the solution came from a completely different field
- 3 problems with multiple independent proofs
- 3 problems that took > 100 years to solve
- 3 problems solved in the last 10 years (2015-2025)

Format the proof genealogy as a numbered sequence with dates and contributors. If the path branches, show both branches and where they merge.
