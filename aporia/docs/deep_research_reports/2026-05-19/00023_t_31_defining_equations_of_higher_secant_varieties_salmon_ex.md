## Introduction to Secant Varieties and Tensor Rank

Knowledge of the defining equations of higher secant varieties is fundamentally important in the study of algebraic geometry and has drawn intense attention in recent years due to its relation to tensor rank problems, complexity theory, and algebraic statistics [cite: 11]. 

### Tensors and the Segre Variety
Let $A, B,$ and $C$ be finite-dimensional vector spaces over the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$. A tensor $T \in A \otimes B \otimes C$ is a multidimensional array of numbers. In quantum mechanics, it may represent a tripartite state; in algebraic statistics, it might represent a joint probability distribution of three random variables [cite: 6, 7].

The simplest tensors are the **pure** or **rank-1 tensors**, which can be written as an outer product of vectors $a \otimes b \otimes c$, where $a \in A, b \in B, c \in C$. The projectivization of the set of all rank-1 tensors forms a classical algebraic variety known as the **Segre variety**. It is defined by the Segre embedding:
\[ \text{Seg}: \mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C \rightarrow \mathbb{P}(A \otimes B \otimes C) \]
\[ ([a], [b], [c]) \mapsto [a \otimes b \otimes c] \]
The Segre variety is geometrically well-understood. Its defining ideal is generated by the $2 \times 2$ minors of the matrices obtained by "flattening" the tensor into matrices [cite: 7].

### Secant Varieties and Border Rank
The **rank** of a general tensor $T$ is defined as the minimum integer $r$ such that $T$ can be expressed as the sum of $r$ rank-1 tensors [cite: 7, 12]. However, unlike matrices (which are 2-tensors), the set of tensors of rank exactly $r$ (for $r > 1$) is generally not closed in either the Zariski or Euclidean topology.

To study these spaces using algebraic geometry, one must take the topological closure. The $r$-th **secant variety** of a projective variety $X \subset \mathbb{P}^N$, denoted $\sigma_r(X)$, is defined as the Zariski closure of the union of all $(r-1)$-planes spanned by $r$ points on $X$ [cite: 13, 14]. In the context of the Segre variety $X = \text{Seg}(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C)$, the $r$-th secant variety $\sigma_r(X)$ represents the set of all tensors of **border rank** at most $r$ [cite: 7, 14]. A tensor has border rank $r$ if it can be approximated arbitrarily well by tensors of rank $r$, even if its true rank is strictly greater than $r$ [cite: 7, 12].

Determining the dimension, structure, and defining equations of higher secant varieties is notoriously difficult. If $X \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ is an irreducible variety of dimension $k$, the "expected" dimension of its $\ell$-th secant variety is $\min(\ell k + \ell - 1, N)$ [cite: 15]. When the actual dimension falls short of this expectation, the variety is said to be *defective* [cite: 16]. Secant varieties of Segre products with more than two factors exhibit complex defectiveness behaviors that have been classically studied but only partially classified [cite: 16].

## The Salmon Problem and Algebraic Statistics

The intense modern focus on $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ stems from an explicit challenge known as the **Salmon Problem** or **Salmon Conjecture**. 

### Origins of the Prize
In 2007, mathematician Elizabeth Allman offered a prize of Alaskan salmon to anyone who could find the defining ideal of the fourth secant variety of the Segre product of three projective 3-spaces [cite: 14, 17]. This algebraic variety, $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$, serves as a statistical model for biological evolution [cite: 6]. The prize itself—a self-caught and smoked Copper River salmon—was subsequently awarded to Shmuel Friedland in 2010 for his solution of the set-theoretic version of the conjecture [cite: 18, 19].

### Phylogenetics and the General Markov Model
In the field of phylogenetics, researchers aim to reconstruct the evolutionary history of species using genetic data. This history is modeled as a tree (or sometimes a network, to account for hybridization) where the leaves correspond to extant (living) species, and the interior nodes represent extinct hidden ancestors [cite: 11]. 

The variables in this model correspond to the four DNA nucleotides: {A, C, G, T}, which mathematically translates to a 4-dimensional complex vector space $\mathbb{C}^4$ (or its projectivization $\mathbb{P}^3$) [cite: 6]. If we consider a simple star tree with one hidden ancestor and three extant species, the joint probability distribution of observing specific nucleotide patterns across the three species can be modeled as a mixture of four independence models (one for each possible nucleotide of the hidden ancestor) [cite: 7]. 

Because the observations at the extant species are conditionally independent given the ancestor, the mathematical structure perfectly maps to the 4th secant variety of $\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3$ [cite: 6]. The invariants of this statistical model—polynomial equations that evaluate to zero precisely when a probability distribution fits the model—are exactly the generators of the ideal of the algebraic variety [cite: 6]. 

The seminal work of Allman and Rhodes in 2003 implies that solving the Salmon Problem (finding these invariants) would provide all the necessary phylogenetic invariants for the general Markov model of any binary evolutionary tree with four states [cite: 7]. Consequently, finding these defining equations has monumental implications for computational biology, as these closed-form solutions help deduce new knowledge about evolutionary interrelations directly from data [cite: 18].

## Theoretical Reductions and Inheritance

Before diving into the exact equations that solve the set-theoretic Salmon Conjecture, it is necessary to understand the geometric reductions that made the problem tractable.

### Subspace Varieties
A critical concept in understanding secant varieties is the **subspace variety**. The subspace variety $\text{Sub}_{p,q,r}(A \otimes B \otimes C)$ is defined as the set of all tensors $T \in \mathbb{P}(A \otimes B \otimes C)$ such that there exist subspaces $C_p \subseteq A$, $C_q \subseteq B$, and $C_r \subseteq C$ of dimensions $p, q,$ and $r$ respectively, where $T \in \mathbb{P}(C_p \otimes C_q \otimes C_r)$ [cite: 7, 12]. In simpler terms, these are tensors that can be written using fewer variables [cite: 7, 20]. 

Crucially, subspace varieties contain secant varieties. Specifically, $\sigma_r(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C) \subseteq \text{Sub}_{r,r,r}(A \otimes B \otimes C)$ [cite: 7, 21]. Therefore, if one can find the equations defining the subspace varieties, one inherently obtains a subset of the necessary equations defining the secant variety [cite: 6, 7]. The ideal of a subspace variety is generated by the minors of the matrices obtained through flattenings [cite: 12, 20].

### The Landsberg-Manivel Inheritance Theorem
In 2004, Landsberg and Manivel developed the concept of **inheritance**, showing how to take equations that vanish on a secant variety in a smaller dimensional space and "lift" them to equations that vanish on a secant variety in a larger space [cite: 20, 21]. 

Applying this to the Salmon Problem, Landsberg and Manivel achieved a major dimension reduction. They proved that, as sets, the variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ is defined by the union of three sets of equations [cite: 21]:
1. Strassen's commutation conditions (degree 5).
2. Equations inherited from the smaller secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$.
3. Equations defining the subspace variety $\text{Sub}_{4,4,4}$ (which are trivial in the $4 \times 4 \times 4$ case).

(Note: Shmuel Friedland later pointed out and corrected a minor error in the original Landsberg-Manivel proof, securing the reduction's validity [cite: 14, 21].)

Because of this inheritance, the crux of the Salmon Problem was reduced to finding the defining equations of $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$. This was mathematically profound, as it shifted the computational burden to a strictly smaller space [cite: 6, 7].

## Algebraic Techniques for Finding Defining Equations

Finding the ideal of a secant variety, denoted $I(\sigma_r(X))$, involves an interplay of commutative algebra, representation theory, and linear algebra.

### Flattenings and the Garcia-Stillman-Sturmfels Conjecture
A **flattening** realizes a tensor $T \in A \otimes B \otimes C$ as a linear map (a matrix) by partitioning the factors. For example, one can map $A \otimes B \otimes C \rightarrow A \otimes (B \otimes C)$ [cite: 12, 22]. The rank of this flattened matrix provides a strict upper bound on the border rank of the original tensor [cite: 11, 12]. 

If a tensor has border rank $r$, then the matrix obtained by flattening it will have rank at most $r$. Therefore, the $(r+1) \times (r+1)$ minors of the flattening matrices yield polynomial equations that must evaluate to zero on the secant variety [cite: 6, 12]. 

Inspired by Bayesian networks and algebraic statistics, the **Garcia-Stillman-Sturmfels conjecture** posited that the ideal of the *first* secant variety of any Segre variety is generated entirely by the $3 \times 3$ minors of flattenings [cite: 7, 22]. While flattenings provide an easy tool for generating equations, they are rarely sufficient for higher secant varieties. For example, $\sigma_3(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ is an example of a secant variety that does not have *any* equations coming from flattenings, because there are no $5 \times 5$ minors of a $3 \times 12$ or $9 \times 4$ matrix [cite: 7]. The Salmon Problem ($\sigma_4$) similarly requires equations of much higher complexity [cite: 22].

### Representation Theory and Schur Modules
Because the Segre variety and its secant varieties are defined purely by multilinear algebra, they possess a large degree of symmetry. Specifically, $\sigma_r(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C)$ is invariant under the action of the group $G = \text{GL}(A) \times \text{GL}(B) \times \text{GL}(C)$ (the product of general linear groups representing changes of coordinates in each factor) [cite: 6, 14]. 

This symmetry is a remarkably powerful tool. It means that the coordinate ring of the variety, and consequently the homogeneous ideal $I(\sigma_r(X))$, decomposes into a direct sum of irreducible $G$-modules [cite: 22]. 

The irreducible submodules of the polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[p_{ijk}]_d$ (where $p_{ijk}$ are the coordinates of the tensor) are isomorphic to **Schur modules**, indexed by partitions $\pi_1, \pi_2, \pi_3$ of the integer $d$ [cite: 6]. Therefore, hunting for a polynomial of degree $d$ in the ideal is equivalent to finding a specific highest weight vector in a Schur module $S_{\pi_1}A \otimes S_{\pi_2}B \otimes S_{\pi_3}C$ that vanishes on the secant variety [cite: 6]. 

This "Ideal Mantra"—that polynomials in $G$-modules are "one for all and all for one" [cite: 7]—allows researchers to test a single highest weight vector. If it vanishes on the secant variety, the entire module of polynomials is in the ideal. This dramatically reduces the computational complexity of the search [cite: 6].

## The Resolution of the Set-Theoretic Salmon Conjecture

By combining Landsberg and Manivel's geometric inheritance reduction with representation theory and numerical methods, the set-theoretic Salmon Conjecture was eventually resolved. The zero set of the variety is defined by a highly complex combination of polynomials of degrees 5, 6, and 9 [cite: 6, 23].

### Degree 5 Equations: Strassen's Commutation Conditions
The degree 5 polynomials arise from Strassen's commutation conditions [cite: 2, 3]. These provide necessary conditions for a tensor to have a specific border rank. The algebraic formulation of these degree 5 polynomials maps to the representation module:
\[ M_5 := S_{(3,1,1)}A^* \otimes S_{(2,1,1,1)}B^* \otimes S_{(2,1,1,1)}C^* \oplus \dots \]
These constraints yield 1,728 equations in degree 5 [cite: 14, 20].

### Degree 6 Equations: The Landsberg-Manivel Polynomials
Using representation theory, Landsberg and Manivel proved the existence of degree 6 polynomials that vanish on $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ but do *not* arise from the degree 5 ideal. These correspond to the module:
\[ M_6 := S_{(2,2,2)}\mathbb{C}^3 \otimes S_{(2,2,2)}\mathbb{C}^3 \otimes S_{(3,1,1,1)}\mathbb{C}^4 \]
This space yields 1,000 equations of degree 6 [cite: 14, 20]. There are 10 linearly independent polynomials of degree 6 in this module, frequently referred to as the LM-polynomials [cite: 3, 6]. Evaluating these symbolic equations reveals massive complexity; they consist of polynomials featuring between 576 and 936 individual monomials each [cite: 6, 7].

### Degree 9 Equations: Symmetrization Conditions
Finally, degree 9 equations are derived from Strassen's classical result on the hyperdeterminant of $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2)$ [cite: 2, 7]. These symmetrization conditions produce an astonishing 8,000 equations of degree 9. A natural basis for the degree 9 polynomials contains upwards of 43,668 monomials spanning 36 variables, equating to a 23 Megabyte text file just to store the symbolic polynomials [cite: 6]. 

### Proof via Numerical Algebraic Geometry (Bertini)
Because of the massive number of terms, classical symbolic commutative algebra (like calculating Gröbner bases) fails to compute the zero sets of these polynomials in a reasonable timeframe [cite: 2]. To overcome this, Daniel J. Bates and Luke Oeding utilized **Numerical Algebraic Geometry (NAG)** [cite: 14, 17]. 

NAG relies on homotopy continuation methods to track paths of solutions to polynomial systems over the complex numbers [cite: 8, 17]. Using the software package *Bertini*, Bates and Oeding computed **witness sets** and **pseudowitness sets** for the varieties [cite: 8, 14]. A witness set consists of a system of equations, a linear space intersecting the variety transversally, and the finite set of points in that intersection [cite: 8].

Through distributed parallel computation, they tracked the numerical paths and confirmed that the zero set of the $M_6$ module consists of precisely two components: the secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ and the subspace variety $\text{Sub}_{3,3,3}$ [cite: 6]. Adding the degree 9 polynomials elegantly isolated the secant variety, numerically verifying the set-theoretic Salmon Conjecture to an extremely high numerical accuracy [cite: 14, 24].

### Formal Theoretical Proof by Friedland and Gross
While the numerical results provided by Bates and Oeding were compelling, a rigorous algebraic proof devoid of floating-point approximations was highly desired. In 2010, Shmuel Friedland published a theoretical proof for the set-theoretic equations [cite: 6, 14]. 

In 2011/2012, Friedland and Elizabeth Gross refined this proof, demonstrating completely symbolically that the irreducible variety of $4 \times 4 \times 4$ complex-valued tensors of border rank at most 4 is precisely the zero set of the Strassen commutative conditions (degree 5), the Landsberg-Manivel polynomials (degree 6), and the symmetrization conditions (degree 9) [cite: 3, 23]. They utilized symbolic computation in *Mathematica* to factor the LM-polynomials and proved their necessity and sufficiency over the required varieties, laying the set-theoretic Salmon Conjecture to rest [cite: 3].

## The Ideal-Theoretic and Scheme-Theoretic Status

Despite the success in defining the *set-theoretic* boundaries of the variety, the ultimate goal—the **ideal-theoretic** or **scheme-theoretic** defining equations—remains notoriously open [cite: 5, 6].

### Set-Theoretic vs. Ideal-Theoretic
A set-theoretic solution provides a list of polynomials $f_1, \dots, f_k$ such that their common zero locus exactly equals the variety $X$. However, these polynomials generate an ideal $J = \langle f_1, \dots, f_k \rangle$ that might only be equal to the vanishing ideal $I(X)$ *up to its radical* ($\sqrt{J} = I(X)$) [cite: 5]. 

The **ideal-theoretic** problem demands finding a set of polynomials that generate the radical ideal $I(X)$ directly [cite: 5]. The **scheme-theoretic** version asks for the defining ideal up to saturation, meaning the ideal accurately describes not just the geometry, but the underlying scheme structure (including potential nilpotents or multiplicities) everywhere except possibly at the irrelevant ideal [cite: 5].

As noted by leading experts like J.M. Landsberg, solving the scheme-theoretic version of the Salmon Problem likely requires entirely new algebraic ideas [cite: 5]. While the degree 5, 6, and 9 polynomials cut out the set, they may not generate the full ideal. Bates and Oeding noted that their degree 6 equations do not lie in the ideal generated by the degree 5 equations (hence they are non-trivial generators), but it is unproven whether further higher-degree generators are strictly necessary to form a Gröbner basis for the entire ideal [cite: 14, 20]. 

## Extensions and Related Higher Secant Varieties

The techniques developed for the Salmon Problem have catalyzed the study of defining equations for other higher secant varieties. This "Matryoshka structure"—a term coined by Sijong Kwak and Fyodor Zak—describes the beautifully nested homological structures and Betti tables governing higher secant varieties [cite: 25].

### Secant Varieties of Segre-Veronese Varieties
An important extension of this research involves **Segre-Veronese varieties**, which parameterize partially symmetric rank-1 tensors [cite: 12, 22]. Claudiu Raicu successfully proved a generalization of the Garcia-Stillman-Sturmfels conjecture, showing that the ideal of the variety of secant lines (the *first* secant variety) to any Segre-Veronese variety is generated in degree 3 by the $3 \times 3$ minors of flattenings [cite: 22]. 

Furthermore, Raicu explicitly described the decomposition of the coordinate ring of these secant line varieties into a sum of irreducible representations under the general linear group, yielding deep plethystic formulas for decomposing symmetric powers of tensor products [cite: 22].

### Marginal Independence Models and Toric Varieties
Recent extensions have pushed the study into discrete marginal independence models. As reported at the SIAM AG25 conference, there is a bijection between marginal independence models on $n$ random variables and split closed order ideals in the poset of partial set partitions [cite: 11]. It has been established that every discrete marginal independence model forms a toric variety in an appropriate coordinate system, opening new pathways to perform explicit computations much like the "Rough Veronese" variety used in stochastic analysis [cite: 11, 25].

## Applications Across Disciplines

The mathematical pursuit of defining equations for higher secant varieties extends far beyond pure algebraic geometry, penetrating multiple applied fields.

### Algebraic Complexity Theory
In theoretical computer science, a primary goal is bounding the complexity of algorithms, such as matrix multiplication. The complexity of matrix multiplication can be measured by the tensor rank of the bilinear map representing the multiplication [cite: 7]. If one considers the tensor $\phi \in \mathbb{C}^{n^2} \otimes \mathbb{C}^{n^2} \otimes \mathbb{C}^{n^2}$ representing $n \times n$ matrix multiplication, the minimum $r$ such that $\phi \in \sigma_r(\mathbb{P}^{n^2-1} \times \mathbb{P}^{n^2-1} \times \mathbb{P}^{n^2-1})$ dictates the border rank and thus the optimal asymptotic complexity of the algorithm [cite: 7]. Lickteig and Strassen leveraged equations of secant varieties to prove lower bounds on these complexities [cite: 7].

### Signal Processing and Machine Learning
In signal processing, decomposing tensors into sums of simpler rank-1 tensors is fundamental to "blind identification of under-determined mixtures" [cite: 7]. This is famously utilized in the CDMA (Code-Division Multiple Access) protocol for mobile phone networks [cite: 6]. 

In machine learning, higher-dimensional datasets are frequently mapped onto Stiefel and Grassmannian manifolds [cite: 11]. The PSC (Principal Stiefel Coordinates) methodology actively leverages the algebraic geometry of secant and subspace varieties to reduce ambient dimensionality while maintaining equivariance with respect to orthogonal group actions [cite: 11].

### Quantum Information Theory
In quantum physics, the state of a composite system of $n$ qubits is represented as a tensor in $(\mathbb{C}^2)^{\otimes n}$. The secant varieties of the Segre embedding of $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \dots \times \mathbb{P}^1$ categorize the levels of **quantum entanglement** [cite: 6]. Tensors of rank 1 represent separable (unentangled) states, while higher ranks correspond to GHZ states, W states, and other complex multi-partite entangled states. Understanding the defining equations of these secant varieties provides explicit polynomial invariants to measure and classify entanglement [cite: 26, 27].
