## 1. Introduction to the Representation Theory of the Symmetric Group

To properly contextualize Saxl's conjecture and the recent developments in 2025 and 2026, it is necessary to establish the rigorous mathematical foundation of the representation theory of the symmetric group $S_n$. The study of how groups act on vector spaces is a cornerstone of modern algebra, with profound implications spanning quantum physics, algebraic geometry, and computational complexity theory. 

### 1.1 Partitions, Young Tableaux, and Specht Modules

The irreducible representations (or "irreps") of the symmetric group $S_n$ over the field of complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ are intricately linked to the combinatorial theory of integer partitions. A partition $\lambda$ of a positive integer $n$ (denoted $\lambda \vdash n$) is a weakly decreasing sequence of positive integers $\lambda = (\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots, \lambda_r)$ such that $\sum_{i=1}^r \lambda_i = n$. 

Each partition $\lambda$ corresponds to a unique geometric shape known as a Young diagram, consisting of left-justified rows of boxes, where the $i$-th row contains $\lambda_i$ boxes. By filling these boxes with the integers $1$ through $n$, one creates a Young tableau. If the entries strictly increase across each row and down each column, the tableau is called a Standard Young Tableau (SYT). The number of standard Young tableaux of shape $\lambda$, denoted $f^\lambda$, gives the dimension of the corresponding irreducible representation of $S_n$, a relation elegantly captured by the hook-length formula.

The actual vector spaces that realize these representations are known as Specht modules, denoted $S^\lambda$. The construction of $S^\lambda$ involves defining an action of the symmetric group on the set of tabloids (equivalence classes of tableaux under row-preserving permutations) and isolating a specific subspace generated by polytabloids. Over fields of characteristic zero, the set $\{S^\lambda \mid \lambda \vdash n\}$ forms a complete set of pairwise non-isomorphic irreducible representations of $S_n$.

### 1.2 Characters and the Murnaghan-Nakayama Rule

The character of a representation is a class function that returns the trace of the representation matrix for each group element. For the symmetric group, conjugacy classes are entirely determined by cycle types, which are also indexed by integer partitions $\mu \vdash n$. Thus, the character table of $S_n$ is a square matrix whose rows and columns are indexed by partitions of $n$. We denote the value of the irreducible character associated with $\lambda$ evaluated at a permutation of cycle type $\mu$ as $\chi^\lambda(\mu)$.

Computing these character values relies on the Murnaghan-Nakayama rule, a recursive combinatorial algorithm. The rule states that:
\[ \chi^\lambda(\mu) = \sum_{T} (-1)^{\text{ht}(T)} \]
where the sum is over all border-strip tableaux $T$ of shape $\lambda$ and type $\mu$, and $\text{ht}(T)$ relates to the height of the border strips [cite: 4]. A crucial consequence of this rule is that if no border-strip tableau exists for a given pair $(\lambda, \mu)$, the character value is identically zero [cite: 4]. The presence and distribution of these zeros in the character table of the symmetric group is a subject of intense contemporary research, playing a critical role in the resolution of tensor product conjectures [cite: 4].

## 2. The Kronecker Problem and Tensor Products

While the irreducible representations of $S_n$ are well understood in isolation, their interactions via the tensor product form one of the most notoriously difficult open problems in algebraic combinatorics.

### 2.1 The Definition of Kronecker Coefficients

Given two Specht modules $S^\mu$ and $S^\nu$ for $\mu, \nu \vdash n$, one can form their inner tensor product $S^\mu \otimes S^\nu$. This tensor product is a valid module for $S_n$ under the diagonal action $g \cdot (v \otimes w) = (gv) \otimes (gw)$. By Maschke's Theorem, this module must decompose into a direct sum of irreducible representations:
\[ S^\mu \otimes S^\nu \cong \bigoplus_{\lambda \vdash n} g(\lambda, \mu, \nu) S^\lambda \]
The non-negative integers $g(\lambda, \mu, \nu)$ are known as the **Kronecker coefficients** [cite: 8]. 

In terms of character theory, the Kronecker coefficients are computed via the standard inner product of characters:
\[ g(\lambda, \mu, \nu) = \langle \chi^\lambda, \chi^\mu \chi^\nu \rangle = \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \chi^\lambda(\sigma) \chi^\mu(\sigma) \chi^\nu(\sigma) \]
Because the formula is symmetric with respect to the three partitions, the Kronecker coefficients exhibit complete $S_3$ symmetry in their arguments [cite: 8]. 

### 2.2 The Search for a Combinatorial Rule

The "Kronecker Problem," originally posed by Murnaghan in 1938, asks for a positive combinatorial interpretation of the Kronecker coefficients—akin to the famous Littlewood-Richardson rule which governs the decomposition of the outer tensor product of symmetric group representations (and equivalently, the multiplication of Schur functions) [cite: 8]. Despite over eight decades of intensive effort, a general combinatorial rule for $g(\lambda, \mu, \nu)$ remains elusive. 

The difficulty of the Kronecker problem is inherently tied to computational complexity. Bürgisser and Ikenmeyer (2008) proved that computing Kronecker coefficients is #P-hard in general [cite: 8]. Furthermore, even the seemingly simpler task of deciding *positivity*—that is, determining whether $g(\lambda, \mu, \nu) > 0$ for a given triple—has been shown to be NP-hard by Ikenmeyer, Mulmuley, and Walter (2017) [cite: 9, 10].

### 2.3 Connections to Geometric Complexity Theory and Quantum Physics

The importance of Kronecker coefficients extends far beyond algebraic combinatorics. In the realm of quantum information theory, the non-vanishing of Kronecker coefficients relates directly to the Quantum Marginal Problem, which asks whether there exists a global quantum state compatible with a given set of reduced density matrices for subsystems. 

In theoretical computer science, Kronecker coefficients are the central characters in Geometric Complexity Theory (GCT), a program initiated by Mulmuley and Sohoni [cite: 11, 12]. GCT attempts to resolve the P vs. NP (or VP vs. VNP) problem by translating it into a problem in algebraic geometry and representation theory [cite: 11, 12]. The strategy involves comparing the orbit closures of the determinant and the permanent under the action of the general linear group. To separate these complexity classes, GCT proposes finding "occurrence obstructions"—irreducible representations that appear in the coordinate ring of the orbit closure of the determinant but not the permanent [cite: 11, 12]. Since the coordinate rings decompose into $GL_n$-modules whose multiplicities are governed by representation-theoretic constants closely related to Kronecker coefficients, understanding the positivity and asymptotics of $g(\lambda, \mu, \nu)$ is considered a critical prerequisite for progress in GCT [cite: 11, 12, 13].

## 3. Saxl's Conjecture

Within the study of Kronecker coefficients, researchers often look for "universality" phenomena—instances where a specific tensor product contains a surprisingly large number of irreducible constituents. The most famous of these phenomena is encapsulated in a conjecture made by the late Jan Saxl in 2012 [cite: 5, 8, 9, 14].

### 3.1 The Staircase Partition

Let $k$ be a positive integer. The $k$-th **staircase partition**, denoted $\rho_k$, is defined as:
\[ \rho_k = (k, k-1, k-2, \dots, 2, 1) \]
The size of this partition is $N = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}$, which corresponds to the $k$-th triangular number $T_k$ [cite: 1, 9, 12, 14]. The Young diagram of $\rho_k$ literally resembles a staircase. 

Staircase partitions possess several unique combinatorial properties. They are self-conjugate (symmetric across the main diagonal), and they are $2$-cores, meaning they have no hook lengths of even parity. Consequently, the irreducible character $\chi^{\rho_k}$ has $2$-defect zero, a property that causes it to vanish on any conjugacy class containing an element of even order [cite: 3, 4].

### 3.2 Formulation of the Conjecture

During a talk at UCLA in 2012, Jan Saxl proposed the following striking conjecture:

**Saxl's Conjecture:** For any integer $k \ge 1$ and $N = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}$, the tensor square of the Specht module $S^{\rho_k}$ contains every irreducible representation of $S_N$ as a subrepresentation. Equivalently, for all partitions $\lambda \vdash N$:
\[ g(\lambda, \rho_k, \rho_k) > 0 \]
[cite: 1, 5, 7, 8, 9, 14].

This conjecture posits that the staircase partition is *Kronecker-universal* [cite: 1, 14]. If true, it means that the geometric and algebraic information encoded in just the tensor square of a single representation manages to span the entirety of the representation space of the symmetric group.

### 3.3 Historical Progress Prior to 2025

Saxl's conjecture generated significant interest. A notable milestone was achieved by Heide, Saxl, Tiep, and Zalesski in 2013, who investigated a Lie-theoretic analogue. They proved that for almost all finite simple groups of Lie type, the tensor square of the Steinberg character contains every complex irreducible character as a constituent [cite: 3, 5]. The Steinberg character serves as a natural analogue to the staircase character, as both are defect-zero characters associated with profound structural symmetries of their respective groups.

For the symmetric group itself, direct progress on the tensor square proved difficult. However, an important relaxation of the conjecture was proven: the **tensor cube version**. Harman and Ryba (2022/2023) provided a short and elegant proof demonstrating that every irreducible representation of $S_N$ appears in the tensor *cube* $S^{\rho_k} \otimes S^{\rho_k} \otimes S^{\rho_k}$ [cite: 5, 8]. 

Despite this success, the tighter tensor square formulation—the authentic Saxl's conjecture—remained stubbornly open as the mathematical community moved into 2025.

## 4. The 2025 Preprints of Soong Kyum Lee and Their Withdrawal

In late 2025, the algebraic combinatorics community was briefly electrified by two preprints authored by Soong Kyum Lee, a researcher affiliated with the Graduate School of Data Science at Kyungpook National University (and linked to computational biology efforts at CHA University) [cite: 9, 15, 16, 17]. These preprints claimed to solve several fundamental, long-standing open problems in the field, including Saxl's conjecture and the derivation of explicit formulas for genuinely three-row Kronecker coefficients [cite: 1, 2, 14, 15]. 

However, both papers were swiftly withdrawn by the author shortly after submission due to fundamental mathematical errors [cite: 1, 2]. To understand the current status of the field, it is imperative to analyze what was claimed, the methodologies employed, and the subsequent retraction.

### 4.1 Preprint 1: "Staircase Minimality and a Proof of Saxl's Conjecture" (arXiv:2512.15035)

Submitted on December 17, 2025 (v1), this manuscript explicitly claimed an unconditional proof of Saxl's conjecture [cite: 1, 9]. 

#### 4.1.1 The Claimed Methodology
Lee's proposed proof heavily relied on bridging characteristic zero representation theory with modular representation theory (specifically, representations over fields of prime characteristic, here $p=2$). The abstract outlined the following logical architecture for the proof:

1.  **The Staircase Minimality Theorem:** Lee claimed to have proven a structural theorem asserting that among all $2$-regular partitions (partitions with no part repeated $2$ or more times) of the triangular number $T_k$, the staircase partition $\rho_k$ is the *unique dominance-minimal element* [cite: 1, 12, 14]. Dominance order is a partial order on partitions where $\lambda \unrhd \mu$ if $\sum_{i=1}^j \lambda_i \ge \sum_{i=1}^j \mu_i$ for all $j$.
2.  **Ikenmeyer's Theorem:** The proof stated that combining this minimality with existing theorems by Ikenmeyer on dominance and Kronecker positivity for staircases would immediately establish that every $2$-regular partition appears as a constituent in the tensor square $S^{\rho_k} \otimes S^{\rho_k}$ [cite: 1, 14].
3.  **Modular Saturation:** To extend the result from $2$-regular partitions to *all* partitions, Lee invoked a concept termed "modular saturation." The manuscript claimed that because the diagonal entries of the decomposition matrix (which records the multiplicities of simple modules in Specht modules in characteristic $p$) are $d_{\mu\mu} = 1$, one could leverage the presence of $2$-regular partitions to force the presence of all other partitions [cite: 1, 14].
4.  **The Bessenrodt–Bowman–Sutton Lifting Theorem:** Finally, the paper claimed to complete the unconditional proof by utilizing lifting theorems developed by Bessenrodt, Bowman, and Sutton, which allow modular constituents to be lifted back to characteristic zero [cite: 1, 14].
5.  **Uniqueness:** As a corollary, Lee claimed to have proved that at triangular numbers, staircases are the *only* Kronecker-universal self-conjugate partitions [cite: 1, 9, 14].

#### 4.1.2 The Withdrawal
The claims of arXiv:2512.15035 were monumentally ambitious, bridging complex modular representation theory to resolve a characteristic zero combinatorial conjecture. However, upon rapid peer evaluation by expert reviewers in the algebraic combinatorics community, critical mathematical gaps were identified in the chain of logic [cite: 1]. 

On December 20, 2025—just three days after the initial submission—Lee formally withdrew the preprint [cite: 1]. The withdrawal notice published on arXiv was explicit and transparent:
> *"Comments: This paper requires significant revision to address mathematical gaps identified by expert reviewers. The claim of a complete proof is not justified in its current form. I am withdrawing to properly address these issues."* [cite: 1, 18]

Furthermore, an administrative note on arXiv indicated that the listed author affiliation was incorrect, adding a layer of procedural complexity to the submission [cite: 1]. As of mid-2026, no revised version of this manuscript bridging the identified gaps has been uploaded, and the proof is generally considered invalid by the mathematical community.

### 4.2 Preprint 2: "Algebraic Obstructions and the Collapse of Elementary Structure in the Kronecker Problem" (arXiv:2511.22856)

Prior to the Saxl conjecture paper, Lee submitted another highly ambitious manuscript on November 28, 2025, attempting to resolve the problem of finding explicit formulas for Kronecker coefficients with three rows [cite: 2, 15].

#### 4.2.1 The Claimed Methodology
Since Murnaghan's original work in 1938, no explicit closed-form formulas for genuinely three-row Kronecker coefficients had been discovered; they were only computable algorithmically via lattice-point methods [cite: 2, 11, 13, 15, 19, 20, 21]. Lee's paper claimed to finally provide these formulas and uncover a deep structural boundary [cite: 2, 13, 15].

The paper introduced a concept dubbed the **"Five Threshold"** [cite: 15]. Analyzing two families of three-row coefficients—symmetric triples $(n,n,k)^3$ and staircase coefficients $g(\rho_k, \rho_k, \lambda)$—Lee claimed that for parameter values $k \le 4$, the formulas exhibited elementary structure [cite: 2, 11, 13, 15, 21]. Specifically, the oscillation bounds supposedly followed a "triangular-Hogben pattern," and the resulting polynomial expressions factored completely over the integers $\mathbb{Z}$ [cite: 2, 11, 13, 15, 21]. 

However, at the critical threshold of $k=5$, Lee claimed this structure completely collapsed. The paper asserted that "algebraic obstructions" emerged—specifically, irreducible quadratic factors with negative discriminant that prevented simple polynomial factorization [cite: 2, 11, 13, 15, 21]. 

To prove these claims, Lee developed a novel technique called **"integer forcing,"** which supposedly exploited the tension between continuous asymptotics and discrete integrality [cite: 2, 13, 15, 21]. As concrete outputs, the paper claimed to prove that $g((n,n,1)^3) = 2 - (n \mod 2)$ for all $n \ge 3$, derive five explicit polynomial formulas for staircase-hook coefficients, and explicitly verify Saxl's conjecture for 132 specific three-row partitions [cite: 2, 13, 15, 21].

#### 4.2.2 The Withdrawal
Similar to the subsequent Saxl conjecture paper, the claims made in arXiv:2511.22856 did not withstand expert scrutiny. On December 20, 2025 (version 3 of the submission, coinciding with the withdrawal of the Saxl preprint), Lee withdrew the manuscript [cite: 2]. 

The withdrawal notice was identical:
> *"Comments: This paper requires significant revision to address mathematical gaps identified by expert reviewers. The claim of a complete proof is not justified in its current form. I am withdrawing to properly address these issues."* [cite: 2]

The simultaneous withdrawal of both foundational preprints effectively reset the state of the art to its pre-November 2025 status. The "Five Threshold" and the "integer forcing" technique, while conceptually interesting, lack the rigorous foundation required for acceptance in the mathematical literature.

## 5. The Current Landscape of Saxl's Conjecture (Mid-2026)

With the retraction of Lee's claimed proofs, Saxl's conjecture remains definitively open. However, the momentum in representation theory has not stalled. As of mid-2026, researchers are approaching the tensor square problem and the behavior of the staircase partition through several distinct, highly sophisticated mathematical frameworks.

### 5.1 Bounding the Zeros of Irreducible Characters (Lee Tae Young, 2026)

Because the Kronecker coefficient $g(\lambda, \rho_k, \rho_k)$ is computed as an inner product of characters, a fundamental obstruction to proving positivity is the presence of zeros in the character table. The staircase partition $\rho_k$ yields a $2$-defect zero character $\psi_k = \chi^{\rho_k}$, which is known to vanish on any conjugacy class associated with a permutation containing a cycle of even length [cite: 3, 4].

In January 2026, Lee Tae Young (Binghamton University) published a significant preprint, "Relations between values and zeros of irreducible characters of symmetric groups" (arXiv:2601.01379) [cite: 3, 4, 22, 23, 24]. This work directly attacks the character-theoretic underpinnings necessary for resolving Saxl's conjecture.

Lee Tae Young established certain polynomial relations between the values of complex irreducible characters of general finite symmetric groups [cite: 3, 4, 23, 24]. The paper utilizes these polynomial relations and Gröbner bases to identify sets of conjugacy classes such that no finite symmetric group has a complex irreducible character that vanishes at every class in the set [cite: 3, 4, 23, 24]. 

Specifically, Lee Tae Young proved that if $n$ satisfies certain conditions, then the non-identity elements of the symmetric group, $S_n \setminus \{1\}$, cannot be covered by the set of zeros of just three irreducible characters [cite: 3, 4, 23, 24]. Crucially for Saxl's conjecture, the paper proves that the values of $2$-defect zero characters (such as the staircase character $\psi_k$) can be expressed explicitly as rational functions in $n$ [cite: 3, 4, 23, 24]. The paper provides a recursive algorithm to compute these rational functions [cite: 3, 4, 23, 24]. 

As Lee Tae Young notes, Jan Saxl's conjecture essentially asserts that the zeros of the staircase character $\psi_k$ are distributed such that its tensor square correlates positively with every other irreducible character [cite: 3, 4]. If researchers can utilize Lee Tae Young's rational function algorithms to generate sufficiently sharp estimates of the non-zero character values, it may become possible to analytically bound the inner product $\langle \chi^\lambda, \psi_k^2 \rangle$ strictly above zero, thereby proving Saxl's conjecture via character bounds [cite: 3, 4].

### 5.2 Analogue for Unipotent Characters of $GL_n(q)$ (Letellier and Nam, 2025/2026)

Another major avenue of research has been to translate Saxl's conjecture from the symmetric group to groups of Lie type. In a breakthrough paper published in the journal *Algebraic Combinatorics* in 2025, Emmanuel Letellier and GyeongHyeon Nam proved a complete analogue of Saxl's conjecture for the unipotent characters of the general linear group over a finite field, $GL_n(F_q)$ [cite: 5, 25, 26].

Unipotent characters are a fundamental class of irreducible representations for finite groups of Lie type, originally classified by Lusztig. For $GL_n(F_q)$, the unipotent characters $\mathcal{U}^\lambda$ are indexed by partitions $\lambda \vdash n$, creating a deep structural parallel with the symmetric group [cite: 5].

It was previously known that if the symmetric group Kronecker coefficient $g(\lambda, \mu, \nu)$ is non-zero, then the corresponding multiplicity of the unipotent character $\mathcal{U}^\lambda$ in the tensor product $\mathcal{U}^\mu \otimes \mathcal{U}^\nu$ is also non-zero [cite: 5]. Because Saxl's conjecture asserts that all symmetric group irreps appear in the tensor square of the staircase character $\chi^{\rho_k}$, it naturally implies a Lie-theoretic analogue: that all unipotent characters of $GL_N(F_q)$ appear non-trivially in the tensor square $\mathcal{U}^{\rho_k} \otimes \mathcal{U}^{\rho_k}$ [cite: 5].

Letellier and Nam successfully proved this unipotent analogue unconditionally [cite: 5]. In their work, they bypassed the need for the original symmetric group Saxl conjecture to be true, instead relying on the distinct algebraic geometry and character sheaves associated with $GL_n(F_q)$. Furthermore, in the second part of their paper, they described conjecturally the complete set of all partitions $\mu$ for which the tensor square $\mathcal{U}^\mu \otimes \mathcal{U}^\mu$ is unipotent-universal (i.e., contains all unipotent characters of $GL_{|\mu|}(F_q)$) [cite: 5]. This work represents the most significant successful extension of the Saxl framework in recent years, firmly establishing the universality of the staircase partition within the context of finite reductive groups.

### 5.3 Spin Representations of Finite Coxeter Groups (Chen, Gu, Osborne, 2024–2026)

Expanding the scope beyond Type A Weyl groups (the symmetric groups), researchers have recently generalized Saxl's conjecture to all finite Coxeter groups. A preeminent contribution in this direction is a 2024 preprint by Yutong Chen (MIT), Li Gu (Oxford), and William Osborne (Oxford), titled "Spin Representations of Finite Coxeter Groups and Generalisations of Saxl's Conjecture" [cite: 6, 7, 27]. Their work has been prominently featured in 2026 academic seminars, including presentations at the University of Oxford [cite: 6, 7, 27].

Finite Coxeter groups serve as the Weyl groups associated with simple Lie algebras, providing the fundamental reflection symmetry structure in representation theory [cite: 7]. These groups are equipped with a root system embedded in a real Euclidean space [cite: 7]. The projective representations of these groups, known as spin representations, are constructed using Clifford algebras and the Pin and Spin groups [cite: 7].

Chen, Gu, and Osborne succeeded in placing Saxl's conjecture into a broader Lie-theoretical framework [cite: 6, 7]. By analyzing the Springer correspondence—which relates the irreducible representations of a Weyl group to the geometry of nilpotent orbits in the corresponding Lie algebra—they formulated a natural generalization of the conjecture applicable to arbitrary Weyl groups [cite: 6, 7]. 

In their findings, they verified this generalized conjecture for exceptional types and revealed deep connections to tensor product decomposition problems [cite: 6, 7]. Furthermore, they provided an alternative description of Lusztig's cuspidal families of characters, proposing a sweeping generalization of the universality phenomenon to all finite Coxeter groups [cite: 6, 7]. This abstraction indicates that the combinatorial "magic" of the staircase partition in $S_n$ is likely a specific manifestation of a ubiquitous geometric property shared by specialized spin representations across all Coxeter types.

### 5.4 Highest Weight Vectors and Geometric Complexity Theory (Amanov and Yeliussizov, 2025/2026)

From the perspective of computational complexity, Saxl's conjecture remains intimately tied to Geometric Complexity Theory (GCT). Understanding the Kronecker coefficients is required to find the "occurrence obstructions" that could separate the VP and VNP complexity classes [cite: 11, 12]. In GCT, Kronecker coefficients manifest as the dimensions of spaces of highest weight vectors for the general linear group acting on tensor spaces [cite: 10].

In a major paper titled "Highest weight vectors of tensors" (arXiv:2504.15413, revised in January 2026), Alimzhan Amanov and Damir Yeliussizov provided a breakthrough in explicitly constructing these spaces [cite: 10, 28]. 

Amanov and Yeliussizov studied the highest weight vectors for symmetric and alternating spaces of tensors whose dimensions are precisely given by generalized Kronecker coefficients [cite: 10, 28]. They provided a unified explicit construction of spanning sets of highest weight vectors, denoted as $\{\Delta\}$ (polynomials) and $\{\nabla\}$ (forms), which are indexed by multi-dimensional contingency tables (hypermatrices) [cite: 10].

Crucially, they proved that these highest weight vectors satisfy a natural yet highly non-trivial duality [cite: 10, 28]. By establishing explicit isomorphisms between dual highest weight spaces, they were able to completely describe the algebraic relations among the vectors [cite: 10, 28]. 

This work connects directly back to the efforts to prove Saxl's conjecture. As Amanov and Yeliussizov note, previous progress on Saxl's conjecture (such as Ikenmeyer's 2015 work) heavily relied on the construction and evaluation of highest weight polynomials [cite: 10]. Furthermore, Amanov and Yeliussizov utilized their duality framework to provide conceptual interpretations for the power expansions of Cayley's first hyperdeterminant and its dual exterior form [cite: 10, 28, 29, 30]. These expansions are closely related to the Alon-Tarsi conjecture on Latin hypercubes, a fundamental problem in combinatorics whose asymptotic variants have implications for the positivity of Kronecker coefficients [cite: 10, 29]. While not directly proving Saxl's conjecture, the explicit algebraic control over highest weight vectors developed in 2026 provides researchers with powerful new invariants and algebraic tools that may eventually circumvent the obstructions that caused Lee's 2025 proof to fail.

## 6. Conclusion

As of mid-2026, the mathematical community's understanding of the Kronecker problem and Saxl's conjecture exists in a state of dynamic tension. The tantalizing possibility that the problem had been unconditionally solved was dispelled by the rapid withdrawal of Soong Kyum Lee's preprints (arXiv:2512.15035 and arXiv:2511.22856) in December 2025 [cite: 1, 2]. The mathematical gaps identified in the proposed use of modular saturation and integer forcing serve as a stark reminder of the profound difficulty inherent in translating continuous or prime-characteristic phenomena back to the characteristic zero combinatorial rigidity of the symmetric group.

The tensor square formulation of Saxl's conjecture—that $S^{\rho_k} \otimes S^{\rho_k}$ contains all irreducible representations of $S_{k(k+1)/2}$—is strictly classified as an **open problem**.

However, the periphery of the conjecture is advancing rapidly. The successful proof of the unipotent analogue for $GL_n(F_q)$ by Letellier and Nam demonstrates that the geometric intuition behind the conjecture is fundamentally sound [cite: 5]. The Lie-theoretic generalizations by Chen, Gu, and Osborne have elevated the problem from a quirk of symmetric group combinatorics to a foundational question about spin representations of Coxeter groups [cite: 6, 7]. Simultaneously, hard analytical progress is being made on bounding the zeros of $2$-defect characters by Lee Tae Young [cite: 3, 4], and deep algebraic structures governing tensor spaces are being unravelled by Amanov and Yeliussizov [cite: 10].

The resolution of Saxl's conjecture will likely require a synthesis of these modern techniques—combining the asymptotic bounds of character theory with the geometric invariants of highest weight spaces. Until such a synthesis is achieved, the staircase partition will continue to hold onto its secrets, maintaining its status as one of the most compelling enigmas in modern representation theory.
