## 1. Introduction and The Origin of the Method

### 1.1 The Landscape of Boolean Complexity Measures
In the late 1980s and early 1990s, the study of Boolean functions $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$ was dominated by efforts to classify and relate various measures of their complexity. These measures quantify how "hard" it is to compute a function given different models of computation. The most prominent measures included decision tree complexity (the depth of the optimal decision tree required to compute $f$), polynomial degree (the degree of the unique multilinear real polynomial representing $f$), approximate degree, and certificate complexity [cite: 1]. 

In a seminal 1992 paper, Noam Nisan and Mario Szegedy established that virtually all of these complexity measures are polynomially related [cite: 1, 2]. This means that if $\alpha(f)$ and $\beta(f)$ are two such measures, there exists some positive constant $C > 0$ such that $\alpha(f) \le C \beta(f)^C$. Nisan and Szegedy's proof utilized an intermediate complexity measure introduced by Nisan known as **block sensitivity** ($bs(f)$). The block sensitivity of a function $f$ at an input $x$ is the maximum number of disjoint subsets (blocks) of indices $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_k \subseteq [n]$ such that flipping the bits in any block $B_i$ changes the output of the function [cite: 1, 3]. 

Block sensitivity was proposed as a generalization of a much older, simpler, and more natural measure: **critical sensitivity**, or simply **sensitivity** ($s(f)$). The sensitivity of $f$ at input $x$ is the number of single bits that, if flipped individually, alter the function's output [cite: 4]. By definition, sensitivity is at most block sensitivity ($s(f) \le bs(f)$), since each sensitive bit can be treated as a block of size one. Nisan and Szegedy posed the reverse question: is block sensitivity polynomially bounded by sensitivity? Because all other major measures were known to be polynomially tied to block sensitivity, this question was equivalent to asking whether sensitivity is polynomially related to degree, decision tree complexity, and the rest [cite: 1]. This question became formally known as the **Sensitivity Conjecture**.

### 1.2 The Gotsman-Linial Formulation
Despite intensive efforts, the Sensitivity Conjecture remained wide open for 27 years. A critical step toward its eventual resolution was provided by Craig Gotsman and Nathan Linial in 1992. They demonstrated that the Sensitivity Conjecture is entirely equivalent to a purely graph-theoretic statement about the Boolean hypercube $Q_n$ [cite: 2, 5]. 

The hypercube $Q_n$ is a graph whose vertices are the $2^n$ binary strings of length $n$, with edges connecting any two strings that differ in exactly one bit. Gotsman and Linial proved that the Sensitivity Conjecture holds if and only if there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that for any induced subgraph $H$ of $Q_n$ containing strictly more than half of the vertices (i.e., $|V(H)| \ge 2^{n-1} + 1$), the maximum degree of $H$, denoted $\Delta(H)$, satisfies $\Delta(H) \ge n^c$ [cite: 5, 6]. 

In 1988, prior to the formulation of the conjecture, Chung, Füredi, Graham, and Seymour had shown that there exist induced subgraphs on $2^{n-1}$ vertices with maximum degree 0, but any subgraph on $2^{n-1} + 1$ vertices must have a maximum degree of at least $\frac{1}{2} \log_2 n$. They also constructed a specific induced subgraph on more than half the vertices whose maximum degree was exactly $\lceil \sqrt{n} \rceil$, providing an upper bound on what could be proved [cite: 5, 7]. Therefore, proving a tight lower bound of $\sqrt{n}$ became the holy grail of this subfield.

### 1.3 Hao Huang's Signed-Graph Spectral Method
In July 2019, Hao Huang published a succinct, two-page preprint that completely resolved the Gotsman-Linial formulation, proving that any $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph of $Q_n$ has a maximum degree of at least $\sqrt{n}$ [cite: 1, 8]. 

Huang's method relied on spectral graph theory, specifically the use of **signed adjacency matrices**. An unsigned adjacency matrix of $Q_n$ has entries of $1$ for adjacent vertices and $0$ otherwise. A signed adjacency matrix, $A$, replaces some of the $1$s with $-1$s. This is equivalent to assigning a signature $\sigma: E(Q_n) \to \{+1, -1\}$ to the edges of the hypercube.

Huang defined a specific sequence of symmetric $2^n \times 2^n$ signed matrices recursively:
1.  $A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$
2.  $A_n = \begin{pmatrix} A_{n-1} & I \\ I & -A_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}$ where $I$ is the $2^{n-1} \times 2^{n-1}$ identity matrix [cite: 9].

A fundamental algebraic property of Huang's matrix $A_n$ is that its square is exactly $n$ times the identity matrix ($A_n^2 = nI$) [cite: 9, 10]. Consequently, every eigenvalue of $A_n$ must be either $\sqrt{n}$ or $-\sqrt{n}$. Because $A_n$ is a symmetric matrix with trace zero, it has exactly $2^{n-1}$ eigenvalues equal to $\sqrt{n}$ and $2^{n-1}$ eigenvalues equal to $-\sqrt{n}$ [cite: 9, 10].

To prove the Gotsman-Linial statement, Huang invoked Cauchy's Interlace Theorem [cite: 9, 11]. If one takes an induced subgraph $H$ with $2^{n-1} + 1$ vertices, its corresponding signed adjacency matrix $A_H$ is a principal submatrix of $A_n$. By Cauchy's Interlacing Theorem, the largest eigenvalue of $A_H$, denoted $\lambda_1(A_H)$, must be at least as large as the $(2^{n-1})$-th largest eigenvalue of $A_n$, which is $\sqrt{n}$ [cite: 9]. Finally, since the maximum degree $\Delta(H)$ of any graph is bounded below by the largest eigenvalue of its signed adjacency matrix, it follows directly that $\Delta(H) \ge \sqrt{n}$ [cite: 9, 12]. This elegant linear algebraic argument immediately proved the Sensitivity Conjecture [cite: 8, 13].

## 2. Algebraic Reframing and Structural Clarifications (2019-2021)

Following the publication of Huang's proof, the mathematics and theoretical computer science communities rapidly sought to understand the "magic" behind the construction of the matrix $A_n$. While the recursive block construction worked perfectly, researchers identified deep algebraic structures underlying it, paving the way for the generalizations that dominated literature from 2020 to 2026.

### 2.1 Clifford Algebras
Almost simultaneously, mathematicians Daniel Mathews, Tom Mrowka, and Terence Tao recognized that Huang's recursive matrix construction was a manifestation of **Clifford algebras** [cite: 11, 14]. A Clifford algebra is a unital associative algebra generated by a vector space equipped with a quadratic form, fundamentally serving as a generalization of the real numbers, complex numbers, and quaternions [cite: 15].

In 2019 (published in later volumes), Daniel Mathews formalized this by demonstrating that Huang's matrices $A_n$ are exactly the matrices of multiplication by a specific natural element $S$ in positive definite Clifford algebras [cite: 15, 16]. The generators of a Clifford algebra inherently anti-commute. The hypercube $Q_n$ is fundamentally composed of 4-cycles. Huang's signing mechanism is essentially a rule that assigns negative signs to the edges of $Q_n$ such that the product of the signs around *every* 4-cycle is $-1$ [cite: 7, 17]. This odd parity of negative signs around 4-cycles perfectly mirrors the anti-commutative property of Clifford algebra generators [cite: 15, 16]. By reformulating the proof in terms of Clifford algebras, Mathews abstracted the geometric properties of the cube, allowing for weighted generalizations [cite: 18]. 

### 2.2 Exterior Algebras and Twisted Convolution
Complementing the Clifford algebra perspective, Roman Karasev published work connecting Huang's signed adjacency matrix to exterior algebras, which represent a special case of Clifford algebras where the quadratic form is degenerate (zero) [cite: 11, 15]. 

Terence Tao provided another structural explanation by viewing Huang's method through the lens of **twisted convolution** [cite: 14, 19]. Tao observed that the signing could be understood by lifting functions on an elementary abelian 2-group to functions on a central extension [cite: 6, 20]. Specifically, twisted convolution acts as a component of ordinary convolution, but rather than operating on the original Boolean group $\mathbb{Z}_2^n$, it operates on a Heisenberg group extension [cite: 19]. Tao noted that the underlying inner product playing a role in Huang's spectral proof is essentially the anti-commutator of two Hermitian matrices [cite: 19]. Furthermore, Tao and others noted that the essential ingredients of Huang's proof were implicitly present in the seminal 2015 work by Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava on interlacing families and bipartite Ramanujan graphs, which utilized 2-lift operations recursively—a continuous analog to Huang's discrete doubling [cite: 19].

### 2.3 Weighted Generalizations
Leveraging these algebraic structures, the earliest extensions of Huang's method were weighted generalizations. Mathews (2021) generalized the matrix construction to allow non-negative real weights $a_1, \dots, a_n$ on the edges of $Q_n$ corresponding to the $n$ different dimensions [cite: 15, 16]. He defined the weighted degree of a vertex as the sum of the weights on its adjacent edges. By replacing the identity and basic block matrices with scaled versions corresponding to the Clifford algebra generators multiplied by the weights, Mathews proved that any $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph $H$ has a maximum weighted degree $\Delta_a(H) \ge \sqrt{\sum a_i^2}$, establishing a robust weighted Sensitivity Theorem [cite: 15, 16].

## 3. Extending the Sensitivity Theorem to New Domains (2020-2026)

With the core methodology algebraically codified, a major line of post-2019 research sought to conquer "sensitivity-style problems" for domains beyond the Boolean hypercube. If Boolean functions represent binary choices, what happens to complexity measures when the inputs are permutations, perfect matchings, or non-binary alphabets?

### 3.1 Complexity Measures on the Symmetric Group and Perfect Matchings
In 2021, a landmark paper by Dafni, Filmus, Lifshitz, Lindzey, and Vinyals extended the entire framework of Boolean complexity measures to the **symmetric group** $S_n$ (the set of all permutations of $n$ elements) and the **perfect matching association scheme** (the set of all perfect matchings on the complete graph $K_{2n}$) [cite: 1, 21]. 

For a function $f: S_n \to \{0,1\}$, defining "sensitivity" required a geometrical interpretation of the domain. Dafni et al. defined the sensitivity $s(f, x)$ of a function at a point (permutation) $x \in S_n$ as the maximum number $s$ of *disjoint transpositions* $\tau_1, \dots, \tau_s$ such that $f(\tau_i x) \neq f(x)$ for all $i$ [cite: 4]. The global sensitivity of $f$ is $\max_x s(f, x)$. 

To relate this to other complexity measures (like decision tree complexity and approximate degree), the authors first generalized classical arguments by Nisan to show that all measures *except* sensitivity are polynomially related on these domains [cite: 21]. To incorporate sensitivity into the polynomial equivalence, they directly executed a reduction to Huang's Sensitivity Theorem [cite: 1, 21]. They achieved this by introducing **pseudo-characters**—algebraic constructs originating in representation theory that witness the degree of a function over the symmetric group [cite: 21]. This approach demonstrated that a function on $S_n$ with sensitivity $s$ can be computed by an unbounded fan-in De Morgan circuit of depth $O(s \log n)$, effectively resolving the Sensitivity Conjecture for the symmetric group [cite: 4]. 

Furthermore, this framework allowed them to simplify the characterization of maximum-size $t$-intersecting families in the symmetric group—a deep result in extremal combinatorics originally proven by Ellis, Friedgut, and Pilpel (2011)—and extended it to perfect matching schemes [cite: 21, 22].

### 3.2 The $m$-ary Sensitivity Conjecture
Another direct generalization appeared in the study of $m$-ary functions $f: T^n \to T$, where $T \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ is a finite alphabet of cardinality $m$ [cite: 3]. These functions represent an intermediate step between Boolean logic and continuous functions and have deep ties to cryptography, coding theory, and strongly regular graphs [cite: 3].

For $m$-ary functions, researchers including Knauer et al. established that block sensitivity, degree, and decision tree complexity remain polynomially equivalent [cite: 3]. They formally proposed the **$m$-ary Sensitivity Conjecture**: For every $m$, there exists a constant $c$ such that $\deg(f) \in O(s(f)^c)$ for every $m$-ary function $f$ [cite: 3]. 

Drawing explicit inspiration from Gotsman and Linial, researchers provided a graph-theoretic formulation for the $m$-ary Sensitivity Conjecture in terms of induced subgraphs of **Hamming graphs** $H(n, m)$ (where the Boolean hypercube is the special case $H(n, 2)$) [cite: 3]. While the full $m$-ary conjecture has not been entirely resolved as of early 2026, Huang's spectral method provided the fundamental upper bounds, proving that $s(f) \le 2(m-1)^3 \deg(f)^2$ [cite: 3]. Furthermore, Dong studied the induced subgraphs of the Hamming graph $H(n, k)$ and the 4-ary $n$-cube $Q_4^n$ specifically utilizing signed adjacency matrices [cite: 3, 9].

### 3.3 Quantitative Bounds and Polynomial Degree ($s_0(f)s_1(f)$)
In 2026, advances in the linear dependencies of vectors associated with the hypercube led to stricter quantitative improvements on Huang's bounds [cite: 7, 17]. Researchers proved that in any induced subgraph of $H_n$ with more than half the vertices, there exist at least two vertices (one of odd parity, one of even parity) that each have at least $n$ vertices at a distance of at most 2 [cite: 7, 17]. 

By employing linear dependency techniques rather than Cauchy's Interlacing Theorem alone, researchers achieved a novel application for Boolean functions: the polynomial degree of any Boolean function $f$ is bounded above by $s_0(f)s_1(f)$, where $s_0$ and $s_1$ represent the sensitivity of the function on the 0-inputs and 1-inputs, respectively [cite: 7, 17]. This specific bound directly implies the Sensitivity Conjecture, serving as a powerful, modern refinement of Huang's 2019 corollary [cite: 7, 17].

## 4. Combinatorial and Graph Theoretic Spillovers (2020-2026)

Huang’s innovation—assigning signs to edges to compress the spectrum of an adjacency matrix—reverberated throughout graph theory, leading to the resolution of multiple open problems that, on the surface, had nothing to do with Boolean function complexity.

### 4.1 The Pachter-Kim Conjecture on Minimum Forcing Numbers
One of the most spectacular unintended consequences of Huang's method was the resolution of the **Pachter-Kim Conjecture** (1998) regarding perfect matchings [cite: 23, 24]. 

Let $M$ be a perfect matching in a graph $G$. A subset $S \subseteq M$ is called a *forcing set* of $M$ if $M$ is the unique perfect matching in $G$ that contains $S$ [cite: 24, 25]. The minimum size of a forcing set over all perfect matchings in $G$ is called the minimum forcing number, denoted $f(G)$ [cite: 23, 24]. In 1998, Pachter and Kim conjectured that the minimum forcing number of the $n$-dimensional hypercube $Q_n$ is exactly $2^{n-2}$ for all $n \ge 2$ [cite: 24, 25]. Riddle (2002) proved the conjecture for even $n$, but the general case remained open [cite: 24, 26].

In 2019, highly inspired by Huang's use of signed matrices on the hypercube, Diwan completely settled the Pachter-Kim conjecture, proving $f(Q_n) = 2^{n-2}$ for all $n \ge 2$ via a simple linear algebra technique utilizing involutory matrices (matrices that are their own inverse, $A^{-1} = A$, a property closely related to Huang's $A_n^2 = nI$) [cite: 13, 24]. Diwan demonstrated a fundamental lemma linking the forcing number to the corank of the matrix [cite: 24].

Between 2021 and 2025, Mohammadian drastically generalized Diwan’s technique [cite: 13, 25]. Mohammadian proved that for any bipartite graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, if there exists an involutory matrix $A$ over a field $F$ acting as a weighted bi-adjacency matrix with orthogonal rows, then the minimum forcing number of the prism graph $G \Box K_2$ is exactly $n/2$ [cite: 23, 25]. By 2025, Shi and Heping Zhang extended this method to circular and prismatic graphs, proving that under the same involutory matrix conditions, $f(G \Box C_{2k}) = n$ for $k \ge 2$, completely charting the forcing numbers for vast families of Cartesian product graphs [cite: 23, 25].

### 4.2 Two-Eigenvalue Covers and Graph Lifts
Huang’s specific signed adjacency matrix generated exactly two distinct eigenvalues ($\pm\sqrt{n}$). Spectral graph theorists quickly realized that signed graphs with exactly two distinct eigenvalues (or symmetric spectra with respect to the origin) represent an incredibly rich combinatorial object [cite: 9, 12]. 

Chris Godsil, Maxwell Levit, and Olha Silina (2020-2021) formalized this by developing the theory of **two-eigenvalue covers** (2ev-covers) [cite: 20, 27]. A covering graph is a structure that locally resembles a base graph but has a more complex global topology [cite: 28]. Godsil et al. defined a 2ev-cover as a covering graph whose adjacency spectrum differs from the graph it covers by exactly two eigenvalues (as a multiset) [cite: 27, 29]. 

Huang's signed adjacency matrix for the hypercube was recognized as being intimately related to the unique, 4-cycle free, 2-fold cover of the hypercube—a structure first proven to exist by Cohen and Tits in 1985 [cite: 6, 20]. By abstracting this connection, Godsil, Levit, and Silina proved several deep theorems:
1.  If a base graph is walk-regular (the number of closed walks of length $k$ starting at a vertex depends only on $k$), then any abelian two-eigenvalue cover is also walk-regular [cite: 20, 27].
2.  They provided a spectral characterization for when a cyclic 2ev-cover of a strongly-regular graph results in a distance-regular graph (a graph where the number of neighbors at various distances depends only on the distance) [cite: 17, 20].

Furthermore, the 2ev-cover framework extended the taxonomy of symmetric structures, linking group-valued adjacency matrices to systems of equiangular lines in complex space, unit-norm tight frames, and Adinkras (signed graphs used to study supersymmetry in physics) [cite: 27, 29]. 

### 4.3 Generalizations to Product Graphs and Oriented Cycles
Huang's result specifically applied to $Q_n$, which is the Cartesian product of $n$ copies of $K_2$. A natural generalization from 2020 to 2022 explored whether the maximum degree bound holds for induced subgraphs of other graph products.

Hou, Tang, Wang, and Dong (2021) investigated Cartesian products and semistrong products of signed graphs [cite: 12]. They defined signed products $\Gamma_1 \tilde{\Box} \Gamma_2$ and $\Gamma_1 \tilde{\bowtie} \Gamma_2$ for a connected signed bipartite graph $\Gamma_1$ and a connected signed graph $\Gamma_2$. They proved that if $\Gamma_1$ and $\Gamma_2$ possess exactly two distinct adjacency eigenvalues $\pm \theta_1$ and $\pm \theta_2$, respectively, then every induced subgraph on $\frac{1}{2}mn + 1$ vertices of their signed Cartesian product has a maximum degree of at least $\theta_1^2 + \theta_2^2$ (and $(\theta_1^2 + 1)\theta_2^2$ for the semistrong product) [cite: 12]. This provided a massive structural generalization of Huang's theorem, applicable to combinations of complete graphs, bipartite graphs $K_{a,b}$, and generic hypercubes [cite: 9, 12].

Additionally, researchers generalized the Cohen-Tits 2-fold cover of the hypercube by replacing the $K_2$ components with directed $l$-cycles. Using a $q$-analogue of Huang's "magic" matrix, they constructed infinite families of 4-cycle-free $p$-fold covers of the Cartesian product of $p$-cycles as Cayley graphs for extraspecial $p$-groups [cite: 6, 30]. Applying Huang’s interlacing method to these covers yielded new lower bounds on the maximum degree of induced subgraphs of Cartesian powers of directed cycles, strictly improving upon previous bounds established by Tikaradze [cite: 6, 30].

### 4.4 Infinite Families of Regular Graphs and Bilu-Linial Expanders
Another theoretical ramification was identified in the context of regular graphs and expanders. Cioaba (2019) noted that Huang's methodology could iteratively produce infinite families of regular graphs and signed adjacency matrices attaining strict equality in spectral bounds [cite: 31]. If $G$ is a $k$-regular graph with a signed adjacency matrix having a spectral radius of $\sqrt{k}$, one can construct a $(k+1)$-regular graph $H$ by taking two disjoint copies of $G$, adding a perfect matching between them, and assigning signs using the block matrix structure $\begin{pmatrix} A & I \\ I & -A \end{pmatrix}$ [cite: 31]. 

This directly interfaced with the Bilu-Linial conjecture (2006), which proposed constructing infinite families of Ramanujan graphs (optimal expander graphs) through a sequence of 2-fold covers by carefully signing the adjacency matrices to maintain a small spectral radius [cite: 6]. The signed graph techniques popularized by Huang provided fresh, explicit algebraic mechanisms (via Clifford and extraspecial $p$-group representations) to bound the eigenvalues of these covers, reinvigorating efforts in deterministic expander graph construction [cite: 6, 32].

## 5. Nonlinear Generalizations: Hypergraphs and Continuous Spectra (2021-2024)

As matrix-based linear algebra was fully exploited to extend Huang's method on graphs, mathematicians began exploring whether the technique could cross into nonlinear spectral theory, specifically for hypergraphs. A hypergraph generalizes a graph by allowing an edge to connect any number of vertices. Consequently, the adjacency structure is represented by a tensor rather than a matrix [cite: 33]. 

A major obstacle was the lack of a Cauchy Interlacing Theorem for tensors [cite: 33]. In 2021, researchers overcame this by developing a systematic framework linking discrete min-max problems to their continuous multilinear extensions (such as the Lovász extension). They defined a Lovász $p$-Laplacian eigenvalue problem for hypergraphs, which allowed for nonlinear analogs of spectral graph theory [cite: 33]. 

Through this continuous extension, researchers derived the first nonlinear generalization of Huang's method [cite: 33]. By evaluating the $m$-th min-max and max-min eigenvalues of pairs of $p$-homogeneous functions, they established inertia bounds for independence numbers in hypergraphs [cite: 33]. This resulted in an abstract formulation where the maximum degrees of induced sub-hypergraphs could be constrained, bypassing the traditional matrix eigenvalue interlacing entirely, and opening applications in chemical hypergraphs and critical point theory [cite: 33].

## 6. Graph Frustration Index and the Erdős 4-Cycle Free Subgraph Problem

Huang's specific signing of the hypercube required that the product of the signs on every 4-cycle in $Q_n$ evaluate to $-1$. The set of negative edges in such a signing is called a *signature*. Because the symmetric difference of any two valid signatures is an edge cut, there are exactly $2^{2^n-1}$ such valid signatures on $Q_n$ [cite: 7, 17]. 

By 2026, a major question in the study of signed graphs—known as finding the **frustration index**—was applied to this space of signatures. The frustration index asks for the minimum size (fewest negative edges) among all valid signatures that satisfy Huang's 4-cycle condition [cite: 7, 17]. Researchers successfully provided upper and lower bounds for the frustration index of $Q_n$, proving that the bounds perfectly match when $n$ is a power of 4 [cite: 7, 17]. 

Fascinatingly, this investigation forged a bridge to an old, unresolved question by Paul Erdős regarding the maximum number of edges in a largest 4-cycle-free subgraph of the hypercube [cite: 7, 17]. The minimal signature size in Huang's framework is mathematically dual to the maximal edge retention in Erdős's framework. Simultaneously, this spectral data was used to establish separations between degree and adversary lower bounds in quantum query complexity using Ambainis functions [cite: 7, 17], demonstrating a cyclical return of Huang's graph-theoretic innovations back into the heart of computational complexity.

## 7. Conclusion

Hao Huang’s 2019 proof of the Sensitivity Conjecture is a paradigmatic example of how a succinct, elegant mathematical solution can instantly shatter the boundaries of its original discipline. Between 2019 and 2026, the signed-graph polynomial method transcended its initial purpose—bounding the sensitivity of Boolean functions—and blossomed into a vast algebraic and combinatorial framework.

In theoretical computer science, the method catalyzed the resolution of analogous sensitivity conjectures for the symmetric group, perfect matchings, and $m$-ary alphabets, unifying complexity measures across discrete domains. In algebra, it revealed deep intersections between hypercube graph theory, Clifford algebras, exterior algebras, and twisted convolutions. In pure combinatorics, it proved instrumental in resolving the 20-year-old Pachter-Kim conjecture on forcing numbers of perfect matchings, drove the classification of two-eigenvalue graph covers, and contributed to the Bilu-Linial expander construction program. Finally, by transitioning into tensor analysis and $p$-Laplacians, the core philosophy of Huang's interlacing method has found a permanent home in nonlinear spectral theory. 

The trajectory of this research from 2020 to 2026 illustrates a profound synthesis of discrete mathematics and spectral algebra, ensuring that Huang’s methodology will remain a foundational tool in discrete analysis for decades to come.
