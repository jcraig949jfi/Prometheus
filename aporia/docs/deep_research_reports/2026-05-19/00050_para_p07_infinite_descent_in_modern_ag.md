# Modern Applications of Infinite-Descent Techniques in Algebraic Geometry and Arithmetic (2024–2026)

**Key Points:**

*   **Faltings Height and Colmez Conjecture:** Faltings's classical descent under isogeny has spurred modern investigations into the arithmetic complexity of abelian varieties. *   **Vojta's Conjecture Progress:** Formidable advances have occurred between 2024 and 2026 in Vojta's conjecture, which bridges Diophantine approximation with Nevanlinna theory. **Layman Summary: Understanding Infinite Descent Today**
The concept of "infinite descent" sounds like a paradox, but it is one of the most brilliant proof strategies in mathematical history. First conceptualized in the 17th century, the technique operates on a simple premise: if you assume a hypothetical solution exists for a given mathematical equation, and you can use that solution to construct an even smaller, simpler solution, you create an infinite loop. Since whole numbers cannot get smaller forever without eventually hitting zero, the original assumption must be false. This method was famously used to prove that certain equations have no whole-number solutions. 

Today, mathematicians are applying this ancient logic to vastly more complex geometric universes. Instead of just looking at regular numbers, researchers study "curves" and "varieties"—shapes defined by algebraic equations. By combining infinite descent with modern tools like "height functions" (which measure how complex a point on a curve is), researchers can put a strict ceiling on the number of rational or integer solutions an equation can have. While the exact mathematics is highly abstract—involving concepts like Arakelov geometry, $p$-adic numbers, and Nevanlinna theory—the underlying philosophy remains the same: we reduce the complexity of mathematical objects step-by-step to understand their fundamental limits. The evidence strongly leans toward the idea that these modern descent frameworks will eventually unlock the remaining mysteries of Diophantine equations, though the complete resolution of grand challenges like Vojta's conjecture remains an ongoing, generational pursuit.

## 1. Introduction: The Foundations of Descent in Arithmetic Geometry

The origins of arithmetic geometry are inextricably linked to the intensive study of Diophantine problems, which seek integer or rational solutions to polynomial equations [cite: 1]. The foundational architecture for this field was significantly expanded upon by the formalization of Pierre de Fermat's method of infinite descent in the 17th century [cite: 1]. Fermat's technique famously proved the absence of non-trivial integer solutions for equations such as $x^4 + y^4 = z^2$ [cite: 1, 2]. By assuming a hypothetical solution and systematically deriving a strictly smaller solution, the method forces an infinite sequence of decreasing positive integers—a logical contradiction that establishes the impossibility of the initial solution [cite: 1, 3]. 

In modern arithmetic geometry, asking for integer solutions to $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$ translates to finding rational points on the projective curve $X^2 + Y^2 = 1$ [cite: 3]. The basic philosophical idea of descent—reducing a problem to minimal cases or proving finiteness by bounding the "size" of solutions—has deeply influenced the study of elliptic curves and higher-dimensional algebraic varieties [cite: 3, 4]. An elliptic curve $E$ over the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ is defined by an equation of the form $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{Q}$ and the discriminant is nonzero [cite: 1]. The rational points on $E$ form an abelian group under a geometric chord-and-tangent addition law [cite: 1, 5]. The celebrated Mordell-Weil theorem establishes that this group, $E(\mathbb{Q})$, is finitely generated [cite: 6]. 

The classical proof of the Mordell-Weil theorem is bipartitely structured, relying fundamentally on an extension of Fermat's method: weak Mordell-Weil (proving the quotient group $E(\mathbb{Q})/mE(\mathbb{Q})$ is finite) and infinite descent via height functions [cite: 6, 7]. Descent in this context resembles division by a scalar $m$ (often $m=2$) in a group of principal homogeneous spaces, translated in modern terms to proving the finiteness of a Galois cohomology group, such as the Selmer group [cite: 6]. The height function acts as a mechanism to measure the arithmetic complexity of points; during the descent, doubling a rational point on an elliptic curve forces its logarithmic height to grow by a factor of roughly four, ensuring that the reverse process of "halving" a point drastically reduces its height until it falls within a finite, bounded foundational set [cite: 7]. 

This classical 2-descent systematically searches for rational points by mapping the curve to related torsors and analyzing their local solvability over $p$-adic fields, allowing mathematicians to bound the rank of the point group [cite: 1, 2]. While this elementary descent relies on the arithmetic of abelian varieties over global fields, modern applications (spanning from 2024 to 2026) have abstracted the concept of descent to an astonishing degree. Contemporary research leverages infinite descent in Arakelov geometry (via Faltings heights), Nevanlinna theory (via Vojta's conjecture), and $p$-adic Hodge theory (via the Chabauty-Kim program) to yield profound bounds on rational points [cite: 6, 8, 9]. Furthermore, descent theory has recently been reinterpreted through the lens of quotient stacks to study primitive integral solutions to generalized Fermat equations of the form $Ax^a + By^b + Cz^c = 0$ (Arango-Piñeros, 2025) [cite: 10]. These developments represent the zenith of the modern Diophantine study, utilizing generalized descents to confront the most intractable conjectures in number theory.

## 2. Faltings-Style Height Descent and Arithmetic Complexity

The transition from classical descent on elliptic curves to descent on higher-genus curves was spearheaded by Gerd Faltings's 1983 proof of the Mordell conjecture (now Faltings's theorem). The Mordell conjecture posits that any smooth projective curve $X$ defined over a number field $K$ with genus $g \geq 2$ contains only a finite number of $K$-rational points [cite: 6, 11]. Faltings's proof is a masterclass in the application of height functions and generalized descent on heights under isogeny, a concept extending early frameworks envisioned by André Weil [cite: 12]. 

### 2.1 Arakelov Geometry and the Definition of the Faltings Height

To formalize the intuitive notion of "size" for an algebraic solution to a Diophantine equation, mathematicians like Northcott and Weil introduced the Weil height machine [cite: 13, 14]. Faltings required a more sophisticated invariant for abelian varieties, leading him to utilize Arakelov geometry [cite: 13, 14]. Arakelov theory provides an approach to arithmetic geometry that explicitly includes the "infinite primes" (archimedean places) by assigning Hermitian metrics to line bundles over arithmetic surfaces [cite: 6, 15]. An Arakelov divisor on a global field is defined as a formal linear combination of finite places (with integer coefficients) and infinite places (with real coefficients) [cite: 6]. 

The **Faltings height** (or modular height) $h_F(A)$ of an abelian variety $A$ over a number field $K$ is an absolute, archimedean invariant [cite: 6, 8]. It is defined via the Arakelov degree of the pushforward of the relative canonical sheaf under the Abel-Jacobi map, providing a deep measure of the arithmetic complexity of $A$ [cite: 8]. Specifically, if $\omega_A$ represents the canonical sheaf of invariant differentials on the Néron model of $A$, the Faltings height incorporates a local sum over the finite places and an archimedean contribution involving a transcendental integral:
\[
h_{Fal}(A) = \frac{1}{[K : \mathbb{Q}]} \log(\#(\omega_A/\alpha \cdot \omega_A)) - \frac{1}{2[K : \mathbb{Q}]} \sum_{\sigma:K \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}} \log \left( \frac{i^d}{(2\pi)^d} \int_{A(\mathbb{C})^\sigma} \alpha^\sigma \wedge \bar{\alpha}^\sigma \right)
\]
This expression demonstrates that the Faltings height is an average of transcendental quantities summed over all embeddings $\sigma$ of the number field $K$ into the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ [cite: 16, 17]. Crucially, the modular height $h_{Fal}(A)$ is independent of the choice of the differential form $\alpha$ and operates as an invariant of the abelian variety over the algebraic closure $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ [cite: 17]. 

### 2.2 Descent under Isogeny and Shafarevich's Conjecture

Faltings's strategy to prove the Mordell conjecture utilized an ingenious reduction developed by Parshin [cite: 11]. Parshin's trick translates the problem of finding rational points on a curve $C$ of genus $g \geq 2$ into the problem of studying families of abelian varieties over $C$ [cite: 11]. This reduced Mordell's conjecture to Shafarevich's conjecture: for a fixed number field $K$, finite set of places $S$, dimension $g$, and polarization degree $d$, there exist only finitely many isomorphism classes of polarized abelian varieties over $K$ with good reduction outside $S$ [cite: 8, 14]. 

To prove Shafarevich's conjecture, Faltings demonstrated that the Arakelov degree of the canonical bundle possesses critical positivity properties [cite: 8]. Because the leading term of the Faltings height satisfies $h_F(A) \approx \frac{1}{g} \deg_{Ar}(\lambda) + O(1)$ and is positive, abelian varieties with bounded Faltings height form a compact subset in the Arakelov metric [cite: 8]. Faltings then deployed a "descent under isogeny" (an extension of the classical infinite descent) by analyzing isogenies between abelian varieties [cite: 12]. Faltings proved that in an isogeny class of abelian varieties, there are only finitely many isomorphism classes of principally polarized abelian varieties [cite: 8]. Combined with the finding that bounded Faltings height implies finiteness, Faltings verified Shafarevich's conjecture, thus indirectly proving the finiteness of rational points on curves of high genus [cite: 8, 14]. 

Despite its historical magnitude, Faltings's theorem is theoretically "ineffective"; while it guarantees that the number of rational points is finite, the descent bounds do not yield an algorithm to compute the exact set of rational points $X(\mathbb{Q})$, motivating the search for effective refinements like those derived from Vojta's conjecture or the Chabauty-Kim method [cite: 8, 11]. 

### 2.3 Refinements of the Colmez Conjecture (2024–2026)

In the decades following Faltings's proof, understanding the Faltings height of specific abelian varieties became a highly active area of research. In 1993, Pierre Colmez formulated a remarkable conjecture relating the Faltings height of an abelian variety $A$ with complex multiplication (CM) to the logarithmic derivatives of Artin L-functions [cite: 16, 18, 19]. The Colmez conjecture states that if an abelian variety $A$ has CM by the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_E$ of a CM field $E$, its Faltings height can be expressed as a linear combination of the sub-leading terms (at $s=0$) of Artin L-functions corresponding to the Galois representations associated with $E$ [cite: 15, 17]. 

While Colmez proved his conjecture for abelian extensions [cite: 17] and subsequent work by Andreatta, Goren, Howard, Madapusi, Yuan, and Zhang proved an averaged version of the conjecture over all CM abelian varieties of a given field [cite: 15, 19, 20], the refined individual behavior of the height remained a target of inquiry. 

In a groundbreaking 2026 paper, Vincent Maillot and Damian Rössler proposed a powerful refinement of the Colmez conjecture [cite: 16, 17]. Noting that the standard Faltings height is an average of transcendental integrals across all embeddings of $K$ into $\mathbb{C}$, Maillot and Rössler investigated whether there is an explicit formula for *each individual term* in this sum [cite: 16, 17]. Their refinement identifies these specific transcendental quantities, suggesting a deep connection to Arakelov theory [cite: 17, 18]. 

Maillot and Rössler (2026) demonstrated that their refined conjecture implies the existence of "fine reciprocity laws" for Siegel modular forms with rational coefficients evaluated at CM points [cite: 17]. Furthermore, using classical analytic number theory results stretching back to Siegel and Hasse concerning elliptic units, Maillot and Rössler proved their refined conjecture unconditionally for the case of CM elliptic curves [cite: 17, 18]. This proof relies on identifying a Siegel modular form—specifically, the discriminant modular form—that satisfies the requisite reciprocity laws and integrality properties with respect to the abelian variety and its CM field [cite: 17, 18]. This 2026 advancement fundamentally refines our understanding of height descent by isolating the arithmetic contributions of individual complex embeddings.

## 3. Progress on Vojta's Conjecture (2024–2026)

While Faltings's proof guarantees the finiteness of rational points on curves of genus $\geq 2$, Vojta's conjecture provides a sweeping, generalized framework that seeks to unify Diophantine geometry and complex analysis [cite: 21]. Formulated by Paul Vojta in 1987, the conjecture builds an intricate analogy between Diophantine approximation (the study of how well real numbers can be approximated by rational numbers) and Nevanlinna theory (value distribution theory for meromorphic functions in complex analysis) [cite: 6, 21]. 

### 3.1 The Framework of Vojta's Conjecture

Vojta's dictionary translates Nevanlinna theory's defect relations (such as the First and Second Main Theorems) into statements about the heights of points on algebraic varieties [cite: 21, 22]. Let $X$ be a smooth projective geometrically integral variety over a number field $K$, and let $D \subset X$ be a normal crossings divisor [cite: 23]. Vojta's conjecture bounds the heights of points relative to a canonical class and the divisor $D$ [cite: 24]. Specifically, it predicts that for a big line bundle $H$, an $S$-integral point $P \in X(K)$ satisfies an inequality where the height relative to the canonical bundle $K_X$ plus $D$ is bounded by a small multiple of the height relative to $H$, modulo an error term [cite: 23]. 

If proven, Vojta's conjecture would immediately imply a vast array of unsolved theorems in arithmetic geometry. It implies the generalized abc-conjecture [cite: 21, 25], which in turn would provide *effective* bounds for Mordell's conjecture (refining Faltings's non-effective finiteness via descent methods akin to Kummer's) [cite: 8]. Furthermore, it implies the Uniformity Conjecture, which states there is a uniform bound on the number of rational points on a curve of genus $g \geq 2$, depending solely on the genus and the field of definition [cite: 6, 14]. The uniform bound on the number of rational points has spurred massive interest, tying into the uniform Mordell-Lang conjecture [cite: 14].

### 3.2 Complex Cases and Campana's Orbifold Conjecture (2024–2026)

One of the most active arenas of research surrounding Vojta's conjecture involves proving its complex-analytic analogues, where the arithmetic heights are replaced by Nevanlinna counting functions for entire holomorphic curves $f: \mathbb{C} \to X$ [cite: 22]. Between 2024 and 2026, mathematician Julie Tzu-Yueh Wang, in collaboration with Ji Guo and Min Ru, made substantial strides in proving Vojta's abc conjecture for algebraic tori and projective spaces [cite: 22, 26, 27]. 

In a 2024–2026 series of papers, Guo, Ji, and Wang proved Vojta's general abc conjecture for the complex projective space $\mathbb{P}^n(\mathbb{C})$, under the stringent geometric assumption that the entire holomorphic curves in $\mathbb{P}^n(\mathbb{C})$ are highly ramified over the coordinate hyperplanes [cite: 22, 28]. This milestone generalized their earlier 2024 findings for the specific case of $n=2$ (the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{C})$) [cite: 22]. The proof machinery relies heavily on establishing a greatest common divisor (GCD) theorem for an analytic map $f: \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{P}^n$ and two homogeneous polynomials, whose coefficients are meromorphic functions matching the growth rate of $f$ [cite: 22]. This translates arithmetic generalized greatest common divisors into Nevanlinna-theoretic defect inequalities [cite: 22]. 

By successfully establishing a truncated Second Main Theorem of level one with explicit exceptional sets for these analytic maps [cite: 22, 27], Ru, Wang, and Guo resolved critical cases of **Campana's orbifold conjecture** for $\mathbb{P}^n$ [cite: 27, 28]. Campana's conjecture posits that special orbifolds (spaces with fractional boundary divisors) dictate the distribution of entire curves and rational points. The 2026 work extends these results to projective toric varieties, confirming that finite ramified coverings of projective toric varieties with three components admitting sufficiently large multiplicities obey Campana's hyperbolicity constraints [cite: 22, 27, 28]. 

### 3.3 Vojta's Conjecture on Weighted Projective Varieties (2023–2025)

Simultaneously, arithmetic geometers Sajad Salami and Tony Shaska have expanded the structural scope of Vojta's conjecture by formally defining it over weighted projective spaces and weighted varieties [cite: 29, 30, 31]. In a manuscript accepted for publication in the *European Journal of Mathematics* in 2025, Salami and Shaska introduced a robust theory of local and global weighted heights for smooth weighted projective varieties [cite: 29, 31, 32]. 

A weighted projective space $\mathbb{P}(w_0, \dots, w_n)$ generalizes standard projective space by assigning integer weights $w_i$ to the coordinates, naturally yielding spaces with cyclic quotient singularities [cite: 30, 33]. To bypass the classical approach of using the Veronese map to study rational points on weighted spaces, Salami and Shaska generalized the concept of logarithmic greatest common divisors to **generalized logarithmic weighted greatest common divisors** ($\log \mathrm{h}_{wgcd}$) [cite: 29, 31, 32]. They elegantly expressed these weighted GCDs as the height of the weighted projective space blown up relative to an exceptional divisor [cite: 29, 32].

Salami and Shaska formulated three highly technical versions of Vojta's conjecture [cite: 29, 32]:
1.  Vojta's conjecture for smooth weighted projective varieties.
2.  Vojta's conjecture using weighted multiplier ideal sheaves (to account for singularities).
3.  Vojta's conjecture for weighted log pairs.

In their 2025 paper, they proved that all three formulations of the conjecture are strictly equivalent [cite: 29, 32]. Furthermore, they demonstrated that assuming Vojta's conjecture for weighted projective varieties allows one to strictly bound the $\log \mathrm{h}_{wgcd}$ for any subvariety of codimension $\geq 2$ and a finite set of places $S$ [cite: 29, 32]. This establishes that the deep Diophantine bounds predicted by Vojta persist even when the ambient geometry is intrinsically weighted and stack-like, a significant theoretical bridge between birational geometry and arithmetic height descent [cite: 31, 32].

## 4. Modern Rational-Point Bounds via Descent (2024–2026)

Determining the exact set of rational points $X(\mathbb{Q})$ on a curve is a profoundly difficult problem because Faltings's theorem is ineffective. Consequently, mathematicians rely on explicit descent methods—ranging from classical 2-descent to non-abelian $p$-adic Chabauty methods—to extract absolute upper bounds on the number of rational points [cite: 11, 34].

### 4.1 $S$-Integral Points and 2-Descent over Function Fields (2025)

A major triumph in establishing precise bounds on points of bounded height was published in October 2025 by Jean Gillibert, Emmanuel Hallouin, and Aaron Levin [cite: 35, 36, 37]. Their work shifts the focus from number fields to function fields of characteristic $\neq 2$, specifically looking at elliptic and hyperelliptic curves over $k(B)$, where $B$ is a smooth projective curve [cite: 35, 38]. 

By performing explicit 2-descent techniques over function fields and combining them with powerful geometric tools like Riemann-Roch and Bézout's theorems, Gillibert et al. (2025) achieved a breakthrough upper bound on the number of rational points of bounded height [cite: 35, 36, 37, 38]. The height of a point over a function field is simply its degree as a rational map to $\mathbb{P}^1$ [cite: 38]. The 2-descent methodology maps the curve to principal homogeneous spaces, tracking the local solubility and ranks of the associated Lang-Néron group [cite: 38]. 

One of the primary corollaries of their research is the derivation of a stringent upper bound on the number of **$S$-integral points** on these curves, where $S \subset B$ is a finite set of places and the ring of $S$-integers $R_S$ consists of functions with no poles outside $S$ [cite: 36, 38]. The theorem significantly improves classical bounds set by Lang (1960) and Voloch (1990) [cite: 36]. Specifically, for a hyperelliptic curve $y^2 = f(x)$, Gillibert's bounds account for the height of the polynomial $f$, the genus of the base curve $g_B$, the size of the set $S$, and the rank of the elliptic curve over $k(B)$ [cite: 38]. 

Moreover, this geometric 2-descent has remarkable applications to the arithmetic of finite fields. Gillibert, Hallouin, and Levin (2025) utilized their $S$-integral point bounds to establish upper bounds on the $p$-torsion subgroups of Jacobians over small finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$ [cite: 35, 36]. By relating the 3-torsion points of Jacobians of hyperelliptic curves (and 2-torsion of trigonal curves) to integral points on specific elliptic curves, they bounded the 3-torsion group $|\text{Jac}(X)(\mathbb{F}_q)[cite: 9]|$ by a factor of roughly $3^{2g}$ when $q < 81$ [cite: 35, 36]. This explicitly improves upon the naive geometric inequalities stemming from the classical Weil bounds and surpasses recent 2-torsion bounds established by Manjul Bhargava et al. [cite: 35]. 

### 4.2 Uniform Bounds and Hurwitz Spaces

The quest for uniform bounds on rational points has seen parallel developments in bounding degree and dimension growth. A fundamental result in Diophantine geometry, known as Northcott's theorem, asserts the finiteness of points of bounded height and bounded degree [cite: 1, 14]. Faltings's theorem assures finiteness, but establishing bounds depending *only* on the degree and genus (the Uniformity Conjecture) requires deeper mechanics [cite: 6, 14]. 

Recent literature (e.g., Novikov, 2023) bounds the number of rational points of height $\leq H$ on irreducible algebraic plane curves of degree $d$ with optimal dependence on $d$, giving bounds such as $C d^2 H^{2/d} (\log H)^\kappa$ [cite: 39]. For algebraic curves over function fields, tracking the distribution of rational points often involves mapping the covers of the curves to Hurwitz spaces $\mathcal{H}_{G,n,X}$ [cite: 40]. Through the Lefschetz trace formula, the arithmetic problem of counting covers with fixed Galois groups and bounded discriminants is translated into controlling the cohomology of these Hurwitz spaces, demonstrating that geometric descent seamlessly connects the algebraic geometry of moduli spaces to the counting of rational points [cite: 40].

### 4.3 The Chabauty-Kim Program and Quadratic Chabauty

When classical descent and the Mordell-Weil sieve are insufficient to identify the exact set of rational points $X(\mathbb{Q})$, arithmetic geometers turn to $p$-adic methods. The classical method of Chabauty and Coleman successfully bounds (and often exactly computes) the set of rational points $X(\mathbb{Q})$ inside the $p$-adic points $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ provided that the rank $r$ of the Jacobian $J(\mathbb{Q})$ is strictly less than the genus $g$ of the curve ($r < g$) [cite: 11, 34]. The technique relies on finding $p$-adic integrals of holomorphic 1-forms that vanish on the closure of $J(\mathbb{Q})$ in $J(\mathbb{Q}_p)$, and subsequently restricting these analytic functions to the curve $X$ [cite: 11, 41].

However, when $r \geq g$, the traditional Chabauty-Coleman method breaks down because the closure of $J(\mathbb{Q})$ may be open in $J(\mathbb{Q}_p)$, leaving no non-trivial differential forms to integrate [cite: 11, 34]. To circumvent this, Minhyong Kim introduced a vast non-abelian generalization known as the **Chabauty-Kim method** [cite: 41, 42]. Rather than operating on the abelian Jacobian, Kim's program utilizes the unipotent $\mathbb{Q}_p$-étale fundamental group $\pi_1^{et, \mathbb{Q}_p}(X, b)$, a pro-algebraic group that captures deeper non-abelian Galois cohomology [cite: 34]. 

The Chabauty-Kim method defines a nested sequence of nested analytic loci:
\[ X(\mathbb{Q}_p) \supset X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_1 \supset X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_2 \supset \dots \supset X(\mathbb{Q}) \]
where the first level $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_1$ coincides with the classical Chabauty-Coleman set [cite: 34, 42]. The higher levels $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_n$ are defined by the vanishing of multiple Coleman analytic functions [cite: 43]. By pushing the descent to non-abelian Selmer varieties associated with Bloch-Kato Selmer groups, the method aims to prove that $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_n$ is finite even when the Mordell-Weil rank $r \geq g$ [cite: 34, 42]. "Kim's Conjecture" asserts that for $n$ sufficiently large, $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_n = X(\mathbb{Q})$ exactly [cite: 43, 44].

#### 4.3.1 Explicit Quadratic Chabauty (2024–2025)

The level $n=2$ of Kim's descent hierarchy is known as **Quadratic Chabauty** [cite: 42, 45]. If the rank of the Jacobian satisfies $r < g + \rho(J) - 1$, where $\rho(J)$ is the rank of the Néron-Severi group of $J$ (the Picard number), Quadratic Chabauty guarantees the finiteness of $X(\mathbb{Q}_p)_2$ [cite: 42, 45]. 

The computational power of Quadratic Chabauty is derived from the theory of $p$-adic heights formulated by Nekovář [cite: 45, 46]. By associating to points $x \in X(\mathbb{Q})$ a mixed extension of $p$-adic Galois representations parameterized by a correspondence $Z$ (often a Hecke correspondence on a modular curve), one can define a global $p$-adic height function [cite: 44, 45]. This height function decomposes into a sum of local $p$-adic heights $h_v$ across all places $v$ [cite: 44, 46]. Because the local heights away from $p$ take only a finite number of values on $X(\mathbb{Q}_v)$, the difference between the global $p$-adic height and the local height at $p$ restricts the rational points to a finite set of $p$-adic zeroes [cite: 45, 46]. 

This method saw a spectacular triumph in the exact computation of rational points on the "cursed" modular curve $X_s(13)$, a curve of genus $3$ with Mordell-Weil rank $r=3$, thus immune to classical Chabauty [cite: 44, 45]. Balakrishnan, Dogra, Müller, Tuitman, and Vonk explicitly computed the $p$-adic heights using Quadratic Chabauty to prove that $X_s(13)(\mathbb{Q})$ contains exactly 7 points (six CM points and one cusp) [cite: 44, 45]. This result crucially completed the classification of non-CM elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ with split Cartan level structures, finishing a classification problem pioneered by Bilu and Parent [cite: 45].

Between 2024 and 2025, the Chabauty-Kim method has been heavily debated and refined in arithmetic geometry workshops [cite: 41, 47]. One major obstacle of current Quadratic Chabauty is the requirement of an explicit affine plane model of the curve [cite: 47]. Research presented at the London Junior Number Theory Seminar (2024/2025) highlights efforts by Chen, Kedlaya, and Lau to develop "model-free" Quadratic Chabauty methods leveraging $q$-expansions of modular forms [cite: 47]. Additionally, discussions at the "Rational Points 2025" workshop in Bayreuth focused on utilizing the Lawrence-Venkatesh method and $p$-adic Hodge theory to verify the Selmer Section Conjecture by exactly computing the Chabauty-Kim loci for a density 1 set of primes $p$ [cite: 41, 43].

## 5. Conclusion

The technique of infinite descent, originating as an elegant trick by Fermat to dismantle isolated Diophantine equations, has transmuted into the overarching architecture of modern arithmetic geometry. As evidenced by the surge of progress from 2024 to 2026, the application of descent principles across different mathematical strata continues to yield profound results.

At the level of heights and Arakelov geometry, Faltings's legacy of isogeny descent is being sharpened by Maillot and Rössler (2026), whose refinement of the Colmez conjecture elegantly bridges the Faltings height of CM abelian varieties to Siegel modular forms and reciprocity laws [cite: 17, 18]. At the intersection of complex analysis and Diophantine approximation, Vojta's conjecture has witnessed a flurry of definitive proofs in complex cases [cite: 22, 28]. The 2026 work of Guo, Ji, and Wang establishing Vojta's abc conjecture for toric varieties ramified over boundary divisors, alongside Salami and Shaska's (2025) rigorous formulation of the conjecture for weighted projective varieties, demonstrates that the Nevanlinna-Diophantine dictionary remains one of the most predictive analogies in mathematics [cite: 22, 28, 29, 32]. 

Simultaneously, the pursuit of explicit, computable rational point bounds has advanced significantly. The classical 2-descent, revitalized by Gillibert, Hallouin, and Levin (2025), has successfully bounded $S$-integral points on function fields, establishing strict geometric limits on torsion subgroups for Jacobians over finite fields [cite: 35, 36]. Furthermore, the non-abelian frontier spearheaded by the Chabauty-Kim program continues to bypass the genus-rank barriers of classical methods, utilizing $p$-adic heights and Selmer varieties to isolate rational points on notoriously difficult modular curves [cite: 44, 45, 46]. 

The synthesis of these domains—geometric height bounds, Arakelov intersection theory, non-abelian Galois cohomology, and Nevanlinna theory—indicates that infinite descent is not merely a tool for contradiction, but the foundational grammar through which the geometry of rational points is articulated and universally constrained.
