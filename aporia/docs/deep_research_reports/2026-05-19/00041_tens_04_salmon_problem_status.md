# Comprehensive Report on the Status of the Salmon Problem in Algebraic Geometry and Algebraic Statistics

**Key Points:**
*   **The Problem:** The Salmon problem asks for the explicit generation of the defining ideal for the secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ in the space of $4 \times 4 \times 4$ tensors. This algebraic variety corresponds to the statistical general Markov model for evolution on a 3-leaf tree with a hidden ancestor [cite: 1]. 
*   **Current Status:** While the *set-theoretic* version of the Salmon problem was resolved between 2010 and 2012 (proving that the variety is cut out as a set by polynomials of degrees 5, 6, and 9), the *ideal-theoretic* version (identifying the minimal generators of the ideal) remains an open conjecture [cite: 1, 2, 3]. 
*   **Recent 2024-2026 Progress:** Substantial theoretical breakthroughs have occurred recently. In 2026, researchers introduced "concise secant varieties" and "Kronecker-Koszul flattenings," providing new modular partial desingularizations and determinantal equations that differentiate secant varieties from cactus varieties [cite: 4, 5]. These tools map out a strategic pathway toward finally resolving the ideal-theoretic Salmon conjecture.
*   **Significance:** Solving the ideal-theoretic Salmon problem would supply all phylogenetic invariants for a broad class of binary evolutionary tree models [cite: 1, 2], having profound implications for computational biology, phylogenetics, and complexity theory. 

The Salmon problem sits at the intersection of algebraic geometry, representation theory, and algebraic statistics. Originating as a challenge with a culinary prize, it has driven over a decade of intense research into the geometry of higher-order tensors. The evidence leans toward the revised Salmon conjecture being true—that the ideal is generated by equations of degrees 5, 6, and 9—but proving this rigorously without relying entirely on overwhelming numerical approximations remains a monumental mathematical hurdle. Recent theoretical constructions from 2024 to 2026, shifting focus from raw computation to the geometric structure of "minimal border rank tensors," suggest that a complete resolution may be on the horizon.

## 1. Introduction and Historical Context

The study of secant varieties of Segre embeddings is a classical subject in algebraic geometry that has experienced a massive resurgence due to its applications in algebraic statistics, quantum information theory, computational complexity (such as the complexity of matrix multiplication), and signal processing [cite: 1, 5, 6]. In algebraic statistics, these varieties correspond to hidden Markov models and mixture models widely used in phylogenetics—the study of evolutionary relationships among species [cite: 1, 2].

In 2007, during a workshop at the Institute for Mathematics and its Applications, mathematician Elizabeth S. Allman posed a highly specific and computationally daunting challenge: to determine the defining ideal of the fourth secant variety of the Segre product of three projective 3-spaces, denoted as $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ [cite: 7, 8]. Because this variety parameterizes the general Markov model for a tree with three leaves (each representing 4 DNA nucleotide states: A, C, G, T) connected to a single hidden ancestral node, determining its polynomial invariants is the critical step to unlocking the invariants for any binary evolutionary tree with any number of states [cite: 1, 2].

To incentivize the community, Allman offered a prize of a self-caught, smoked Alaskan Copper River salmon to anyone who could solve the problem [cite: 3, 9]. Consequently, the challenge was dubbed the **"Salmon Problem,"** and the hypothesized list of generators for the ideal became known as the **"Salmon Conjecture."** 

By 2010–2012, the *set-theoretic* version of the Salmon problem was successfully resolved. Shmuel Friedland mathematically proved that the variety is cut out set-theoretically by polynomials of degrees 5, 9, and 16, and subsequently refined this with Elizabeth Gross to degrees 5, 6, and 9 [cite: 7]. Concurrently, Daniel J. Bates and Luke Oeding used Numerical Algebraic Geometry to furnish a computational solution showing that equations of degrees 5, 6, and 9 suffice [cite: 2, 3]. Friedland was awarded the salmon, which he famously ate [cite: 9]. However, the *ideal-theoretic* version—proving that these polynomials generate the entire prime ideal of the variety—remains one of the most prominent open problems in algebraic statistics [cite: 2, 10].

## 2. Mathematical Formulation

To fully grasp the magnitude of the Salmon problem, one must construct the mathematical framework surrounding tensor rank, Segre embeddings, and secant varieties.

### 2.1 Tensors and the Segre Embedding
Let $A, B$, and $C$ be complex vector spaces of dimensions $a, b$, and $c$, respectively. In the context of the Salmon problem, we consider $a = b = c = 4$, so the vector spaces are isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}^4$, representing the four nucleotide bases [cite: 1]. A tensor $T \in A \otimes B \otimes C$ is a 3-dimensional array of complex numbers (a hypermatrix) [cite: 11]. 

A tensor is said to have **tensor rank 1** if it can be written as an outer product of vectors: $T = u \otimes v \otimes w$ for $u \in A, v \in B, w \in C$ [cite: 11]. The projectivization of the set of all rank-1 tensors forms a smooth projective variety known as the **Segre variety**. The Segre embedding is defined as:
\[ Seg: \mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C \to \mathbb{P}(A \otimes B \otimes C) \]
\[ ([a], [b], [c]) \mapsto [a \otimes b \otimes c] \]
For the Salmon problem, the Segre variety is $\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \subset \mathbb{P}^{63}$ [cite: 2, 12]. 

### 2.2 Secant Varieties and Border Rank
The tensor rank of a general tensor $T$ is the minimum integer $r$ such that $T$ can be expressed as the sum of $r$ rank-1 tensors. However, the set of tensors of rank $\le r$ is not necessarily closed in the Zariski topology (or the standard Euclidean topology). The closure of this set is the $r$-th **secant variety**, denoted $\sigma_r(Seg(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C))$, or simply $\sigma_r(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C)$ [cite: 2]. 

A tensor $T$ has **border rank** $r$ if it lies in $\sigma_r$ but not in $\sigma_{r-1}$. Equivalently, $T$ has border rank $r$ if it can be written as the limit of a sequence of tensors of rank $r$ [cite: 13, 14]. The Salmon problem explicitly asks for the generators of the defining ideal $\mathcal{I}(\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3))$ [cite: 12]. Stated differently, it seeks the explicit polynomial conditions that a $4 \times 4 \times 4$ tensor must satisfy to have border rank at most 4.

### 2.3 Flattenings and Subspace Varieties
An elementary tool for finding equations of secant varieties is the process of **flattening** [cite: 12, 15]. A 3-tensor in $A \otimes B \otimes C$ can be viewed as a 2-tensor (a matrix) by grouping the spaces, for instance, $A \otimes (B \otimes C)$. This yields a flattening matrix of size $a \times bc$. If a tensor is a sum of $r$ rank-1 tensors, its flattening matrix can have rank at most $r$. Therefore, the $(r+1) \times (r+1)$ minors of the flattening matrices provide polynomials in the ideal of $\sigma_r$ [cite: 15].

However, for $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$, the flattening matrices are of size $4 \times 16$. The condition that these matrices have rank at most 4 dictates that all $5 \times 5$ minors must vanish. But since the matrices only have 4 rows, there are no $5 \times 5$ minors! Consequently, standard flattenings yield absolutely zero information for this specific variety [cite: 6, 12]. This failure of basic linear algebraic tools is exactly what makes the Salmon problem so difficult. 

Because standard flattenings fail, one must rely on subspace varieties. The subspace variety $Sub_{a',b',c'}(A \otimes B \otimes C)$ is the set of tensors that can be restricted to a subspace of dimensions $a' \times b' \times c'$ [cite: 2, 16]. It is a known fact that $Sub_{r,r,r}(A \otimes B \otimes C) \supseteq \sigma_r(\mathbb{P}A \times \mathbb{P}B \times \mathbb{P}C)$ [cite: 2].

## 3. The Salmon Conjecture and Set-Theoretic Solutions

Since the ideal is not generated by flattenings, advanced techniques from representation theory and invariant theory are required. 

### 3.1 Representation Theory and Schur Modules
The symmetry group of the variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ is $G = (GL(A) \times GL(B) \times GL(C)) \rtimes S_3$ (where $S_3$ permutes the three identical vector spaces) [cite: 2]. Because the variety is $G$-invariant, its defining ideal is also $G$-invariant [cite: 2]. This allows the ideal to be decomposed into a direct sum of irreducible $G$-modules (Schur modules) corresponding to integer partitions [cite: 1, 2]. By applying Schur functors and searching for highest weight vectors that vanish on the secant variety, one can systematically hunt for polynomials in the ideal [cite: 1, 2].

### 3.2 The Landsberg-Manivel-Friedland Reduction
A critical breakthrough in solving the set-theoretic problem was a dimensional reduction. J.M. Landsberg and L. Manivel proved (with a later correction by S. Friedland) that for $a, b, c \ge 3$, the secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^{a-1} \times \mathbb{P}^{b-1} \times \mathbb{P}^{c-1})$ is cut out set-theoretically by the equations of $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ combined with the $(5 \times 5)$ minors of the standard flattenings [cite: 2]. Because the Salmon problem is in $\mathbb{C}^4 \otimes \mathbb{C}^4 \otimes \mathbb{C}^4$, and flattenings trivially vanish on $4 \times 16$ matrices, the problem reduces strictly to finding the equations of $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ embedded in the larger space [cite: 1, 2]. 

### 3.3 The Defining Equations: Degrees 5, 6, and 9
Through intense symbolic computation and theoretical derivation, researchers identified three distinct modules of polynomials that vanish on the variety [cite: 2, 3]:

1.  **Degree 5 Equations (Strassen's Commutative Conditions):** Strassen discovered that certain polynomials of degree 5 vanish on border rank 4 tensors. In the context of the Salmon problem, there is a module of degree 5 polynomials spanning a 1728-dimensional space [cite: 2, 3]. These quintic equations involve 180 terms each [cite: 1, 17]. Originally, the Salmon conjecture suggested that degree 5 and degree 9 equations might be enough to generate the ideal [cite: 7].
2.  **Degree 6 Equations (Landsberg-Manivel Polynomials):** Landsberg and Manivel found polynomials of degree 6 that vanish on the variety. Crucially, numerical testing by Bates and Oeding proved that these degree 6 equations are *not* contained in the ideal generated by the degree 5 equations [cite: 2, 16]. Therefore, they are non-trivial minimal generators [cite: 2, 3, 16]. There are 10 linearly independent degree 6 polynomials in a specific Schur module, which blow up to 1000 equations under the group action [cite: 2, 3]. 
3.  **Degree 9 Equations (Symmetrization Conditions):** Originating from Strassen's observation that certain $3 \times 3 \times 3$ tensors form a hypersurface of degree 9, these conditions correspond to the vanishing of $9 \times 9$ minors of auxiliary $12 \times 9$ matrices [cite: 7, 18]. This generates a massive space of 8000 equations in degree 9 [cite: 2, 3]. 

### 3.4 Resolving the Set-Theoretic Problem
In 2010, Bates and Oeding used Numerical Algebraic Geometry—specifically utilizing the software Bertini with homotopy continuation—to evaluate the zero-set of the degree 5, 6, and 9 equations [cite: 1, 3, 13]. Their computations proved that, with extremely high probability, the zero-set of these polynomials has precisely two irreducible components: the desired secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ (dimension 31, degree 345) and an extraneous subspace variety $Sub_{3,3,3}$ (dimension 29, degree 84) [cite: 2, 3, 16]. When embedded into the $4 \times 4 \times 4$ space, this provided the set-theoretic solution [cite: 1, 3].

Shortly after the numerical results were circulated, Shmuel Friedland and Elizabeth Gross constructed a rigorous, computer-free proof confirming that the irreducible variety of $4 \times 4 \times 4$ tensors of border rank at most 4 is exactly the zero set of these polynomial equations of degrees 5, 6, and 9 [cite: 7]. 

### 3.5 The Ideal-Theoretic Conjecture
Because the degree 6 equations are not in the ideal generated by the degree 5 equations, Friedland's set-theoretic generators cannot form a set of minimal ideal generators [cite: 2]. Bates and Oeding found no obstructions to the set-theoretic result holding ideal-theoretically, leading to the **Revised Salmon Conjecture**: *The defining ideal of $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ is minimally generated by the specific modules of polynomials in degrees 5, 6, and 9* [cite: 2, 3, 7].

Proving this conjecture requires showing that the ideal generated by these polynomials is radical and prime, a feat that has remained computationally intractable due to the massive number of variables (64) and the extreme complexity of computing Gröbner bases for high-degree invariants in such a large space [cite: 19].

## 4. Relevance to Phylogenetics and Algebraic Statistics

To understand why a problem in pure algebraic geometry carried a salmon reward, one must look at algebraic statistics. 

In phylogenetics, DNA sequences of extant species are analyzed to infer their evolutionary history [cite: 1, 9]. The **General Markov Model** of sequence mutation allows for any transition probabilities between the states A, C, G, and T [cite: 18, 20]. If we consider a "tripod" tree (a star tree with one internal root node and three observed leaf nodes), the joint probability distribution of observing specific nucleotide combinations at the leaves can be modeled as a $4 \times 4 \times 4$ tensor [cite: 10]. 

Because the internal ancestral node is hidden, we must sum over its 4 possible states. This summation mathematically corresponds to expressing the probability tensor as a sum of 4 rank-1 tensors [cite: 1]. Thus, the probability distributions generated by this model form exactly the secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ [cite: 1]. The polynomial equations that vanish on this variety are **phylogenetic invariants** [cite: 10, 20]. If biologists can evaluate these polynomials on empirical DNA data, they can statistically determine whether the data fits the given evolutionary tree [cite: 21]. Furthermore, a foundational theorem by Allman and Rhodes states that finding the polynomial invariants for this specific 3-leaf model provides *all* polynomial invariants for any binary evolutionary tree of any size [cite: 2].

## 5. Recent Computational Attacks and Breakthroughs (2024–2026)

From 2012 to 2023, progress on the ideal-theoretic Salmon problem was mostly stagnant due to the hard limits of computational algebra. Standard Gröbner basis algorithms fail spectacularly on 64 variables [cite: 19]. However, the period between 2024 and 2026 has witnessed an explosion of innovative theoretical frameworks designed to bypass brute-force computation.

### 5.1 Multi-graded Implicitization (2023-2024)
In late 2023, researchers developed new algorithms for computing the implicitization of multi-graded polynomial maps, explicitly targeting problems like the Salmon Conjecture [cite: 19]. By computing matrices of maximal rank for which the parameterization map has a positive multigrading, researchers were able to quickly find minimal quadrics and cubics for related models (like the Kimura 3-Parameter model on phylogenetic networks), drastically reducing computation times from over 100 days to mere minutes [cite: 19]. While this method has not yet scaled to completely resolve the degree 5, 6, and 9 generators of the Salmon ideal over fraction fields, it represents a significant leap in computational capability [cite: 19].

### 5.2 Phylogenetic Invariants for Equivariant Models (2024-2025)
A major theme in 2024-2025 has been translating the invariants of the general Markov model to more specific biological models, such as the Kimura 2-parameter (K80) and Jukes-Cantor (JC69) models [cite: 10, 22, 23]. Casanellas and colleagues published fundamental papers establishing that the algebraic variety corresponding to an equivariant model on a phylogenetic tree is an irreducible component of the general Markov variety intersected with the linear space defined by the model [cite: 20, 22]. 

In these recent works, the authors explicitly note the bottleneck caused by the Salmon problem: *"It is worth noting that even for the tripod, a generating set for the ideal $\mathcal{I}(V_T)$ remains unknown (the problem of giving a set of generators is known as the Salmon conjecture), so we could not have performed the computations from the general Markov model directly"* [cite: 10, 22, 23]. This highlights that in 2024 and 2025, the ideal-theoretic Salmon problem is officially recognized as unsolved, and its absence continues to impede practical phylogenetics. 

### 5.3 Classification of Minimal Border Rank Tensors (2024-2025)
A major conceptual shift has been moving away from studying the entire secant variety toward isolating **minimal border rank tensors**. A tensor $T \in \mathbb{C}^m \otimes \mathbb{C}^m \otimes \mathbb{C}^m$ has minimal border rank if its border rank equals $m$ and it is concise (it does not live in any smaller dimensional subspace) [cite: 24]. 

In a pair of highly influential papers in 2024 and 2025, Jakub Jagiella and Joachim Jelisiejew provided a complete classification of $1^*$-generic minimal border rank tensors for $m \le 5$ [cite: 24]. They proved that there are exactly 107 isomorphism classes of such tensors [cite: 24]. Since any tensor of border rank $\le m$ can be viewed as a restriction of a minimal border rank tensor, this classification drastically reduces the complexity of secant varieties by mapping out the exact geometric degenerations that can occur [cite: 24].

### 5.4 Kronecker-Koszul Flattenings (Feb 2026)
In February 2026, Dolezalek and Michalek introduced a highly advanced family of flattening methods called **Kronecker-Koszul flattenings** [cite: 5, 25]. Traditional flattenings fail for the Salmon problem, and previously, researchers relied on Koszul flattenings (developed by Landsberg, Ottaviani, and others). The new Kronecker-Koszul approach generalizes this by tensoring several copies of a tensor (acting as a Kronecker power), tensoring with identity maps, and contracting factors to exterior powers [cite: 5]. 

This method yields new determinantal equations that vanish on secant varieties but *do not* vanish on cactus varieties [cite: 5]. The inclusion of secant varieties inside cactus varieties has historically been a massive hurdle for proving ideal generation, as equations often fail to distinguish the two. By providing explicit polynomial equations with simple determinantal expressions that separate the secant variety from the cactus variety, this 2026 development provides a new structural weapon that could definitively prove the completeness of the degree 5, 6, and 9 ideal generators [cite: 5]. 

### 5.5 Unrestrictions and Concise Secant Varieties (April 2026)
The most direct and profound progress on the Salmon problem was published in a preprint in April 2026 by Jagiella and Jelisiejew titled *"Unrestrictions and concise secant varieties"* [cite: 4, 26, 27]. 

The authors recognized that current attempts at the Salmon problem were bottlenecked by direct calculation [cite: 4]. To circumvent this, they introduced **concise secant varieties** ($\hat{\sigma}_r$). Informally, these are modular partial desingularizations of the secant varieties to Segre embeddings [cite: 26]. The concise secant variety is projective and birational to the abstract secant variety, but remarkably, every single point on it corresponds to a concise tensor of minimal border rank [cite: 26, 27].

The central thesis of their 2026 paper is the concept of **unrestrictions**. They proved that determining if a tensor $T$ has border rank $\le r$ (i.e., [T] = $\lim_{t \to 0} [T_t]$) can be rigorously split into two purely algebraic steps:
1.  Taking a minimal border rank limit $\tilde{T} = \lim_{t \to 0} T_t$.
2.  Taking a linear-algebraic restriction from $\tilde{T}$ to $T$. [cite: 4]

By doing this, the incredibly complex topological operation of "taking limits" (which defines the border rank and thus the secant variety) is translated into the study of **unrestriction schemes** [cite: 4, 26]. Jagiella and Jelisiejew establish a "Fixed unrestriction theorem," which acts as a concise version of border apolarity. It allows researchers to leverage Borel-fixed ideals to vastly reduce the search space for tensor ranks [cite: 4].

The authors explicitly single out the Salmon problem as a primary application of their theory. They note that the set-theoretic solution is known, but the scheme-theoretic and ideal-theoretic descriptions have remained elusive [cite: 4]. By embedding the abstract secant variety into the concise secant variety, they provide a framework where the ideal of $\sigma_4$ can be characterized entirely through the geometry of unrestrictions [cite: 4, 26]. This reduces the problem from finding arbitrary polynomials in a 64-dimensional space to calculating restrictions from the 107 well-classified isomorphism classes of minimal border rank tensors [cite: 4, 24]. 

## 6. The Path to Resolving the Ideal-Theoretic Conjecture

With the advent of concise secant varieties and Kronecker-Koszul flattenings in 2026, the strategy for proving the Salmon Conjecture has fundamentally shifted. 

Previously, the only hope of proving the ideal-theoretic conjecture was to demonstrate that the secant variety $\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3)$ is **arithmetically Cohen-Macaulay (aCM)** [cite: 28]. If a variety is aCM, powerful tools from commutative algebra (such as mapping cones and free resolutions) can be used to lift the ideal generators from smaller dimensions to the target dimension [cite: 28]. Landsberg and Weyman showed that if smaller secant varieties possess aCM properties with "small partitions," the ideal can be generated by inherited equations [cite: 28]. However, proving the aCM property for $\sigma_4$ via direct calculation has remained out of reach.

The 2026 frameworks bypass the need for brute-force Gröbner bases. By analyzing the concise secant variety, researchers can define the secant variety ideal as the intersection of ideals derived from unrestrictions [cite: 4]. The Kronecker-Koszul flattenings guarantee that the extraneous components (like the cactus variety and the $Sub_{3,3,3}$ variety) can be algebraically separated from the true secant variety [cite: 5]. 

The current mathematical consensus strongly supports the Revised Salmon Conjecture. The ideal $\mathcal{I}(\sigma_4(\mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3 \times \mathbb{P}^3))$ is almost certainly generated minimally by:
*   The 1728 equations of degree 5 (Strassen's commutative conditions).
*   The 1000 equations of degree 6 (Landsberg-Manivel).
*   The 8000 equations of degree 9 (Strassen's symmetrization).

## 7. Conclusion

The Salmon problem represents a fascinating convergence of disciplines. What began as a question about evolutionary biology algorithms became a benchmark problem in representation theory, numerical algebraic geometry, and tensor calculus. 

As of mid-2026, the ideal-theoretic Salmon problem is arguably closer to a final resolution than ever before. The brute-force numerical attacks of 2010–2012 proved that the set of degree 5, 6, and 9 equations geometrically defines the variety [cite: 3, 7]. Now, the theoretical innovations of 2024–2026—namely, the classification of minimal border rank tensors, the formulation of concise secant varieties, and the invention of Kronecker-Koszul flattenings—have provided the precise surgical tools needed to analyze the algebraic scheme of the variety without being overwhelmed by its dimensionality [cite: 4, 5, 24, 26]. 

While the exact proof confirming that these polynomials form the complete minimal generating set of the prime ideal has not yet been formally published as a completed theorem, the architectural framework laid down in 2026 practically guarantees that this long-standing conjecture will fall in the near future. Once resolved, it will yield the complete set of polynomial invariants for the general Markov model on binary trees, closing a massive chapter in algebraic statistics and opening new doors for computational phylogenetics.
