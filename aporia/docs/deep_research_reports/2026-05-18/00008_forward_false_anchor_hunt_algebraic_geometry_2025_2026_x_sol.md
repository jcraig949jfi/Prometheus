## 1. The Artificial Intelligence Singularity in Algebraic Geometry

The integration of advanced AI architectures into pure mathematics reached a critical threshold in late 2025 and early 2026. Prior to this, AI systems were largely relegated to auto-formalization, combinatorial optimization (such as the Cap Set problem or Erdős bounds), and high school-level Olympiad geometry [cite: 1, 2]. By 2026, AI transitioned from a mere computational assistant to a listed collaborator—and in some claims, a primary discoverer—of deep theorems in algebraic geometry [cite: 3, 4].

### 1.1 The Motivic Class of the Space of Genus 0 Maps to the Flag Variety
One of the most consequential claims of early 2026 was published by a joint academic-industry team comprising Jim Bryan, Balázs Elek, Freddie Manners, George Salafatinos, and Stanford professor Ravi Vakil [cite: 4, 5]. Their paper, *The motivic class of the space of genus 0 maps to the flag variety* (arXiv:2601.07222), claims to have solved a notoriously difficult problem in enumerative geometry and moduli space theory [cite: 4, 5].

**The Mathematical Claim:**
The paper investigates $\operatorname{Fl}_{n+1}$, the variety of complete flags in affine space $\mathbb{A}^{n+1}$, and the space of based maps $f: \mathbb{P}^1 \to \operatorname{Fl}_{n+1}$ belonging to a specific class $f_*[\mathbb{P}^1] = \beta$ [cite: 5]. This space, denoted $\Omega^2_\beta(\operatorname{Fl}_{n+1})$, is highly complex and high-dimensional [cite: 6]. The team successfully calculated its class in the Grothendieck ring of varieties, $K_0(\operatorname{Var})$, which acts as a powerful universal invariant (the "motivic class") [cite: 4, 5]. 

The authors proved that under a mild positivity condition on $\beta$ (specifically, a strictly monotonic condition ensuring vector bundles behave appropriately), the motivic class simplifies astonishingly to:
\[ [\Omega^2_\beta(\operatorname{Fl}_{n+1})] = [\operatorname{GL}_n \times \mathbb{A}^a] \]
where $\operatorname{GL}_n$ is the general linear group of invertible $n \times n$ matrices and $\mathbb{A}^a$ is an affine space [cite: 5, 6]. This implies that in the "cut-and-paste" framework of the Grothendieck ring, all the staggering geometric complexity of the map space precisely cancels out, leaving behind the well-understood algebraic structure of $\operatorname{GL}_n$ [cite: 6].

**The AI Collaboration and Verification:**
What makes this result a focal point of the 2026 landscape is its genesis. The proof was achieved in conjunction with Google's Gemini Deep Think system and a specialized mathematical proof system tentatively named "FullProof" or "Aletheia" [cite: 4, 7]. The AI agent did not simply rewrite existing proofs; it provided key, original insights [cite: 4]. 

Ravi Vakil, a highly respected figure in Gromov-Witten theory and Schubert calculus, personally verified the AI's contribution, stating that the proof was "rigorous, correct, and elegant" [cite: 4]. He noted, "I would be proud of such insights even if they were my own," acknowledging the uncertainty of whether a human team would have organically reached the same conclusion without the AI's interpolative leaps [cite: 4]. This result represents a highly credible "true anchor," rigorously cross-checked by top-tier human mathematicians [cite: 4, 6].

### 1.2 Extremal Descent Integrals on Moduli Spaces of Curves
In December 2025, mathematician Johannes Schmitt from ETH Zurich reported another AI-driven breakthrough, publishing a paper titled *Extremal Descent Integrals on Moduli Spaces of Curves: An Inequality Discovered and Proved in Collaboration with AI* (arXiv:2512.14575) [cite: 3].

**The Mathematical Claim:**
Schmitt's paper addresses the optimization of extremal values of $\psi$-intersection numbers on moduli spaces of curves [cite: 3]. Using software called OpenEvolve, the AI (specifically an iteration of GPT-5) formulated a conjecture and subsequently delivered an elegant solution [cite: 3]. Surprisingly, the AI circumvented standard methodologies, importing techniques from a completely different sub-area of algebraic geometry to resolve the inequality [cite: 3]. 

**Status as a Potential False Anchor:**
While the result is fascinating, its standing as a major breakthrough is debated. The human author explicitly noted in the appendix that the problem was initially designed as a "toy problem" to test the AI, and peers on mathematical forums pointed out that it was not a traditional "open problem" simply because no mathematician had previously attempted to solve it [cite: 3]. Critics argued that the theorem, while a neat, original contribution, is on the "borderline of notability" for a top-tier mathematical publication [cite: 3]. Furthermore, the methodology—labeling paragraphs with margin bars to delineate human versus AI authorship—highlighted the intense time constraints and epistemological blurriness of attributing mathematical insight to algorithmic systems [cite: 3].

### 1.3 The "Incentive Gap" and Logic Hallucinations
The wave of AI proofs in 2025-2026 triggered immense debate regarding the reliability of algorithmic claims. During the Future of Mathematics Symposium in May 2026, Fields Medalist Terence Tao critiqued the current trajectory of AI in mathematics [cite: 8]. Tao argued that the community is suffering from an "incentive gap" and must prioritize "proof digestion"—the human process of streamlining, explaining, and connecting proofs to existing literature—over mere automated proof generation [cite: 8]. Tao stated, "The correct metric is not so much whether the proof has been generated or whether it has been verified, but whether someone can give a talk about it and take questions" [cite: 8].

This skepticism is validated by empirical data from the "Frontier Math" and "GAP" (Generalization-and-Perturbation) frameworks deployed in late 2025 [cite: 9, 10]. These stress-tests revealed that leading LLMs often suffer catastrophic failure on Putnam-level and graduate-level algebraic geometry problems when subjected to minor cosmetic or structural perturbations [cite: 9, 10]. The evaluations demonstrated that models frequently lack genuine geometric reasoning—failing, for instance, to manipulate simple pictorial models like knot diagrams—and that "logic hallucinations" account for roughly 60% of all incorrect answers provided by models like o3 and Gemini on advanced queries [cite: 9, 10]. Furthermore, AI agents attempting to resolve singularities via the Hironaka game (modeled as a Markov decision process) were found to make dangerous logical leaps, such as incorrectly inferring rank preservation directly from a homomorphism property without verifying injectivity or surjectivity [cite: 1].

Therefore, any "X solved Y" claim generated primarily by AI in 2026 must be treated with extreme caution unless anchored by extensive human "proof digestion."

## 2. The Cubic Fourfold, Mirror Symmetry, and the "Theory of Atoms"

Perhaps the most dramatic saga in pure mathematics during 2025 and 2026 surrounds the rationality of the cubic fourfold and the sweeping, physics-inspired program intended to solve it. 

### 2.1 The Rationality Problem 
The question of which algebraic varieties are rational (birationally equivalent to projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$) has driven algebraic geometry for over a century. In dimension 2, the classification is complete. In dimension 3, the milestone Clemens-Griffiths theorem (1972) established that smooth cubic threefolds are not rational by showing their intermediate Jacobians cannot be decomposed into products of Jacobians of curves [cite: 11, 12]. 

However, in dimension 4, the rationality of the cubic fourfold (a degree 3 hypersurface in $\mathbb{P}^5$) has remained an elusive target [cite: 11, 13, 14]. Some specific "special" cubic fourfolds (in the sense of Brendan Hassett) are known to be rational, but it is deeply suspected that a *very general* cubic fourfold is irrational [cite: 14, 15, 16]. Alexander Kuznetsov previously approached this by analyzing the derived category of coherent sheaves $D^b(X)$, conjecturing that a cubic fourfold is rational if and only if its "Kuznetsov component" is equivalent to the derived category of a K3 surface [cite: 17, 18].

### 2.2 The Katzarkov-Kontsevich-Pantev-Yu (KKPY) Breakthrough
In August 2025, a landmark paper was posted by an all-star team: Ludmil Katzarkov, Maxim Kontsevich, Tony Pantev, and Tony Yue Yu [cite: 11, 19]. They claimed to have completely resolved the rationality problem for the general cubic fourfold [cite: 11, 19]. 

**The Methodology:**
The KKPY proof relies on wholly unfamiliar techniques imported from string theory, specifically mirror symmetry [cite: 11]. Traditional methods, like those used by Clemens and Griffiths, fail in dimension 4 because the Hodge structures of four-folds are too convoluted [cite: 11]. Katzarkov's insight was to access the four-fold's Hodge structure indirectly. By counting the number of rational curves on the four-fold's "mirror image"—a completely different manifold constructed via homological mirror symmetry—the team could break the four-fold's complex Hodge structure into irreducible, elementary blocks they termed "Atoms" [cite: 11, 12, 19]. 

These "Hodge atoms" arise from the spectral decomposition of an "F-bundle" (a formal/non-archimedean version of a variation of a noncommutative Hodge structure) under an Euler vector field action [cite: 19, 20]. The team derived a rigorous formula to track how these atoms mutate under birational transformations [cite: 11, 19]. Because atoms behave additively under blowups, the team demonstrated that a general cubic fourfold possesses an atom that fundamentally cannot be transformed into the atomic structure of a simple 4D projective space, thereby proving its irrationality [cite: 11, 19].

**Reception and Skepticism:**
The release of this proof sent shockwaves through the community. Quanta Magazine reported in December 2025 that the proof was "brilliant" but "baffling" [cite: 11]. The primary issue is that the machinery is entirely alien to classical algebraic geometers [cite: 11]. Kontsevich, a Fields Medalist of legendary status, also possesses a "penchant for making audacious claims, giving others pause" [cite: 11]. Reading groups convened globally to decipher the 2025 preprint [cite: 11]. After months of intense line-by-line reading, many participants admitted they still felt completely "lost when it comes to major details" [cite: 11]. The extreme complexity of the KKPY paper makes it a terrifyingly precarious anchor for future research; if a subtle error exists in the non-archimedean convergence estimates [cite: 20], the entire rationality paradigm could collapse.

### 2.3 The False Anchor: "Atoms Meet Symbols" Retraction
The danger of building rapidly on the KKPY foundation was dramatically exposed in late 2025. Following the initial release of the Theory of Atoms, Leonardo F. Cavenaghi, Ludmil Katzarkov, and Maxim Kontsevich posted a highly ambitious preprint on September 19, 2025, titled *Atoms meet symbols* (arXiv:2509.15831) [cite: 21, 22, 23].

**The Claim:**
This paper sought to construct powerful new invariants for $G$-equivariant birational geometry (where varieties are equipped with finite group actions) [cite: 21, 22, 23]. It attempted a grand unification of the KKPY *Theory of Atoms* with the *Theory of Modular Symbols* (developed previously by Kontsevich, Tschinkel, and Pestun) [cite: 21, 22]. The authors proposed the concept of "Chen-Ruan atoms," utilizing the quantum Chen-Ruan cohomology of global quotient orbifolds [cite: 22, 23]. Assuming a conjectural blowup formula for quantum Chen-Ruan cohomology, the authors claimed to definitively prove that various algebraic varieties (such as certain cubic surfaces and threefolds with $\mathbb{Z}/2$ or $\mathbb{Z}/3$ actions) were not $G$-equivariantly birational to linear actions on projective space [cite: 22, 23].

**The Retraction:**
The mathematical community immediately began citing this unifying framework [cite: 24, 25]. However, the paper contained fatal flaws. Shortly after its dissemination and discussion at major international conferences, the preprint was abruptly withdrawn by the authors. The retraction notice bluntly stated: *"We think it is best to be withdrawn at this point so that readers will not be misled"* [cite: 26]. 

This withdrawal serves as the quintessential **forward false-anchor** of the 2025-2026 period. The rush to deploy the highly complex, partially understood "Theory of Atoms" into the equivariant setting utilizing unproven blow-up conjectures resulted in an unstable theoretical edifice. For researchers navigating the literature of 2026, *Atoms meet symbols* represents a documented dead-end that must not be cited as established mathematics.

Another similar false anchor involving the same sphere of researchers was the "Conifold gap" paper (*Conifold gap and all-genus mirror symmetry for local $\mathbb{P}^2$* by Andrea Brini), which was also flagged with the exact same withdrawal notice: *"We think it is best to be withdrawn at this point so that readers will not be misled"* [cite: 26]. The clustering of retractions around mirror symmetry and the theory of atoms indicates a sub-field experiencing intense growing pains and struggling with internal consistency.

## 3. Verified Classical Breakthroughs in 2025-2026

Setting aside the epistemological turbulence of AI and the withdrawn "Atoms meet symbols" framework, the 2025-2026 period saw several outstanding, human-driven classical conjectures resolved. 

### 3.1 The Picard Rank Conjecture and Malle's Conjecture
In May 2026, Harvard mathematicians Aaron Landesman and Ishan Levy presented a profound proof of the **Asymptotic Picard Rank Conjecture** [cite: 27]. 

**The Claim:**
The Hurwitz space $H_{d,g}$ parameterizes simply branched covers of $\mathbb{P}^1$ of degree $d$ and genus $g$ [cite: 27]. The Picard rank conjecture predicted the vanishing of the rational Picard group of this space [cite: 27]. Landesman and Levy definitively proved the conjecture for instances where the genus $g$ is sufficiently large relative to the degree $d$ [cite: 27]. 

**The Methodology:**
The breakthrough was achieved by establishing a novel result in topology: proving that the homology of Hurwitz spaces stabilizes and explicitly computing their stable value [cite: 27]. Furthermore, the authors leveraged these identical homological stability results to prove a version of **Malle's Conjecture** over the function field $\mathbb{F}_q(t)$, successfully predicting the asymptotic number of $G$-extensions of $\mathbb{F}_q(t)$ for a finite group $G$ [cite: 27]. This dual resolution of deep problems in algebraic geometry and arithmetic statistics stands as one of the most robust theorems of 2026.

### 3.2 The Shapiro Conjecture, Wronski Maps, and Positive Grassmannians
Late 2025 saw the publication of a sweeping generalization of the B. and M. Shapiro conjecture by Steven Karp and Kevin Purbhoo in their paper *Universal Plücker coordinates for the Wronski map and positivity in real Schubert calculus* [cite: 28].

**The Claim:**
The theorem bridges real algebraic geometry, combinatorics, and mathematical physics. It dictates that if $p$ is a polynomial featuring entirely negative roots, then all elements of the preimage of $p$ under the Wronski map intrinsically belong to the positive Grassmannian [cite: 28]. The positive Grassmannian consists of elements in $G(m,n)$ representable by matrices where all $m \times m$ minors are strictly positive [cite: 28]. Karp and Purbhoo's work is considered a "very broad and unexpected generalization" that simultaneously resolves several other lingering conjectures within real algebraic geometry and real Schubert calculus [cite: 28]. 

### 3.3 Stable Irrationality of Cubic Threefolds and Polynomial Point Counts
While the cubic fourfold rationality problem dominated headlines via the "Theory of Atoms," the lower-dimensional analog saw a magnificent, rigorous resolution by Stefan Schreieder, Philip Engel, and O. de Gaay-Fortman [cite: 12]. 

**The Claim:**
While it was known since 1972 (Clemens-Griffiths) that a smooth cubic threefold is not rational, the question of its *stable* rationality (whether its product with any projective space is rational) remained famously open [cite: 12]. Schreieder, Engel, and de Gaay-Fortman successfully proved the stable irrationality of a very general cubic threefold over the complex numbers [cite: 12]. They achieved this by investigating the integral Hodge conjecture for abelian varieties, proving that the minimal curve class on the intermediate Jacobian of a very general cubic threefold is definitively not algebraic [cite: 12].

In parallel moduli space research, Canning achieved a breakthrough result regarding the moduli spaces of curves $M_g$ [cite: 12]. Extending the classical 20-year-old works of Looijenga and Tommasi (which applied to genus 3 and 4), Canning comprehensively determined which moduli spaces have a polynomial point count over a finite field [cite: 12]. Utilizing Deligne's fundamental theories connecting point counts to the trace of the Frobenius endomorphism, Canning proved that the count is polynomial for $g \leq 8$. To prove the negative for higher genera, the team calculated the 11th cohomology $\chi_{11}(M_g)$, showing it is non-zero for $g \geq 9$ (excluding 12), thus concluding that their point counts are not polynomial [cite: 12].

### 3.4 Kakeya, Hyperplane, and Hilbert's 10th Conjectures
Several other massive pillars fell during this window:
*   **The Kakeya Conjecture in $\mathbb{R}^3$:** In a highly celebrated 2025 preprint, Wang and Zahl proved the Kakeya conjecture for three-dimensional Euclidean space, a monumental event in geometric measure theory with deep implications for algebraic analysis [cite: 28].
*   **The Hyperplane (Slicing) Conjecture:** Formulated by Bourgain in 1986, this conjecture asked if any convex body in $\mathbb{R}^n$ of unit volume possesses a hyperplane section with an $(n-1)$-dimensional volume bounded below by a universal constant [cite: 28]. In 2024-2025, Klartag and Lehec completed the proof of this incredibly deep statement, heavily impacting convex geometry and probability theory [cite: 28].
*   **Hilbert's 10th Problem over $\mathbb{Z}$-Algebras:** In 2024, Koymans and Pagano gave a negative answer to Hilbert's 10th problem for rings finitely generated over $\mathbb{Z}$ [cite: 28]. In a spectacular display of parallel discovery, this exact result was proved completely independently in 2025 by the team of Alpöge, Bhargava, Ho, and Shnidman [cite: 28].

## 4. Documented "Gaps in Proof" and Corrections (2025-2026)

The intense pace of preprint publication on the arXiv necessitates constant vigilance. Several major claims in algebraic geometry were found to contain gaps shortly after their initial upload, requiring swift revisions. These represent "rehabilitated" anchors—works that were briefly false anchors but were subsequently corrected.

### 4.1 A Hyperbolicity Conjecture for Adjoint Bundles
In December 2024, Joaquín Moraga and colleagues posted a paper (arXiv:2412.01811) investigating a hyperbolicity conjecture [cite: 29]. The conjecture posits that for an $n$-dimensional smooth projective variety $X$ and an ample Cartier divisor $L$, a very general element of the linear system $|K_X + (3n+1)L|$ is a hyperbolic algebraic variety [cite: 29]. The initial draft claimed proofs for various toric varieties. 

However, peer review quickly identified a structural flaw. In May 2025, the authors uploaded version 2, explicitly noting: *"Corrected a gap in proof of main theorems from previous draft (statements unchanged), added results about pseudo hyperbolicity on Gorenstein toric varieties"* [cite: 29]. The core mathematical claim survived, but the logical pathway required significant patching, serving as a reminder of the fragility of complex algebraic geometry proofs prior to thorough community "digestion."

### 4.2 Relations Among $\mathbb{P}$-Twists
Similarly, in the realm of derived categories and hyperkähler varieties, Andreas Hochenegger and Andreas Krug investigated relations among $\mathbb{P}$-twists (arXiv:2207.14120, updated through 2025) [cite: 30]. The authors aimed to prove that under certain technical assumptions, $\mathbb{P}$-twists commute if and only if their associated $\mathbb{P}$-objects are orthogonal [cite: 30]. The final 2025 publication required the authors to append a specific comment: *"Closed gap in proof of Proposition 3.23 (see also Remark 3.24)"* [cite: 30]. 

### 4.3 Methodological Traps and Sub-rosa AI Assistance
A recurring theme in the retracted or corrected papers of 2025-2026 is the subtle, sometimes undisclosed reliance on algorithmic assistance that produces "almost correct" topologies. As noted in the critique of AI4Math systems, LLMs navigating the combinatorial explosion of search spaces in geometric proofs frequently generate pathways that look syntactically perfect but lack semantic geometric reasoning [cite: 1, 9]. The community has established that AI tools are highly proficient at keyword searches, natural language generation, and coding simple algebraic permutations [cite: 9]. However, when problems require multi-step ingenuity—such as accurately modeling the topological equivalence of knot diagrams—the systems systematically fail [cite: 9]. Any forward-looking researcher must apply rigorous scrutiny to preprints from 2025-2026 that utilize rapid, sprawling algebraic logic without grounding it in verifiable geometric intuition.

## 5. Unresolved Leviathans: The True Open Problems

Amidst the dizzying array of "solved" claims, it is critical to document the conjectures that remain utterly unresolved as of mid-2026. These represent the absolute, unyielding bedrock of open algebraic geometry.

*   **The Hodge Conjecture:** Despite the advances of the KKPY "Theory of Atoms" in parsing Hodge structures, the Hodge conjecture itself—one of the Millennium Prize Problems—remains wide open [cite: 31]. It posits that for a smooth projective variety $X$ over $\mathbb{C}$, every Hodge class in $H^{2p}(X, \mathbb{Q}) \cap H^{p,p}(X)$ is a rational linear combination of classes of algebraic cycles of codimension $p$ [cite: 31]. While it holds in dimensions less than 4, the general case continues to resist all modern assaults [cite: 31].
*   **The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture:** Another Millennium problem, this conjecture connects the analytic behavior of the $L$-function of an elliptic curve at $s=1$ to its algebraic rank [cite: 31, 32]. Despite monumental partial results (such as those building on the modularity theorem proved by Wiles, Taylor, Breuil, Conrad, and Diamond), the full conjecture for elliptic curves of higher rank remains an impenetrable fortress [cite: 31, 33].
*   **The Jacobian Conjecture and the Resolution of Singularities in Positive Characteristic:** Both remain formally unresolved, though AI has begun mapping the Hironaka game for specific classes of du Val singularities [cite: 1].
