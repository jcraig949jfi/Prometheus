## 1. Introduction: The Landscape of Analytic Number Theory in 2025-2026

## 2. The Riemann Hypothesis: A Convergence of Artificial Intelligence and Independent Disproofs

First proposed by Bernhard Riemann in his seminal 1859 paper, the Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function, \(\zeta(s)\), lie on the "critical line" where the real part of the complex variable \(s\) is exactly \(1/2\) [cite: 4]. The hypothesis is intimately connected to the distribution of prime numbers and is widely considered the most important unsolved problem in pure mathematics [cite: 5, 6]. It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems, carrying a $1 million reward from the Clay Mathematics Institute for a peer-reviewed proof [cite: 4, 7].

### 2.1. The Grok-3 Incident: Artificial Intelligence and the Illusion of Proof

According to Pham's initial statement, the AI was deemed "so smart that it becomes a danger to humanity," prompting the halt in training to verify the proof [cite: 10]. When pressed about the specific dangers of solving the RH, Pham cryptically referenced Matt Haig's science fiction novel *The Humans*, which features an alien visitor taking the form of a Cambridge professor who solves the hypothesis [cite: 10].

The announcement caused immediate shockwaves, sparking intense debates about the timeline of artificial general intelligence (AGI) and its capacity to perform abstract, Nobel-level mathematical reasoning [cite: 8, 11]. The narrative was further amplified by Mario Nawfal, founder of the IBC Group, who framed the event as a demonstration of "human-AI collaboration" that could unlock a 160-year-old mystery [cite: 8]. 

However, the claim was rapidly unmasked as a complex inside joke and false alarm [cite: 11]. Hours after the initial furor, Pham clarified that the post was satirical [cite: 11]. The reality behind the suspension of Grok-3's training was not a sudden mathematical epiphany, but rather a "catastrophic event" involving corrupted network weights [cite: 10, 11]. Another xAI researcher, Heinrich Kuttler, explained that the training run experienced severe instability, and the engineering team had to replace the "bad weights with nan [Not a Number] to recover" the model [cite: 10, 11]. xAI co-founder Greg Yang humorously leaned into the absurdity, sarcastically confirming that Grok-3 had "started attacking the security guards in the office while training" [cite: 11].

## 3. The Collatz Conjecture: Phase Expressions and Structural Dynamics

The Collatz Conjecture, also known as the \(3n+1\) problem, was proposed by Lothar Collatz in 1937 [cite: 30, 31]. It posits that for any positive integer \(n\), if the number is even, it is divided by 2; if it is odd, it is multiplied by 3 and 1 is added. The conjecture states that regardless of the starting value, this iterative process will invariably reach the trivial loop of 4-2-1 [cite: 30, 32]. Despite its simple arithmetic premise, the Collatz Conjecture exhibits chaotic and unpredictable behavior, prompting the legendary mathematician Paul Erdős to famously declare that "mathematics may not be ready for such problems" [cite: 30, 33]. 

However, in 2025 and 2026, the Collatz Conjecture became a primary target for "solved" claims, driven largely by novel structural representations of integers and heavy reliance on AI for logic formatting.

### 3.1. Yoshiki Ueoka and the Collatz Phase Representation (CPE)

The most elaborate and widely circulated Collatz claim of the period came from Yoshiki Ueoka (who identifies as a "yurufuwa-performer and researcher"), along with collaborators Taichi Hyuga, Hojin Yamadori, and the artificial intelligence model Google Gemini [cite: 33, 34]. Between August 2025 and May 2026, Ueoka published a series of Medium articles and preprints on Zenodo and figshare claiming a "Positive Proof of the Collatz Conjecture using Collatz Phase Representation" [cite: 33, 35]. 

Ueoka's methodology involved completely abandoning standard numerical iteration in favor of a structural and geometric translation of integers [cite: 33, 36]. The foundation of this approach was the **Alternating Binary Notation (ABN)**, which encodes integers as alternating-sign powers of 2 [cite: 31, 35]. From the ABN, Ueoka constructed the **Collatz Phase Expression (CPE)**, treating numbers not as magnitudes, but as sequences of logical blocks [cite: 31, 35]. 

Within the CPE framework, integers are decomposed into fundamental structural units named "Chains" (nonzero alternating blocks denoted by \(R\)), "Singles" (\(T\)), and "Nodes" (runs of zeros separating chains, denoted by \(K\)) [cite: 31, 35]. Ueoka further classified these into intuitive units he termed "Ketsu" (\(H_K\), representing the total length of zero-runs), "Tan" (\(\mu_T\)), and "Ren" (\(\mu_R\)) [cite: 34]. The total structural complexity of an integer was measured by \(\mu\), the number of chain-blocks [cite: 31].

Ueoka argued that the Collatz update rule (\(3x+1\)) acts as a "local rewrite system" on this chain/node structure [cite: 31]. To formalize this, the team devised the **New Collatz Operation (NCO)** algorithm, which translates the arithmetic multiplication of 3 into a "local triple formula" that reproduces the relationship \(3N = 2N + N\) entirely within the sign string and positional system of the CPE [cite: 33]. 

The core of Ueoka's proof rested on a "high-\(\mu\) descent argument" [cite: 36]. He sought to prove that with each application of the Collatz operation, the internal structural complexity (the number of "Knots" or "Units") must monotonically decrease [cite: 34]. A critical obstacle was the potential for a "fully frozen tail"—a terminal configuration in the CPE that remains unchanged under the accelerated Collatz map, which could theoretically allow a number to diverge to infinity or enter a non-trivial loop [cite: 36]. Ueoka claimed to have definitively excluded the possibility of fully frozen tails through local unit analysis, ensuring that every orbit in the high-\(\mu\) region eventually descends to a low-\(\mu\) region in finite time [cite: 36]. Ultimately, he asserted that every positive sequence is structurally forced to converge to the minimal complexity state (\(H_K = 0\)), leading exclusively to the trivial \(\{1\}\) loop [cite: 35]. He also claimed this framework resolved the negative Collatz conjecture by establishing a universal structural upper bound (\(H_{RT} < 6\)), guaranteeing convergence to the known negative finite loops [cite: 35].

Ueoka framed his discovery not just as mathematical, but deeply philosophical, calling it the "resolution of knowledge disparity" and an example of how human intuition combined with AI logic can bypass traditional academic gatekeeping [cite: 34]. Despite initial rejections from journals such as the Journal of the Mathematical Society of Japan (JMSJ), Ueoka continually refined the proof, heavily utilizing Gemini AI for structural editing and translation, and maintaining a high profile on open-science platforms [cite: 37]. 

### 3.2. Kirk Hahn's Rule-Based Equalities and Dendritic Patterns

Concurrently, another prominent claim was advanced by Kirk Hahn, who had previously presented his ideas at the West Coast Number Theory Conference in December 2023 [cite: 38]. In August 2025, Hahn published a preprint titled "Collatz Conjecture Is True for All Positive Integers" on Preprints.org, and subsequently presented his findings at a meeting of the Mathematical Society of Japan in early 2025 [cite: 30, 39, 40]. 

Hahn's approach eschewed the complex notation of Ueoka's CPE in favor of basic algebraic equalities. He observed that the Collatz rule for odd numbers, \(3x+1\), necessarily generates an even number, which can subsequently be factored into a power of 2 and a new odd integer. Hahn generalized this as \(3X + 1 = 2^a \cdot X'\), where \(X\) and \(X'\) are odd positive integers and \(a \geq 1\) [cite: 30, 40]. 

By treating the problem from the perspective of these generated equalities, Hahn argued that the rules inherently organize all even positive integers into distinct sub-sets and generate a predictable "dendritic pattern" connecting all odd positive integers [cite: 39, 40]. Hahn claimed that by recursively selecting equalities that share the same odd number, one can generate a massive system of equations that directly links the termination value of 1 to every conceivable odd positive integer [cite: 40]. 

Hahn's central thesis was that this interconnected web of equalities structurally prevents the formation of any non-trivial loops or endless divergence to infinity [cite: 39, 41]. While Hahn's observations about the connectivity of odd numbers via the Collatz map (often visualized as the Collatz tree or graph) are mathematically standard, his assertion that this observation constitutes a definitive, rigorous proof of the conjecture for all integers was widely viewed by institutional mathematicians as failing to address the fundamental analytic difficulties of the problem, particularly the chaotic distribution of the exponent \(a\) in the \(2^a\) reduction steps [cite: 30, 40].

## 4. The Goldbach Conjecture: Covariance Inequalities and Disproofs

First proposed in a 1742 letter from Christian Goldbach to Leonhard Euler, the strong Goldbach Conjecture asserts that every even integer greater than 2 can be expressed as the sum of two prime numbers [cite: 3, 43]. While computationally verified up to \(4 \times 10^{18}\) (four quintillion), a universal proof has eluded mathematicians for nearly 300 years, largely because prime numbers are multiplicatively defined, making their additive properties highly resistant to classical analysis [cite: 3, 16]. 

### 4.1. The Bahbouhi $\lambda$-Analytic Program and Covariance Control

In late 2025, a highly sophisticated approach to the Goldbach Conjecture emerged in the form of the "Bahbouhi \(\lambda\)-analytic program." Outlined in preprints published in November and December 2025, this framework attempted to fundamentally reclassify Goldbach's Conjecture from an additive number theory problem into a "pure correlation inequality" [cite: 44, 45].

Historically, mathematicians have attacked the Goldbach problem using the circle method (Hardy-Littlewood), large sieve techniques, and major-minor arc decompositions [cite: 44]. The Bahbouhi program claimed to bypass these classical obstructions by utilizing a continuous "\(\lambda\)-field," where the local density of primes is expressed analytically as \(\lambda(x) \approx 1/\ln x\) [cite: 45]. 

The core geometric idea is to view an even number \(E\) as the center point of a symmetric window. If \(p\) and \(q\) are primes such that \(p + q = E\), they can be written as \(E/2 - t\) and \(E/2 + t\) [cite: 44]. The Bahbouhi framework models this as two "mirrored density functions" (left and right prime streams) centered symmetrically at \(E/2\) [cite: 44, 45]. The conjecture is thus reduced to proving that these two prime density functions must intersect at least once inside a shrinking window [cite: 44].

To prove this intersection, the framework relies on a strict **covariance inequality**. The expected number of prime pairs within distance \(H\) is roughly \(H / \ln^2 E\) [cite: 45]. The program asserts that the variance between the two mirrored fields is strictly bounded: \(\text{Var}(R_H) \leq C \cdot H / (\ln E)^{2+\eta}\) [cite: 45]. By applying Chebyshev's inequality, the probability of the overlap being zero becomes vanishingly small as \(E \to \infty\) [cite: 45]. 

The authors claimed this was a "decisive structural reduction," rendering the Goldbach Conjecture structurally solved and analytically localized, pending only the final proof of the single two-point correlation inequality (which they linked to the Elliott-Halberstam conjecture) [cite: 44, 45]. The elegance of this approach lies in its interpretation: the "covariance wall" separating left and right prime populations dissolves as numbers grow larger, making Goldbach's symmetry not a rare coincidence, but a statistical and topological necessity [cite: 45].

### 4.2. Sandeep Jaiswal's Ten Independent Disproofs of Goldbach's Conjecture

Standing in direct opposition to structural attempts to prove the conjecture was Sandeep Jaiswal, who, alongside his attacks on the Riemann Hypothesis, launched a massive theoretical assault on the Goldbach Conjecture in 2025 and 2026 [cite: 14, 16]. Jaiswal's work, heavily promoted on ResearchGate, claimed to be the "first complete and definitive disproof" of the conjecture, utilizing ten robust, independent mathematical frameworks [cite: 16, 19].

Jaiswal's fundamental premise was that the empirical resilience of Goldbach's Conjecture at computationally accessible scales (up to \(10^{18}\)) is a mirage [cite: 19, 21]. He argued that as numbers scale toward infinity, the available "symmetric candidates" face hard subset-sum limitations and "spectral decomposition breakdowns" [cite: 19, 21]. 

Among his ten disproofs, Jaiswal emphasized "Logical Self-Negation through Bounded Exhaustion" and "Algorithmic Halting Failure in Constructive Proofs" [cite: 19]. He proposed that if the conjecture were valid for all \(2n\), a deterministic function evaluating the prime pairs would eventually contradict its own nature, leading to "looping or plateauing" across increasing ranges and a "recycling of finite intervals" [cite: 19]. Jaiswal further claimed that mapping primes as topological points along a number line reveals "topological discontinuity" and "voids across prime-pair lattices," ensuring that at unimaginably large scales, even integers will eventually fail to admit valid prime-pair decompositions [cite: 19, 21]. 

Like his claims regarding the Riemann Hypothesis, Jaiswal's Goldbach disproofs were characterized by grand, historic rhetoric, challenging the mathematical community to "distinguish between empirical tradition and provable truth," and declaring the conclusion of the "era of Goldbach" [cite: 19]. However, his reliance on vague concepts like "spectral silence at high frequencies" and alleged contradictions in the Prime Number Theorem (PNT) led to profound skepticism from institutional experts [cite: 16, 19].

## 5. The Twin Prime Conjecture: Distributed Computing and the Legacy of the Calculus Teacher

The Twin Prime Conjecture postulates that there are infinitely many pairs of prime numbers that differ by exactly 2 (e.g., 11 and 13, or 41 and 43) [cite: 2, 46]. While Euclid proved the infinitude of primes in 300 B.C., proving that the gap between primes does not eventually expand to infinity indefinitely has been one of the great challenges of modern mathematics [cite: 47, 48].

### 5.1. PrimeGrid Forums and the Limits of Asymptotic Approximations

In 2025, the community-driven computing project PrimeGrid, which utilizes the BOINC platform to discover massive prime numbers, became the locus of a popular claim regarding the Twin Prime Conjecture [cite: 49, 50]. A user known as "ryzenmulti" posted a thread titled "Twin prime conjecture is solved !!" which garnered significant attention [cite: 49]. 

The user's argument relied heavily on asymptotic estimates and the behavior of primes over massive intervals. They asserted that it is "trivial to show in the limit as x increases above about 300 digits every extra digit adds 10 fold increase in primes and twin primes" [cite: 49]. To support this, ryzenmulti developed python scripts utilizing curve fitting and asymptotic approximations. They critiqued standard approximations, noting that the crude estimate \(x/\ln(x)\) is vastly inferior to \(x/\ln(x/2)\), which they claimed provided 50% better accuracy for \(x\) tending to infinity [cite: 49].

By demonstrating that the estimated number of twin primes in an interval \((x_1, x_2)\) continually scales upward, ryzenmulti confidently declared: "There are infinite twin primes. You're welcome" [cite: 49]. However, as more mathematically rigorous users on the forum pointed out, demonstrating that an asymptotic formula (like the one incorporating the twin prime constant \(C_2 \approx 0.66016\)) predicts infinite twin primes is not a mathematical proof [cite: 49]. Heuristic and statistical models universally predict the truth of the Twin Prime Conjecture, but analytic number theory requires strict bounded proofs to eliminate the possibility of a sudden, catastrophic deviation from the statistical average at infinity [cite: 49, 51]. 

### 5.2. The Enduring Trope of the "Calculus Teacher"

The sociological discourse surrounding the Twin Prime Conjecture in 2025 and 2026 was heavily colored by the legacy of Yitang Zhang. In 2013, Zhang, working as a temporary, part-time calculus teacher at the University of New Hampshire, shocked the world by proving that there are infinitely many pairs of primes separated by at most 70 million [cite: 46, 47, 52]. This bound was subsequently reduced to 600 by James Maynard, and further to 246 by the Polymath Project led by Terence Tao [cite: 46, 47].

Zhang's story—an unknown, 58-year-old lecturer with no recent publications solving a century-old problem—became a powerful cultural mythos [cite: 48, 53, 54]. It proved that monumental discoveries could still originate outside the elite, tenured circles of the Ivy League [cite: 48, 54]. 

In 2025, this trope resurfaced in a viral Reddit thread where a student claimed, "My former calculus teacher claims to have solved the Twin Primes Conjecture using the Chinese Remainder Theorem" [cite: 55]. While the specific details of this new teacher's algebraic proof were sparse, the immediate traction the post gained underscores how the mathematical community and the general public remain primed to believe in the "unlikely genius" archetype [cite: 48, 55]. The shadow of Yitang Zhang ensures that any claim originating from an obscure educator is initially met with a mixture of intense curiosity and historical romanticism, even if the mathematical machinery (such as relying solely on the Chinese Remainder Theorem for an analytic problem) appears fundamentally inadequate [cite: 55].

## 7. The Sociology of "Independent Researchers" and Preprint Culture

The 2025-2026 claims highlight a stark divergence in mathematical sociology. Institutional mathematics operates on a slow, peer-reviewed timeline. The 800-page proof of the geometric Langlands conjecture by Gaitsgory and Raskin took over a decade of intensive research and collaboration before its publication in 2024, and its vetting will continue for years [cite: 28]. 

Conversely, the "Independent Researcher" operates in an accelerated, decentralized ecosystem. Platforms like ResearchGate, Preprints.org, Zenodo, and figshare allow individuals like Sandeep Jaiswal, Alexandar Balevsky, Kirk Hahn, and Yoshiki Ueoka to publish complex, highly formatted manuscripts instantly [cite: 15, 32, 34, 40]. These platforms provide digital object identifiers (DOIs) and professional aesthetics, granting a veneer of institutional legitimacy to unreviewed work [cite: 14, 40]. 

This ecosystem fosters a specific rhetorical style. Independent claims are frequently accompanied by aggressive marketing, appeals to historical grandeur, and a defensive posture against "institutional orthodoxy" [cite: 17, 19]. Jaiswal's framing of his Goldbach disproof as the end of an era, and Ueoka's framing of his Collatz proof as a triumph over "knowledge disparity," reflect a democratization of science that values disruptive innovation over incremental consensus [cite: 19, 34]. While this open culture allows for the free flow of ideas, it places the massive burden of filtering signal from noise entirely on the mathematical community, which often simply ignores such claims, leading claimants to argue they are being unfairly silenced [cite: 14, 30].

## 8. Conclusion

The 2025-2026 period in analytic number theory will likely be remembered not just for the genuine progress made by Fields Medalists, but for the unprecedented volume of viral, AI-assisted, and independent "solved" claims. The Riemann Hypothesis, Collatz Conjecture, Goldbach Conjecture, and Twin Prime Conjecture remain monumental challenges precisely because they resist the superficial logic and finite computational checks that characterize many of these recent assertions. 

The false anchors generated by AI hallucinations, asymptotic misunderstandings, and structural oversimplifications (from Collatz Phase Representations to geometric-temporal frameworks) serve as a fascinating mirror to contemporary society. They reflect our collective desire for sudden, genius-level breakthroughs and our growing anxiety and awe regarding the capabilities of artificial intelligence. While the true proofs of these historic problems remain hidden, the hunt for them continues to evolve, deeply entangled with the technological and sociological transformations of the 21st century.
